# What biochemistry is

Biochemistry is the chemistry of life — the molecular
mechanism by which living systems extract energy from their
environment, build themselves, store + retrieve genetic
information, and respond to signals.

It sits at the intersection of three disciplines:

- **Organic chemistry** — bond chemistry + functional
  groups + reaction mechanisms.  Gives us the language for
  describing how individual molecules behave.
- **Cell biology** — compartments, membranes, organelles,
  signalling networks.  Gives us the spatial framework
  inside which biochemistry happens.
- **Genetics + molecular biology** — DNA → RNA → protein
  + the regulatory networks that control which proteins
  get made when.

Biochemistry is the molecular bridge between all three.

## The four molecular currencies

Almost all of biochemistry runs on four classes of polymer:

- **Proteins** — chains of 20 standard amino acids; do
  almost everything (structure, catalysis, signalling,
  transport, motility, immunity).
- **Nucleic acids** — DNA stores genetic information; RNA
  carries it + does its own catalysis (ribozymes, tRNA,
  rRNA, regulatory ncRNAs).
- **Carbohydrates** — sugar polymers; energy storage
  (glycogen, starch), structure (cellulose, chitin),
  cell-surface signalling (glycoproteins, glycolipids).
- **Lipids** — fatty acids + glycerolipids + sterols;
  membranes, energy storage, signalling (steroids,
  eicosanoids).

Plus four crucial small-molecule classes:

- **Cofactors + coenzymes** — NAD+/H, FAD, CoA, SAM, ATP,
  PLP, B12, etc.  See BC-2.0 catalogue.
- **Metabolites** — small molecules of metabolism (sugars,
  amino acids, intermediates of glycolysis + TCA + lipid
  catabolism).
- **Hormones + neurotransmitters** — small-molecule
  signals (steroids, catecholamines, peptides).
- **Inorganic ions** — Na+, K+, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Zn²⁺, Cl⁻,
  phosphate.  Don't underestimate the inorganic role.

## The central dogma + its modern refinements

Crick's 1958 proposal:

```
DNA ─transcription→ RNA ─translation→ Protein
```

DNA replication adds a self-perpetuating loop on the DNA
node.  This is still the bedrock — but with important
modern additions:

- **Reverse transcription** — retroviruses (HIV) +
  retrotransposons + telomerase write RNA back to DNA.
- **RNA → RNA replication** — RNA viruses use RNA-
  dependent RNA polymerase.
- **Non-coding RNAs** — miRNAs, lncRNAs, siRNAs,
  piRNAs are themselves the functional output for many
  genes; they don't code for protein.
- **Epigenetics** — DNA methylation + histone
  modifications regulate which genes are accessible to
  transcription, heritable across cell divisions
  without changing DNA sequence.
- **RNA editing + modification** — m6A, m5C, pseudo-
  uridine, A-to-I editing add a layer of post-
  transcriptional regulation.

## The thermodynamic constraint

Living systems are open thermodynamic systems — they
import energy + matter, export waste + heat.  The Second
Law of Thermodynamics constrains what's possible:

- **Catabolism** breaks complex molecules to simple ones,
  releasing energy as ATP + reduced cofactors (NADH,
  FADH₂).  Net entropy increase.
- **Anabolism** uses ATP + reducing equivalents to build
  complex molecules from simple ones.  Local order, but
  paid for by the global entropy increase of catabolism +
  heat dissipation.

Photosynthesis is the unique exception that brings new
energy into the biosphere — chlorophyll absorbs photons
to drive water oxidation + CO₂ fixation.  Almost all the
free energy in the biosphere ultimately comes from sunlight.

## Why biochemistry matters

- **Medicine** — every disease has a molecular basis;
  every drug acts on a biochemical target.  Modern
  medicine is largely applied biochemistry.
- **Agriculture** — crop improvement + pesticide design +
  fertiliser synthesis (Haber-Bosch is industrial-scale
  biochemistry).
- **Industry** — fermentation (beer, antibiotics, bio-
  fuels), enzyme catalysis (laundry detergents, baking,
  paper-pulp processing), industrial biotech (insulin,
  factor VIII, monoclonal antibodies).
- **Forensics** — DNA fingerprinting, toxicology.
