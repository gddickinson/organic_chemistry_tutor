# Enzymes — the basics

An enzyme is a protein (or, less commonly, an RNA) that
catalyses a chemical reaction without being consumed in
the process.  Enzymes are the engines of metabolism +
signalling — almost every reaction inside a cell is
enzyme-catalysed.

## Catalysis = lower activation energy

A chemical reaction at body temperature has a free-energy
profile with a transition-state barrier.  Enzymes lower
that barrier, making the reaction faster — typically by
**factors of 10⁶ - 10¹⁷** over the uncatalysed rate.

The textbook example:

- Uncatalysed CO₂ + H₂O → HCO₃⁻ + H⁺ — would take
  ~ seconds at body temperature.  Already fast for
  uncatalysed chemistry.
- **Carbonic anhydrase II**-catalysed: ~ 10⁶ molecules
  per second.  ~ 10⁷-fold rate enhancement.  One of the
  fastest enzymes known.

The enzyme doesn't change the **equilibrium** — both
forward + reverse reactions are accelerated equally; the
final equilibrium constant is unchanged.  Enzymes change
**rates**, not thermodynamics.

## The active site

Catalysis happens in a small pocket on the enzyme — the
**active site** — where:

- The substrate(s) bind in a defined geometry.
- Catalytic residues (often Asp, Glu, His, Cys, Ser,
  Lys, Tyr) interact directly with the substrate.
- Cofactors (metal ions, NAD+, FAD, PLP, etc.) provide
  reactive chemistry the protein side chains can't.

Active-site geometry gives enzymes:

- **Substrate specificity** — the right shape + charge
  pattern for one substrate, not others.
- **Stereoselectivity** — usually only one stereoisomer
  binds productively (chymotrypsin recognises L-amino-
  acid substrates; cellulase but not amylase cleaves
  β-1,4-glucosidic bonds).
- **Regioselectivity** — the right bond is acted on,
  not nearby ones.

## Catalytic strategies

How does the enzyme actually accelerate the reaction?
A few common strategies:

- **Proximity + orientation** — bring substrates together
  in the right geometry; this alone can give ~ 10⁵-fold
  rate enhancement.
- **Acid-base catalysis** — proton transfer to/from the
  substrate.  His residues often sit at near-neutral
  pKa to act as both acid + base.
- **Covalent catalysis** — the enzyme forms a transient
  covalent bond with the substrate (Ser-O-acyl in
  serine proteases; Lys-imine in PLP enzymes; Cys-
  thioacyl in some metabolic enzymes).
- **Metal-ion catalysis** — Lewis-acid activation,
  redox chemistry, electrostatic stabilisation.
- **Transition-state stabilisation** — the active site
  is **complementary to the transition state**, not the
  substrate, so binding lowers the activation energy.
  Linus Pauling's 1948 insight is still the dominant
  framework.

## EC classification — the 7 enzyme classes

The Enzyme Commission (1961, 7-class system since 2018)
classifies every enzyme by its catalysed reaction:

| Class | Reaction | Examples |
|-------|----------|----------|
| EC 1 — Oxidoreductase | Redox | Alcohol dehydrogenase, GAPDH, Complex IV |
| EC 2 — Transferase | Group transfer | Hexokinase, EGFR-TK, COMT, UGT |
| EC 3 — Hydrolase | Hydrolysis | Chymotrypsin, lysozyme, ACE, HIV protease |
| EC 4 — Lyase | Bond breaking without hydrolysis | Aldolase, carbonic anhydrase, adenylate cyclase |
| EC 5 — Isomerase | Rearrangement | TIM, phosphoglycerate mutase, cyclophilin A |
| EC 6 — Ligase | Bond-forming with ATP | DNA ligase, glutamine synthetase, ACC |
| EC 7 — Translocase | Active transport across membrane | Na+/K+-ATPase, F1F0-ATP synthase, P-glycoprotein |

The 7th class (Translocase) was carved out from the older
EC 3.6 + EC 5 in 2018 — recognising that ATP-driven
membrane transport is its own catalytic biology.

## Naming conventions

Enzymes are typically named for:

- **Substrate + reaction** (most modern names): glucose-6-
  phosphatase hydrolyses glucose-6-phosphate; lactate
  dehydrogenase oxidises lactate.
- **Trivial names** (historical): trypsin, pepsin,
  papain — names predating the systematic system.
- **EC number** — the canonical identifier: EC 4.2.1.1
  is carbonic anhydrase II.

## Why enzymes matter

- **Life is impossible without them** — metabolism would
  be far too slow at body temperature.
- **Drug targets** — most drugs are enzyme inhibitors
  (statins → HMG-CoA reductase; ACE inhibitors → ACE;
  HIV protease inhibitors; aspirin → COX).
- **Industrial catalysts** — laundry detergents (subtilisin,
  amylase, lipase), diagnostics (glucose oxidase),
  research (DNA polymerase + restriction enzymes for
  cloning), brewing + baking + cheese-making.
