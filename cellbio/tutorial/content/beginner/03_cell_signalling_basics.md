# Cell signalling — the basics

Cells need to talk to each other. The molecular machinery
that makes this possible is **cell signalling** — and it's
the central organising theme of Cell Biology Studio.

## Why cells signal

Multicellular life requires coordination. A liver cell
needs to know when blood glucose drops; a T cell needs to
know that a virus-infected target is in front of it; a
neuron needs to fire when its upstream input crosses
threshold.

All of these decisions are made by signalling pathways —
molecular cascades that detect a signal, amplify it, and
translate it into a cellular response (gene expression,
metabolism, motility, division, death).

## The four signalling modes

Distance + delivery method classify signalling:

- **Endocrine** — hormone released into blood, reaches
  distant target. Insulin from pancreas → muscle.
- **Paracrine** — local signal to nearby cells. Histamine
  from mast cell → nearby vessels.
- **Autocrine** — cell signals to itself. IL-2 from
  activated T cell → same cell's IL-2 receptor.
- **Synaptic** — neuron releases neurotransmitter into a
  narrow synaptic cleft (~ 20 nm) to a specific
  postsynaptic cell. Acetylcholine at neuromuscular
  junction.

Direct cell-cell contact (gap junctions, juxtacrine via
notch, immune synapses) is sometimes counted as a fifth
mode.

## The signalling pipeline

Almost every signalling pathway has the same five-step
shape:

1. **Reception** — a ligand binds a receptor (cell-surface
   or intracellular).
2. **Transduction** — the receptor activates a downstream
   second messenger or enzyme cascade.
3. **Amplification** — one ligand → many downstream events
   (one GPCR can activate many G-proteins; each G-protein
   can activate adenylate cyclase to make hundreds of
   cAMP).
4. **Integration** — multiple inputs converge on shared
   downstream nodes (MAPK, NF-κB, p53). Cross-talk allows
   conditional decisions.
5. **Response + termination** — gene transcription,
   metabolic switch, etc., then phosphatases + GTPase-
   activating proteins + receptor internalisation shut
   the signal off.

## Receptor superfamilies

Most signalling fits one of four receptor classes:

- **GPCRs** — 7-transmembrane receptors coupling to
  heterotrimeric G-proteins. ~ 800 in the human genome,
  > 30 % of approved drugs. Examples: β-adrenergic, μ-
  opioid, muscarinic.
- **RTKs** — receptor tyrosine kinases. Single-pass
  transmembrane receptors that dimerise + trans-
  autophosphorylate on ligand binding. EGFR, insulin
  receptor, VEGFR.
- **Cytokine receptors** — single-pass transmembrane,
  signal via JAK-STAT. IFN, IL-2, GH.
- **Nuclear receptors** — intracellular, ligand-activated
  transcription factors. Glucocorticoid, oestrogen,
  thyroid, vitamin D.

Plus assorted others: TLRs (innate immunity), ion-channel
receptors (nAChR, NMDA), guanylate cyclases, TGF-β family,
notch, frizzled (Wnt), patched (Hedgehog).

## Second messengers

Small intracellular molecules that propagate signals from
membrane to cytoplasm + nucleus:

- **cAMP** — generated by adenylate cyclase from ATP;
  activates PKA. Downstream of Gαs-coupled receptors.
- **cGMP** — by guanylate cyclase; activates PKG.
- **IP3 + DAG** — from PIP2 by PLCβ; IP3 releases ER Ca²⁺,
  DAG activates PKC.
- **Ca²⁺** — universal second messenger; cytosol [Ca²⁺]
  is held ~ 100 nM at rest, rises 10-100× on signalling.
- **Inositol phosphates + lipid second messengers** —
  PIP3 (PI3K product) recruits Akt to membrane.

## Why this matters for medicine

About a third of approved drugs target GPCRs alone. Cancer
therapeutics increasingly target signalling nodes (kinase
inhibitors, monoclonal antibodies). Auto-immunity targets
cytokine signalling (anti-TNF, JAK inhibitors).
Understanding signalling pathways IS understanding modern
pharmacology.
