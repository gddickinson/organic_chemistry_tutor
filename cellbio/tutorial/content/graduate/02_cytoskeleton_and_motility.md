# Cytoskeleton + cell motility

The cytoskeleton is three protein networks (actin
microfilaments, microtubules, intermediate filaments) that
give the cell its shape, internal organisation, division
machinery, and the ability to move + change form. It's
also the substrate for several major drug classes.

## Actin microfilaments

**Globular actin (G-actin)** monomers polymerise into
**filamentous (F-actin)** double-helical filaments,
~ 7 nm diameter. Polymerisation is **polar** — the barbed
(+) end grows ~ 10× faster than the pointed (-) end.

The basic monomer cycle:

1. ATP-G-actin binds the barbed end + adds.
2. Filament-bound actin slowly hydrolyses ATP → ADP-Pi
   → ADP.
3. ADP-actin at the pointed end depolymerises.

This **treadmilling** keeps filaments at steady-state
length while individual subunits flow through them — the
basis of cell migration.

Major regulators:

- **Arp2/3 complex** — nucleates branched-network
  assembly (lamellipodia at the leading edge).
- **Formins** (mDia, FH-family) — nucleate unbranched
  parallel bundles (filopodia, stress fibres).
- **Profilin** — keeps G-actin ATP-loaded + ready to add.
- **Cofilin / ADF** — severs ADP-actin filaments at the
  pointed end.
- **Capping protein** — caps barbed ends to terminate
  growth.
- **Rho GTPases** — RhoA → stress fibres + contractility;
  Rac1 → lamellipodia; Cdc42 → filopodia. Master
  regulators of all of the above.

## Microtubules

**α + β tubulin** heterodimers polymerise into hollow
tubes ~ 25 nm diameter, 13 protofilaments. Like actin, MTs
are polar (+ end + - end), but the dynamics are dramatic:

**Dynamic instability** (Mitchison + Kirschner 1984) —
individual MT plus-ends switch between growth phases
(slow, GTP-tubulin addition) and rapid shrinkage
("catastrophe"). After GTP hydrolysis on β-tubulin, the
GTP-cap protects the growing end; loss of the cap →
catastrophe → rapid disassembly.

Why dynamic instability? It lets the mitotic spindle
**search-and-capture** kinetochores — MTs constantly grow
+ shrink in random directions until they find a binding
target.

Major regulators:

- **γ-tubulin ring complex (γ-TuRC)** at centrosomes
  nucleates new MTs.
- **EB1, +TIPs** track + protect growing plus-ends.
- **Kinesins** — plus-end-directed motors. Drive vesicle
  transport, mitotic spindle organisation.
- **Dynein** — minus-end-directed motor. Drives
  retrograde axonal transport, kinetochore-to-pole MT
  transport in mitosis.
- **Stathmin / Op18** — sequesters tubulin dimers,
  promotes catastrophe.
- **Katanin, Spastin** — sever MTs.

## Intermediate filaments

The third class — ~ 10 nm diameter, more stable + more
"structural" than actin or MTs. Tissue-specific:

- **Cytokeratins** (50+ in humans) — epithelia. Type I +
  II coiled-coil.
- **Vimentin** — mesenchyme. Migration + EMT.
- **Desmin** — muscle.
- **GFAP** — astrocytes.
- **Neurofilaments** — axons.
- **Lamins** (A/B/C) — nuclear lamina, mechanical
  support of nuclear envelope. Mutations cause
  laminopathies (Hutchinson-Gilford progeria).

IFs assemble without nucleotide hydrolysis — they're
strictly structural, not motile.

## Motor proteins — myosins, kinesins, dyneins

All three superfamilies share an ATP-binding + force-
generating "motor head" + a tail that engages cargo.

- **Myosins** — ~ 39 human genes. Myosin II drives
  muscle contraction + cytokinesis; myosin I drives
  membrane trafficking; myosin V drives vesicle transport
  on actin.
- **Kinesins** — ~ 45 human genes. Kinesin-1 (KIF5)
  drives axonal transport; kinesin-5 (Eg5) drives
  spindle bipolarity (target of monastrol family).
- **Dyneins** — cytoplasmic + axonemal (cilia/flagella);
  smaller gene family but huge protein complex (~ 1.5 MDa).

All step ~ 8 nm per ATP hydrolysed at a few µm / sec.
The mechanochemistry is exquisite: bound state, ADP-Pi
release coupled to lever-arm swing, ADP release coupled
to release from substrate.

## Cell migration mechanics

Crawling cell migration in fibroblasts + leukocytes:

1. **Leading-edge protrusion** — Rac1-driven Arp2/3-
   nucleated branched actin pushes the membrane forward
   into a lamellipodium.
2. **Filopodia** extend from leading edge — Cdc42-driven
   formin-nucleated parallel actin bundles.
3. **Adhesion** — integrins clutch onto ECM at the
   leading edge → focal complex → focal adhesion.
4. **Contraction** — RhoA-driven myosin II contracts
   stress fibres → cell body is pulled forward.
5. **Rear retraction** — calpain cleaves trailing
   integrins → cell tail pulls forward.

Coordinated by Rho-GTPase spatial gradient (Rac at front,
Rho at rear) + mechano-feedback from substrate stiffness.

## Drugs that target the cytoskeleton

- **Microtubule stabilisers** — taxanes (paclitaxel,
  docetaxel, cabazitaxel) bind β-tubulin + lock MTs in a
  polymerised state. Spindle becomes nonfunctional →
  mitotic arrest → apoptosis. First-line breast + ovarian
  + lung + prostate cancer.
- **Microtubule destabilisers** — vinca alkaloids
  (vincristine, vinblastine, vinorelbine), eribulin.
  Bind tubulin + prevent polymerisation. Same downstream
  → mitotic catastrophe.
- **Colchicine** — gout therapy + research tubulin
  inhibitor. Same destabilising mechanism.
- **Cytochalasins** — actin inhibitors; not therapeutic
  but ubiquitous research reagent.
- **Latrunculins** — sequester G-actin; also research-
  only.
- **Blebbistatin** — myosin II inhibitor.
- **Eg5 (kinesin-5) inhibitors** — monastrol, ispinesib,
  filanesib. Mitotic-spindle-specific; in oncology
  trials but no approval.

## Disease associations

- **Cancer** — taxane + vinca therapy is built around
  cytoskeletal targeting.
- **Neurodegeneration** — defects in axonal transport
  (KIF5, dynein) cause hereditary spastic paraplegia +
  Charcot-Marie-Tooth.
- **Ciliopathies** — defective primary cilia (basal-body
  + IFT defects) cause polycystic kidney disease,
  Bardet-Biedl, polydactyly.
- **Laminopathies** — LMNA mutations → Emery-Dreifuss
  muscular dystrophy, dilated cardiomyopathy, progeria.
