# Mendelian genetics + classical chromosomes

The genetic concepts that pre-date molecular biology
remain essential — they frame how molecular techniques
are interpreted in clinical + agricultural + research
contexts.

## Mendel's laws

Gregor Mendel (1856-1863, *Pisum sativum*):

1. **Law of segregation** — each parent contributes
   one of two alleles per gene to each offspring;
   gametes carry only one allele.
2. **Law of independent assortment** — alleles of
   different genes segregate independently (true for
   genes on different chromosomes; modified by linkage
   for genes on the same chromosome).
3. **Principle of dominance** — one allele can mask
   another.

Mendel's pioneering paper (1866 *Verhandlungen des
naturforschenden Vereines in Brünn*) was overlooked
for ~ 35 years; rediscovered independently by de Vries,
Correns, + Tschermak in 1900.

## Allele relationships

For a gene with two alleles (A, a):

- **Complete dominance** — heterozygote shows the
  dominant phenotype.
- **Incomplete dominance** — heterozygote is
  intermediate.
- **Co-dominance** — both alleles expressed
  simultaneously (ABO blood group).
- **Multiple alleles** — one gene with > 2 allele
  options (ABO has 3; HLA has thousands).
- **Lethal alleles** — homozygous-lethal (yellow
  agouti / *AY/AY* in mouse).
- **Pleiotropy** — one gene affects multiple traits
  (sickle-cell HbS).

## Mendelian inheritance patterns

### Autosomal dominant
- One copy expresses; affected parent has 50 % chance
  of affected child.
- Examples: Huntington's, achondroplasia, NF1, FAP,
  Marfan, OI, ADPKD.

### Autosomal recessive
- Both copies must carry the variant; carriers (one
  copy) are usually unaffected.
- Examples: cystic fibrosis, sickle-cell, Tay-Sachs,
  PKU, β-thalassaemia, Friedreich's ataxia.

### X-linked recessive
- Males (XY) need only one copy → far more often
  affected; females (XX) usually carriers.
- Examples: haemophilia A + B, DMD, colour blindness,
  G6PD deficiency.

### X-linked dominant
- Affected mother → 50 % of all children affected;
  affected father → 100 % daughters affected, 0 %
  sons.
- Examples: incontinentia pigmenti, vitamin-D-resistant
  rickets, Rett syndrome.

### Y-linked
- Father → all sons affected, no daughters.
- Examples: rare; some male-infertility loci.

### Mitochondrial (maternal)
- Only the mother transmits; all children of an
  affected mother carry the variant (heteroplasmy
  modifies expression).
- Examples: LHON, MELAS, MERRF, Leigh syndrome.

## Beyond strict Mendelian

Many traits don't follow simple Mendelian patterns:

- **Incomplete penetrance** — only a fraction of
  variant carriers express the phenotype (BRCA1 +
  BRCA2 ~ 60-80 % lifetime breast-cancer risk).
- **Variable expressivity** — same genotype, different
  severity (NF1).
- **Genetic anticipation** — disease severity worsens
  + onset earlier in successive generations
  (trinucleotide-repeat disorders — Huntington's,
  Fragile X, myotonic dystrophy, SCAs).
- **Imprinting** — only the maternal or paternal
  allele expressed at certain loci (Prader-Willi,
  Angelman, BWS).
- **X-inactivation (Lyonisation)** — random
  inactivation of one X chromosome in female cells →
  mosaic expression of X-linked traits.
- **Mosaicism** — somatic-mutation post-zygotic
  cells carry different genotypes within an individual.
- **Polygenic + multifactorial** — most common
  diseases (T2DM, hypertension, schizophrenia, common
  cancers).

## Chromosomes — classical view

Human cells:
- **22 pairs of autosomes** + **1 pair of sex
  chromosomes** = 46 chromosomes (diploid; 2N).
- **Gametes** are haploid (1N = 23 chromosomes).
- **Karyotype** notation: 46,XY (normal male) /
  46,XX (normal female) / 47,XX,+21 (Down syndrome) /
  45,X (Turner) / 47,XXY (Klinefelter).

Chromosome morphology:
- **Centromere** — kinetochore-binding constriction
  (metacentric, submetacentric, acrocentric).
- **Telomere** — protective end-cap (TTAGGG repeat
  in vertebrates).
- **Banding patterns** — G-banding (Giemsa) +
  R-banding (reverse) + Q-banding (quinacrine);
  classical cytogenetic readout.

## Chromosomal abnormalities

### Aneuploidies (whole-chromosome)
- **Trisomy 21** — Down syndrome (1 / 700 live
  births).
- **Trisomy 18** — Edwards syndrome.
- **Trisomy 13** — Patau syndrome.
- **47,XXY** — Klinefelter (1 / 600 males).
- **45,X** — Turner (1 / 2 500 females).
- **47,XYY** + **47,XXX** — usually milder.

### Structural rearrangements
- **Translocations** — segment swap between non-
  homologous chromosomes.  Reciprocal vs Robertsonian.
  Philadelphia chromosome (t(9;22) BCR-ABL fusion in
  CML) is canonical.
- **Inversions** — segment flipped within
  chromosome.  Pericentric vs paracentric.
- **Deletions** — DiGeorge / 22q11 (most common
  microdeletion); 1p36 / 5p- (cri-du-chat).
- **Duplications** — Charcot-Marie-Tooth 1A (PMP22).
- **Insertions** + **isochromosomes** + **rings**.

### Copy-number variants (CNVs)
- Sub-chromosomal duplications + deletions (~ kb to
  Mb scale).
- Detected by chromosomal microarray (CMA) — first-
  line for developmental delay / autism workup.
- Common-CNV background — average individual has ~
  100-200 deletions + ~ 30-40 duplications relative
  to reference.

### Microdeletion syndromes
- **DiGeorge / 22q11.2**.
- **Williams (7q11)**.
- **Smith-Magenis (17p11)**.
- **Prader-Willi + Angelman (15q11-13)** —
  imprinting-dependent.
- **Cri-du-chat (5p-)**.

## Cytogenetic + molecular diagnostic methods

- **Karyotype** — G-banded metaphase; ~ 5 Mb
  resolution.
- **FISH** — locus-specific 100-kb resolution.
- **CMA / arrayCGH** — 50-200 kb resolution, genome-
  wide CNV.
- **MLPA** — gene-targeted CNV analysis.
- **Targeted panel sequencing** — gene-specific
  variant detection.
- **Whole-exome sequencing (WES)** — coding-region
  variant discovery.
- **Whole-genome sequencing (WGS)** — comprehensive;
  including structural variants + non-coding.
- **Optical genome mapping** (Bionano Saphyr) —
  direct visualisation of large structural
  variants.

A broad shift since ~ 2010 has been from karyotype
+ FISH toward CMA + WES + WGS as first-line tests
for paediatric + prenatal diagnostics.

## Pedigree analysis

Traditional analytical tool:
- **Squares** = males; **circles** = females; filled
  = affected; vertical line down from couple to
  children; horizontal line between siblings.
- **Diamond** = unknown sex; **diagonal slash** =
  deceased.
- Roman numerals (I, II, III) for generations;
  Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3) for individuals within
  a generation.

Pedigree patterns reveal the inheritance mode +
guide molecular testing strategy.

## Carrier screening + reproductive genetics

- **Carrier screening** — pre-conception or
  prenatal; expanded panels test for ~ 100-500
  recessive conditions.
- **Non-invasive prenatal testing (NIPT)** — cell-
  free fetal DNA in maternal plasma; T13 + T18 + T21
  + sex chromosomes; gaining microdeletion +
  monogenic-disease coverage.
- **CVS** + **amniocentesis** — invasive; karyotype
  / CMA / sequencing on fetal cells.
- **Pre-implantation genetic testing (PGT)** — IVF
  embryos screened before transfer; PGT-A
  (aneuploidy), PGT-M (monogenic), PGT-SR
  (structural rearrangement).

## Why this matters for molecular biology

Understanding Mendelian + classical genetics is
essential for:
- Designing + interpreting genetic-disease studies.
- Communicating risk + counselling families.
- Choosing appropriate molecular tests.
- Understanding linkage + GWAS results (the
  intermediate / advanced lessons cover this in
  depth).
- Building model-organism crosses + transgenic /
  knockout strategies.
