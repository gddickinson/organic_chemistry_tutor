# Nervous systems + neuroscience essentials

The nervous system is animal biology's signature
innovation — a network of specialised cells (neurons)
that transmit electrical + chemical signals enabling
sensing, integration, motor control, behaviour, and
ultimately cognition.

## Evolution of nervous systems

### Sponges + placozoa

No nervous system.  Yet sponges have many
"neuronal" gene homologues — neural-like signalling
existed at the cellular level before neurons did.

### Cnidarians + ctenophores

The first nervous systems appear here:
- **Cnidarian nerve nets** — diffuse non-centralised
  network; uniform neuron polarity unclear; can
  conduct action potentials in any direction.
- **Ctenophores** — possibly an INDEPENDENT origin
  of nervous systems (the "alternative origins"
  hypothesis from comparative genomics).

### Bilaterians

Centralised nervous systems with cephalised brains
appeared with bilaterian symmetry.  Two main plans:

- **Ladder-like ganglia** — flatworms, annelids,
  arthropods; segmental ganglia connected by
  longitudinal nerve cords; cephalic ganglion
  prominent.
- **Vertebrate dorsal hollow nerve tube** — chordates;
  derived from neural-tube formation; CNS = brain +
  spinal cord; PNS = cranial + spinal nerves.

### Cephalopod intelligence

Octopuses + cuttlefish + squid have the largest
invertebrate brains + complex behaviours rivalling
vertebrates.  Two-thirds of an octopus's neurons are
in its arms — distributed cognition.  Convergent
evolution of intelligence.

### Vertebrate brain elaboration

Major events:
- **Forebrain elaboration** in early jawed
  vertebrates.
- **Cerebellum** for motor coordination.
- **Mammalian neocortex** — six-layered; expanded
  enormously in primates + cetaceans.
- **Avian pallium** — different anatomy but
  functionally cognitive equivalent (corvid /
  parrot intelligence).

## Neuron architecture

A typical neuron:
- **Soma (cell body)** — nucleus + protein synthesis.
- **Dendrites** — receive signals (often thousands of
  synaptic inputs).
- **Axon** — single output process; can be > 1 m
  long (giraffe sciatic nerve).
- **Axon terminals** — synapse with target cells.

Neurons fall in many morphological + functional
classes:
- **Sensory** (afferent) — detect stimuli.
- **Motor** (efferent) — drive muscles + glands.
- **Interneurons** — local integration in CNS.
- **Projection neurons** — long-range CNS-CNS
  communication.

## Action potentials

The fundamental unit of neural signalling.

Mechanism:
1. Resting membrane potential ~ -70 mV maintained by
   Na/K-ATPase + leak K+ channels.
2. Depolarisation past threshold (~ -55 mV) →
   voltage-gated Na+ channels open → Na+ influx →
   further depolarisation (positive feedback) →
   peak ~ +30 mV.
3. Na+ channels rapidly inactivate; voltage-gated
   K+ channels open → K+ efflux → repolarisation.
4. Brief hyperpolarisation as K+ channels close
   slowly.
5. Refractory period (Na+ channels need time to
   recover).

Hodgkin-Huxley 1952 — quantitative model of action
potential; Nobel 1963.

### Conduction

- **Unmyelinated** axons — continuous propagation
  along axon membrane; slow (0.5-2 m/s).
- **Myelinated** axons (vertebrates) — saltatory
  conduction between Nodes of Ranvier; fast
  (up to 100+ m/s).
- **Demyelination** (multiple sclerosis,
  Guillain-Barré) impairs conduction → motor +
  sensory symptoms.

### Channelopathies

Mutations in voltage-gated ion channels cause many
diseases:
- **SCN1A** (Nav1.1) — Dravet syndrome epilepsy.
- **CACNA1S** (Cav1.1) — hypokalaemic periodic
  paralysis.
- **KCNQ1** + **KCNH2** + **SCN5A** — long QT
  syndrome.
- **CFTR** (Cl- channel) — cystic fibrosis.
- **TRPV1** — capsaicin receptor + pain pathway.

## Synapses

Where signals transfer between neurons (or
neuron-to-effector cell):

### Chemical synapses (most common)

1. Action potential reaches presynaptic terminal.
2. Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open; Ca²⁺ enters.
3. Ca²⁺ triggers SNARE-mediated synaptic-vesicle
   fusion with presynaptic membrane.
4. Neurotransmitter released into synaptic cleft.
5. Diffuses (10s of nm) to postsynaptic receptors.
6. Receptors activate → postsynaptic potential
   (excitatory EPSP or inhibitory IPSP).
7. Reuptake / enzymatic degradation / diffusion
   clears transmitter.

The fast Ca²⁺ sensor is **synaptotagmin-1** (Sudhof);
SNARE complex zippering driven by **synaptobrevin /
syntaxin / SNAP-25**.  Botulinum + tetanus toxins
cleave SNAREs (covered in CB-3.0 graduate "Membrane
trafficking" lesson).

### Electrical synapses

Direct electrical coupling via gap junctions
(connexins).  Bidirectional + fast (no synaptic
delay).  Important in cardiac myocardium + retinal
+ developmental + escape circuits.

## Neurotransmitters

### Small-molecule (classical)

- **Glutamate** — main excitatory NT in CNS;
  ionotropic (AMPA, NMDA, kainate) +
  metabotropic (mGluR1-8) receptors.
- **GABA** — main inhibitory NT in adult CNS;
  GABA-A (ionotropic Cl- channel; benzo / barb /
  alcohol target) + GABA-B (metabotropic).
- **Glycine** — inhibitory in spinal cord +
  brainstem; strychnine antagonist.
- **Acetylcholine** — neuromuscular junction;
  parasympathetic; cholinergic projections in
  brain (basal forebrain — Alzheimer relevance).
  Nicotinic + muscarinic receptors.
- **Dopamine** — reward, motor (substantia nigra
  → striatum; Parkinson's),  prefrontal cortex
  cognition.  D1 / D2 / D3 / D4 / D5 receptors.
- **Serotonin (5-HT)** — mood, sleep, appetite;
  raphe nuclei → widespread projection.  > 14
  receptor subtypes.  SSRI target.
- **Noradrenaline (norepinephrine)** — arousal,
  attention; locus coeruleus.
- **Adrenaline (epinephrine)** — minor CNS NT but
  major hormone.
- **Histamine** — wakefulness; tuberomammillary
  nucleus; H1 antihistamine sedation.

### Neuropeptides

> 100 known.  Co-released with classical NTs.
- **Endogenous opioids** — endorphins, enkephalins,
  dynorphins.  μ + δ + κ opioid receptors.
- **Substance P** — pain transmission.
- **CGRP** — migraine pathophysiology.
- **CRH, ACTH, oxytocin, vasopressin, TRH, somatostatin**
  — hypothalamic + pituitary peptides.
- **Neuropeptide Y, melanocortins, orexin /
  hypocretin** — feeding + arousal.

### Other transmitters

- **Adenosine** — sleep regulation; coffee blocks
  A1/A2A receptors.
- **ATP** — purinergic signalling (P2X / P2Y
  receptors).
- **Endocannabinoids** (anandamide, 2-AG) —
  retrograde signalling; CB1 / CB2 receptors.
- **Nitric oxide (NO)** — gaseous + diffuses freely;
  retrograde signalling.

## Neural circuits + integration

### Reflex arcs

Simplest functional unit:
- Stimulus → sensory neuron → spinal-cord
  interneuron → motor neuron → effector.
- Knee-jerk (monosynaptic) is one of the simplest;
  most reflexes have multiple interneurons.

### Central pattern generators (CPGs)

Networks of interneurons producing rhythmic outputs
(walking, swimming, breathing) without continuous
sensory input.

### Cortical microcircuits

Six-layered neocortex with stereotyped local
microcircuit motifs (canonical microcircuit) +
long-range connectivity.

### Brain regions + functions

- **Cortex** — sensation, motor, perception, cognition,
  language, executive function.
- **Hippocampus** — episodic memory, spatial navigation
  (place cells, grid cells; O'Keefe + Mosers Nobel
  2014).
- **Amygdala** — emotion + fear processing.
- **Basal ganglia** — action selection + motor
  control + reward.
- **Cerebellum** — motor coordination + timing +
  some cognition.
- **Thalamus** — sensory + motor relay; gateway to
  cortex.
- **Hypothalamus** — homeostasis + endocrine + autonomic
  + drives.
- **Brainstem** — autonomic + sleep / arousal +
  cranial-nerve nuclei.
- **Spinal cord** — sensory + motor pathways +
  reflex circuitry.

## Glial cells

> 50 % of human brain cells.  Once dismissed as
"glue", now recognised as active participants:

- **Astrocytes** — synaptic support, BBB, K+ buffering,
  glutamate uptake, gliotransmitter release.
- **Oligodendrocytes** + **Schwann cells** — myelination.
- **Microglia** — CNS immune cells; phagocytose debris;
  synaptic pruning during development.
- **Ependymal cells** — line ventricles + spinal canal.

## Plasticity + learning

### Synaptic plasticity

Synaptic strength modifiable by experience:
- **LTP** (long-term potentiation) — Hebbian
  "neurons that fire together, wire together".
  NMDA-receptor-dependent in hippocampal CA1.
- **LTD** (long-term depression) — opposite of LTP.
- **STDP** (spike-timing-dependent plasticity) —
  temporal precedence determines potentiation
  vs depression.
- Mechanisms: AMPA-receptor trafficking, dendritic-
  spine remodelling, gene expression changes,
  protein synthesis.

### Adult neurogenesis

Limited regions in adult mammals:
- Hippocampal subgranular zone → dentate granule
  cells.
- Subventricular zone → olfactory bulb (in rodents;
  controversial in humans).

### Critical periods

Sensitive periods in early development when neural
circuits are most modifiable (visual cortex,
language acquisition, song-bird vocal learning).
Reopening critical periods is an active research
area for stroke + sensory rehabilitation.

## Major neurological + psychiatric diseases

- **Alzheimer's** — amyloid + tau; cholinergic
  degeneration.
- **Parkinson's** — substantia nigra dopamine neuron
  loss; α-synuclein aggregation.
- **Multiple sclerosis** — autoimmune demyelination.
- **Stroke** — cerebrovascular ischemia.
- **Epilepsy** — synchronised hyperactive networks.
- **Depression + anxiety** — monoamine systems +
  HPA axis + plasticity.
- **Schizophrenia** — dopaminergic + glutamatergic
  + neurodevelopmental.
- **ASD** — neurodevelopmental, polygenic + rare
  CNVs.
- **ALS** — motor neuron degeneration; SOD1, TDP-43,
  FUS, C9orf72.
- **Huntington's** — CAG-repeat in huntingtin gene.
