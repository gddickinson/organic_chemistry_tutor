# Comparative animal physiology

Animals have solved the same physiological problems
— gas exchange, circulation, digestion, excretion,
osmoregulation, thermoregulation — in a remarkable
diversity of ways.  This lesson maps the major
strategies across phyla.

## Circulation

### Open circulation

Hemolymph (blood-equivalent) bathes organs in
sinuses (haemocoel); pumped sluggishly by tubular
hearts.  No clear distinction between blood + tissue
fluid.

- Most molluscs (except cephalopods).
- Arthropods.
- Some smaller invertebrates.

Pros: low energetic cost, no need for high
pressures.  Cons: low efficiency, limited oxygen
delivery, restricts maximum body size + activity
level.

### Closed circulation

Blood confined to vessels; pumped at higher pressure
through a defined circuit.

- Vertebrates.
- Cephalopod molluscs.
- Annelids.
- Some other phyla.

Pros: efficient O2 delivery, supports active
lifestyles + large size, allows specialised
microcirculation (capillaries).

### Single-circuit (fish)

Heart → gills (oxygenation) → systemic
circulation → heart.  One pass through the heart.

Disadvantage: oxygenated blood has lost much pressure
crossing gill capillaries before reaching tissues.

### Double-circuit (amphibian / reptile / bird /
mammal)

Pulmonary circuit (heart → lungs → heart) +
systemic circuit (heart → body → heart) operate
in parallel — heart pumps each twice.

Progression:
- **Amphibians** — 3-chambered heart (2 atria + 1
  ventricle); some mixing of oxygenated +
  deoxygenated blood.
- **Most reptiles** — 3-chambered with partial
  ventricular septum; less mixing.
- **Crocodilians + birds + mammals** —
  4-chambered (2 atria + 2 ventricles); complete
  separation; high systemic + low pulmonary
  pressures.

## Respiration

### Diffusion across body surface

Sponges, cnidarians, flatworms, small bryozoans —
no specialised respiratory organs; rely on body
surface + thinness.  Limits body size.

### Gills

Highly vascularised lamellae extracting O2 from
water.

- **Internal gills** in fish (covered by operculum;
  unidirectional water flow via buccal pumping +
  ram ventilation).
- **External gills** in larval amphibians + many
  invertebrates.
- **Book gills** in horseshoe crabs.

Counter-current exchange in fish gills allows
~ 80-90 % O2 extraction (water flows opposite to
blood).

### Tracheae

Insect respiratory system: branching air-filled
tubes (tracheae → tracheoles) deliver O2 directly to
tissues; no role for blood in O2 transport.  Spiracles
on body surface (closable) regulate water loss.

Pros: efficient at small sizes, decouples O2 delivery
from circulation.  Cons: doesn't scale beyond
~ 10-cm body length without active ventilation.

### Lungs

Internalised gas-exchange surfaces of vertebrates +
some pulmonate snails + other taxa.

- **Amphibians** — sac-like, low SA-to-volume; need
  cutaneous respiration too.
- **Reptiles** — multi-chambered with greater SA.
- **Birds** — UNIDIRECTIONAL air flow through
  parabronchi via 9 air sacs → continuous
  oxygenation, even during exhalation.  ~ 2× more
  efficient than mammalian.  Enables high-altitude
  flight (bar-headed goose over Himalayas).
- **Mammals** — alveolar lungs; bidirectional flow;
  surfactant reduces alveolar surface tension.

### Cutaneous + buccal + pharyngeal respiration

Some species (amphibians, lungless salamanders,
sea snakes) supplement or fully replace pulmonary
with skin-based gas exchange.

## Digestion

### Intracellular digestion

Sponges + some cnidarians digest intracellularly —
choanocytes phagocytose food particles + digest in
food vacuoles.

### Incomplete (sac-like) digestive cavity

Cnidarians + flatworms — single opening serves as
mouth + anus.

### Complete (one-way) digestive tract

Most bilaterians — mouth → gut → anus.  Allows
specialisation of regions:
- **Foregut** — ingestion, mechanical breakdown,
  initial chemical digestion.
- **Midgut** — most chemical digestion + nutrient
  absorption.
- **Hindgut** — water reabsorption + faecal
  consolidation.

### Specialised herbivore strategies

Plant cellulose is hard to digest (vertebrates
lack cellulase) → animals partner with microbial
fermenters:

- **Foregut fermentation (ruminants)** — cattle,
  sheep, goats, deer, giraffe, antelope.  4-chambered
  stomach (rumen + reticulum + omasum + abomasum);
  microbial fermentation in the rumen first;
  regurgitation + re-mastication ("chewing the
  cud").  ~ 70 % of digestion is microbial.  Methane
  belching is a major greenhouse-gas source.
- **Hindgut fermentation** — horses, rabbits,
  rodents, koalas, gorillas.  Caecum + colon
  fermentation; less efficient than foregut but
  faster transit.  Coprophagy (rabbits eat their
  cecotrophs) recovers nutrients.
- **Foregut fermentation (non-ruminant)** — hippos,
  hoatzin (a bird!).
- **Specialised eaters** — anteater + pangolin
  (ants/termites; long sticky tongue + simplified
  GI), nectar-feeders (long tongues + crops).

### Avian digestion

- **Crop** — food storage.
- **Proventriculus** — glandular stomach.
- **Gizzard** — muscular grinding organ; often
  contains swallowed grit.
- **Cloaca** — common urinary + reproductive +
  digestive opening.

### Carnivore + omnivore vertebrate digestion

- Shorter GI relative to body size than herbivores.
- Stomach: high HCl + pepsin for protein digestion.
- Pancreas: trypsin + chymotrypsin + lipase + amylase.
- Liver / bile salts: emulsify fats.
- Small intestine villi + microvilli: absorb nutrients.

## Excretion + osmoregulation

The kidney problem: dispose of nitrogenous waste +
maintain water-salt balance.

### Nitrogenous waste forms

| Excretory product | Solubility / toxicity | Animal type |
|-------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Ammonia (NH3) | Highly soluble + toxic; needs lots of water to dilute | Most aquatic invertebrates + freshwater bony fish |
| Urea | Less toxic; needs less water; energy-cost to synthesise | Mammals, amphibians, marine cartilaginous fish (use as osmolyte!) |
| Uric acid | Insoluble + non-toxic; excreted as paste; minimal water loss | Birds, reptiles, insects, terrestrial snails |

Choice driven by water availability + life history
(uric-acid + egg-laying go together — embryos can't
dispose of ammonia/urea inside an eggshell).

### Excretory organs

- **Protonephridia** (flatworms, rotifers) — flame
  cells with cilia drive filtrate into tubules.
- **Metanephridia** (annelids, molluscs) — open
  funnel + tubule + bladder; collects coelomic fluid.
- **Malpighian tubules** (insects) — empty into
  hindgut; uric-acid secretion + water reabsorption
  in hindgut + rectum.
- **Antennal / maxillary glands** (crustaceans).
- **Vertebrate kidneys** — paired retroperitoneal
  organs; nephron-based filtration + reabsorption +
  secretion.
  - Pronephros (embryonic in vertebrates; functional
    in some larval fish).
  - Mesonephros (functional in fish + amphibians;
    embryonic in amniotes).
  - Metanephros (definitive amniote kidney).

### Vertebrate nephron

The functional unit:
1. **Glomerulus** — high-pressure filtration
   (~ 180 L / day filtrate in human).
2. **Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT)** — bulk
   reabsorption of water + Na + K + glucose + amino
   acids + bicarbonate.
3. **Loop of Henle** — counter-current multiplier;
   establishes medullary osmotic gradient.
4. **Distal convoluted tubule (DCT)** — fine-tuned
   Na + K + Ca + Mg handling.
5. **Collecting duct** — final water + acid /
   base + K+ adjustment under hormonal control
   (ADH / vasopressin → water reabsorption;
   aldosterone → Na + K).

Mammalian kidney is uniquely capable of producing
HYPERTONIC urine (concentrated above plasma
osmolality) due to the long medullary loop +
counter-current mechanism — enables life in dry
environments.

### Marine vertebrates — diverse strategies

- **Marine cartilaginous fish (sharks, rays)** —
  retain urea + TMAO as osmolytes → match seawater
  osmolality without losing water.
- **Marine bony fish** — drink seawater; gills
  actively excrete excess salt; produce small
  volumes of isotonic urine.
- **Marine mammals + reptiles** — kidney-based
  hypertonic urine + (in reptiles) salt glands.
- **Seabirds** — nasal salt glands (e.g. albatross)
  excrete concentrated NaCl solution.

## Thermoregulation

### Ectothermy

Body temperature determined largely by environment
+ behavioural choice (basking, retreat).

- Most invertebrates + fish + amphibians + reptiles.
- Energetic efficiency — low metabolic baseline; need
  ~ 10× less food than equivalent endotherm.
- Activity tightly tied to ambient temperature.
- Hibernation / torpor common.

### Endothermy

Body temperature maintained by internal heat
production.

- Birds + mammals (and a few unique exceptions:
  some sharks, some bony fish — opah, tuna —
  partial endothermy).
- Sustained activity across temperature range.
- ~ 5-10× higher resting metabolic rate.
- Insulation (fur, feathers, blubber) + countercurrent
  circulation (rete mirabile in extremities) +
  brown adipose tissue (BAT thermogenesis,
  UCP1-mediated).

### Heterothermy

Some animals shift modes:
- **Hibernation** — bears, ground squirrels, marmots
  → metabolic depression + body-temperature drop.
- **Torpor** — daily metabolic depression in some
  small mammals + birds (e.g. hummingbirds at night).
- **Regional endothermy** — tuna + great-white shark
  warm muscles + viscera while maintaining cold
  extremities.
- **Insect endothermy** — bees + bumblebees + moths
  pre-flight warm flight muscles via shivering.

## Sensory physiology — brief sampler

Animals exploit diverse sensory modalities:
- **Vision** — diverse eye types (compound vs camera
  eyes; some species see in UV / IR / polarised
  light).
- **Hearing** — bat + dolphin echolocation (ultra-
  sonic); insect ears in unusual locations (legs,
  abdomen).
- **Mechanoreception** — lateral line in fish +
  amphibians.
- **Electroreception** — sharks + rays + platypus
  + electric fish.
- **Magnetoreception** — birds + sea turtles +
  honeybees.
- **Chemoreception** — moth pheromone detection at
  attomolar concentrations; lobster antennae +
  diverse arthropod chemoreceptors.
- **Proprioception** — joint + muscle position sense.
