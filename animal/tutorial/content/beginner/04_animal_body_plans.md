# Animal body plans

The animal kingdom's morphological diversity reduces
to a small number of recurring architectural themes:
symmetry type, number of germ layers, presence or
absence of a body cavity, segmentation pattern,
direction of mouth-vs-anus development.  Together
these define the major body plans.

## Symmetry

- **Asymmetry** — sponges + placozoa.  No discernible
  axis of mirror or rotational symmetry.
- **Radial** — cnidarians + echinoderms (secondarily).
  Multiple planes of symmetry through a single axis;
  sessile or pelagic floating lifestyle.
- **Bilateral** — all other animals.  Single plane
  of mirror symmetry; cephalisation (head end);
  associated with directed locomotion.

Bilateral symmetry is so dominant that all
bilaterally-symmetric animals are grouped as
**Bilateria** — > 99 % of described animal species.

## Cephalisation

The concentration of sensory + neural structures at
one end of the body — directly tied to bilateral
symmetry + directed locomotion.  Drives:
- Anterior nervous system / brain.
- Anterior sensory organs (eyes, antennae, mouth).
- Anterior-posterior axis as the primary body
  organisation.

Cephalisation correlates strongly with predatory
or active-foraging lifestyles; sessile lineages
(barnacles, bivalves) often exhibit reduced
cephalisation.

## Germ layers

Embryonic tissues from gastrulation:
- **Diploblasts** (2 layers) — ectoderm +
  endoderm.  Cnidarians + ctenophores.  Tissues
  + nerves but no true mesoderm-derived organs.
- **Triploblasts** (3 layers) — ectoderm +
  mesoderm + endoderm.  All bilaterians + sponges
  (debated).  Mesoderm provides muscles, gonads,
  most internal organs.

| Germ layer | Adult derivatives |
|------------|-------------------|
| Ectoderm | epidermis, nervous system, sense organs |
| Mesoderm | muscles, bones, blood, gonads, kidneys, dermis, heart |
| Endoderm | gut lining, liver, pancreas, lungs |

## Body cavity (coelom)

A fluid-filled cavity within the mesoderm that
houses internal organs.  Three states:

- **Acoelomate** — no cavity; mesoderm fills space.
  Flatworms.
- **Pseudocoelomate** — cavity from blastocoel
  remnant, lined incompletely by mesoderm.
  Roundworms (nematodes), rotifers.
- **Eucoelomate (coelomate)** — true cavity entirely
  lined by mesoderm.  Annelids, molluscs,
  arthropods, echinoderms, chordates.

The eucoelom enables:
- Independent movement of organs vs body wall.
- Hydrostatic skeleton in soft-bodied taxa.
- Larger body sizes (organ packing efficiency).
- Specialised peritoneal tissues + suspension
  membranes.

## Segmentation

Repeating modular body units along the AP axis:

- **Lacking** — sponges, cnidarians, flatworms,
  molluscs (mostly), echinoderms.
- **Present** — annelids (every body segment a
  metamere), arthropods (tagmata = grouped
  segments — head + thorax + abdomen in insects),
  vertebrates (somites → segmental muscles +
  vertebrae + spinal nerves).

Segmentation likely evolved at least twice —
annelid + arthropod segmentation appears
homologous; vertebrate somitogenesis uses different
genes.  Some recent comparative work argues for a
common deep-ancestor origin (the "articulata"
hypothesis), but molecular phylogeny + modern
evo-devo suggest convergent evolution.

## Protostomes vs deuterostomes

The two major bilaterian super-clades, distinguished
by embryology:

### Protostomes ("mouth first")

- Blastopore (gastrulation invagination) becomes the
  MOUTH.
- Cleavage typically spiral + determinate (cell fates
  fixed early).
- Coelom forms by **schizocoely** (mesoderm splits
  to form cavity).
- Most invertebrate phyla.

Two super-clades:
- **Lophotrochozoa** — molluscs, annelids, flatworms,
  rotifers, brachiopods, nemerteans.
- **Ecdysozoa** — arthropods, nematodes, priapulids,
  tardigrades, onychophorans.  Defined by ECDYSIS
  (moulting + cuticular shedding).

### Deuterostomes ("mouth second")

- Blastopore becomes the ANUS; mouth forms second.
- Cleavage typically radial + indeterminate (cell
  fates flexible until later).
- Coelom forms by **enterocoely** (mesoderm
  out-pockets from gut endoderm).
- Phyla:
  - **Echinodermata**.
  - **Hemichordata** (acorn worms).
  - **Chordata** (sea squirts, lancelets,
    vertebrates).

The protostome / deuterostome dichotomy was
established embryologically + has held up
beautifully under molecular phylogenetics.

## Hox + body-plan patterning

The discovery of **Hox genes** (homeobox-containing
transcription factors) revealed how body plans are
genetically encoded.

- A single ancestral Hox cluster (probably 7-8
  genes) had a co-linear arrangement: position on
  chromosome ↔ position of expression along the AP
  axis.
- **Whole-genome duplications** in vertebrate
  ancestors gave 4 clusters (HoxA, B, C, D) with
  ~ 39 paralogs total in mammals.
- Hox genes specify segmental identity:
  - Hox-1 → most-anterior structures.
  - Hox-13 → most-posterior structures.
  - Anterior Hox typically expressed first; posterior
    later (temporal collinearity).
- Homeotic mutations swap segment identities —
  *Antennapedia* (legs in place of antennae in fly),
  *Ultrabithorax* (4-winged fly with second thoracic
  pair).

Hox genes + their cis-regulatory landscape are the
"genetic toolkit" of body-plan evolution + a
foundational discovery of evolutionary developmental
biology (evo-devo).

Beyond Hox, dozens of other transcription-factor
families (Pax, Sox, T-box, Forkhead, GATA, MyoD,
Nkx) regulate organ + tissue + cell-type identity
across animals.

## Evo-devo + body-plan evolution

Key insights:
- Different animal body plans share a common
  underlying genetic toolkit that pre-dates the
  Cambrian explosion.
- New morphologies arise through **regulatory
  changes** (cis-element mutations, gene duplications,
  network rewiring) rather than new genes.
- **Deep homology** — limb development uses similar
  signalling pathways (Shh, Wnt, FGF, BMP) in fish
  fins, mouse limbs, fly wings, sea-urchin
  larvae — despite very different morphology.
- **Cambrian explosion** likely triggered by a
  combination of:
  - Genetic toolkit + Hox elaboration.
  - Oxygen rise enabling larger body sizes.
  - Ecological feedback (predation drives armour +
    sensory organs + mobility).
  - Continental + climatic configurations.

## Body-plan diversity tour

| Phylum | Body plan |
|--------|-----------|
| Porifera | Asymmetric, no germ layers, no nervous system, no organs, choanocyte-driven filter feeding |
| Cnidaria | Radial, diploblast, gastrovascular cavity, nematocysts |
| Platyhelminthes | Bilateral, triploblast, acoelomate, flat for diffusion |
| Nematoda | Bilateral, triploblast, pseudocoelomate, hydrostatic skeleton |
| Mollusca | Bilateral but variable, coelomate, mantle + radula + foot, calcium-carbonate shell often |
| Annelida | Bilateral, coelomate, segmented, hydrostatic skeleton |
| Arthropoda | Bilateral, coelomate (reduced), segmented (tagmosis), exoskeleton (chitin), jointed appendages |
| Echinodermata | Pentaradial (secondary; bilateral larva), coelomate, calcium-carbonate ossicles, water-vascular system |
| Chordata | Bilateral, coelomate, dorsal hollow nerve cord, notochord, pharyngeal slits, post-anal tail |

## Vertebrate body plan in detail

A subset of chordates that elaborates the chordate
plan with:
- Vertebral column replacing notochord (mostly).
- Cranium protecting brain.
- Neural-crest cells (a "fourth germ layer")
  contributing to head + face + dental + adrenal +
  enteric nervous system.
- Hox cluster duplications → flexibility in body-
  plan elaboration (limbs, jaws, organ systems).
- Adaptive immune system (jawed vertebrates).
- Endothermy + sustained activity (birds + mammals).

Vertebrate clades:
- Hagfish + lampreys (jawless).
- Cartilaginous fish (sharks, rays).
- Bony fish (most living vertebrates by species
  count).
- Lobe-finned fish.
- Amphibians.
- Reptiles + birds (amniotes; birds nested within
  dinosaurs).
- Mammals (synapsid amniotes).
