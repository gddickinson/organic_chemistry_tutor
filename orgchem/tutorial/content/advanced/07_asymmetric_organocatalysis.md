# Asymmetric organocatalysis

The 2021 Nobel Prize in Chemistry (List + MacMillan) recognised
**organocatalysis** — using small chiral organic molecules,
*not metals*, to perform asymmetric catalysis. By 2025
organocatalysis is one of the three pillars of asymmetric
synthesis alongside metal catalysis + biocatalysis.

## Why it took so long

Asymmetric catalysis was dominated by transition metals
(Sharpless, Noyori, Knowles — 2001 Nobel). Enzymes were known.
But "small chiral molecules drive asymmetric reactions" sat
unappreciated until 2000 even though proline-mediated aldol
chemistry had been published by Hajos + Parrish (1971) +
Eder + Sauer + Wiechert (1971).

List's 2000 *JACS* paper + MacMillan's 2000 *JACS* paper
re-launched the field within months of each other.

## Two activation modes

### 1. Enamine catalysis (List et al.)

A chiral secondary amine (proline, MacMillan imidazolidinones,
diaryl prolinol silyl ethers — the *Jørgensen-Hayashi
catalyst*) condenses with an aldehyde to form a chiral
enamine. The enamine attacks an electrophile from the less-
hindered face → enantioenriched aldol / Michael / Mannich
products.

### 2. Iminium catalysis (MacMillan et al.)

Same secondary amines condense with α,β-unsaturated aldehydes
to form chiral iminium ions. The LUMO of the C=N⁺ activates
the substrate for Michael addition + Diels-Alder + Friedel-
Crafts. Chirality of the catalyst dictates which face of the
iminium is exposed.

## Chiral Brønsted-acid catalysis

**BINOL-phosphoric acids** (Akiyama, Terada, 2004) — chiral
binaphthol-derived strong Brønsted acids that protonate
imines + carbonyls + activate Mannich + transfer-hydrogenation
+ Friedel-Crafts. Phosphoric pKa ~ 2 makes them strong enough
for substrate activation; the 3,3'-substituents control
enantioselectivity.

**Chiral thiourea catalysts** (Jacobsen, Takemoto) — H-bond
donor catalysts that activate nitroalkenes, imines, and
epoxides through dual H-bonding to two sites on the
substrate.

## NHC catalysis

**N-heterocyclic carbenes** generated from imidazolium /
triazolium salts (Bode, Rovis, Glorius) catalyse:

- **Benzoin condensation** (umpolung of aldehydes).
- **Stetter reaction** (acyl anion + Michael acceptor).
- **Homoenolate chemistry** — α,β-unsaturated aldehyde + NHC
  → vinyl Breslow intermediate, attacks at the β-carbon.

## Phase-transfer organocatalysis

**Maruoka catalysts** (chiral binaphthyl-derived quaternary
ammonium salts) enable asymmetric alkylation of glycine
imines under phase-transfer conditions → unnatural amino
acids in > 99 % ee.

**Cinchona alkaloids + their quaternary salts** — quinine,
quinidine, cinchonine, cinchonidine are the workhorse chiral
PTC scaffolds; cheap (extracted from cinchona bark).

## Why organocatalysts win in some niches

- **Air + moisture stable** — no glovebox required.
- **Non-toxic** — pharmaceutical-friendly (no metal residues
  to remove for FDA filings, < 10 ppm Pd specs).
- **Cheap** — proline at $0.50/g vs Rh-BINAP at $50/g.
- **Tunable** — swapping a 3,3'-aryl group on a BINOL acid
  takes one Suzuki coupling.

## Limits

- **Slower** than metal catalysis (TON ~ 100-1000 vs 10⁵-10⁷).
- **Higher loadings** (5-20 mol % typical vs 0.1-1 mol %).
- **Limited reaction scope** — no oxidative additions, no
  C-H activation.
