# Mechanochemistry — synthesis without solvent

**Mechanochemistry** drives reactions by mechanical force —
ball milling, grinding, extrusion, sonication. The
solvent-free or solvent-minimal regime is transformative for
green chemistry: ~ 90 % of organic-synthesis waste is
solvent.

## Hardware

### Ball mills

Two main configurations:

- **Vibratory (mixer) mill** — small jar with one or two
  balls (ZrO₂ or stainless), shaken at 25-30 Hz. Cheap,
  ubiquitous. SPEX 8000, Retsch MM 400.
- **Planetary mill** — larger jars in a revolving carousel,
  jars + carousel rotating in opposite directions. Higher
  energy. Retsch PM 100, Fritsch P-7.

Ball-to-powder ratio (~ 5-30:1 by mass), milling
frequency, ball material, jar volume + atmosphere all
affect outcome.

### Twin-screw extruders

Continuous-flow mechanochemistry: substrate + reagents fed
into a heated extruder; screws shear + mix; product comes
out the die. Used at kg-scale by GSK + others.

### Resonant Acoustic Mixing (RAM)

ResonantAcoustic Mixing platforms (Resodyn LabRAM)
oscillate the whole sample at 60 Hz with no balls;
amplitude up to 100 g. Used for hazardous + heat-sensitive
chemistries.

## What kinds of reactions?

Almost everything has been milled by 2025:

- **Cocrystal formation** — pharmaceutical polymorphs.
- **Co-amorphisation** for drug formulation.
- **Suzuki + Heck + Buchwald cross-couplings** — Pd, base,
  ArX, partner — milled together with ~ 1 drop of solvent
  ("liquid-assisted grinding", LAG).
- **Knoevenagel, aldol, Michael** condensations — solid
  base + carbonyl + nucleophile → product, often higher
  yield than solution.
- **Asymmetric organocatalysis** — proline aldol, MacMillan
  Diels-Alder; one report achieved > 99 % ee in a ball mill.
- **Peptide synthesis** — Hernandez et al. ball-mill SPPS
  with no DMF.
- **C-H activation** — Pd-catalysed C-H arylation in jars
  with no DMA.
- **Cocondensations of MOFs + COFs** — liquid-free, fast.

## Why mechanochemistry differs

The reaction kinetics + thermodynamics aren't always the
same as in solution:

- **Higher local temperature** — ball impact spikes a
  small volume to ~ 200-400 °C briefly.
- **High-pressure spots** — momentary GPa pressures during
  ball collisions.
- **Triboelectric / triboredox** chemistry — electron
  transfer on freshly cleaved surfaces.
- **Different polymorphs** — solid-state reactions often
  produce polymorphs not accessible in solution
  (cocrystal screening).

## Liquid-assisted grinding (LAG)

A few drops of solvent (η = solvent volume / sample mass,
typically η = 0.25-0.5 µL/mg) often accelerate
mechanochemical reactions enormously without dissolving
anything. The solvent acts as a lubricant + a transport
medium for solid surfaces.

Example: Pd-catalysed aryl amination requires DMF in
solution; with LAG, 5 µL EtOH + 100 mg of substrates +
catalyst + base → full conversion in 30 min of milling.

## Polymer mechanochemistry

Force can break covalent bonds in polymers. **Mechanophores**
— specific weak bonds embedded in a polymer chain — open
under stress + signal damage:

- **Spiropyran** — colourless → coloured merocyanine on
  stress.
- **Bis-cyclo(but-2-enyl) dichlorides** — release HCl on
  stretch (a mechano-acid).
- **Self-healing materials** — mechanophore opens to
  initiate radical chemistry that re-crosslinks.

## Industrial scale

- **GSK's twin-screw extruder programme** — kg/day of
  pharmaceutical intermediates without solvent.
- **Hosokawa MD ball mills** — pharma-grade GMP-compliant
  milling for cocrystal screening.
- **Pharmacrysta** + **Crystalsit** — in-house mechanochemical
  pharmaceutical co-crystal formation, used for drugs with
  poor solubility (carbamazepine-saccharin).

## Limits

- **Heat transfer** — milling generates heat that needs to
  dissipate; air-cooling jackets standard.
- **Wear contamination** — Fe / Cr / Ni from stainless-
  steel jars + balls leach into product. ZrO₂ or PTFE jars
  for clean / metal-sensitive reactions.
- **Hard to monitor in situ** — IR / Raman + acoustic
  measurements becoming standard.
- **Dust hazards** — fine powders + inert atmospheres needed
  for moisture-sensitive reagents.
