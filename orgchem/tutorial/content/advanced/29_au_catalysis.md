# Au catalysis — π-acid chemistry

Gold seemed too noble for catalysis until ~ 2000. Then
Hashmi, Toste, Echavarren + others showed Au(I) + Au(III)
are extremely **π-acidic** + selectively activate alkenes,
alkynes, allenes for nucleophile addition.

## Au(I) — the soft π-acid

Au(I) (linear, 2-coordinate; e.g. Ph₃P-Au-Cl + AgX → Ph₃P-
Au+) coordinates to π-systems strongly. A bound alkyne
becomes electrophilic + susceptible to nucleophile attack:

```
RC≡CR' + [Au]⁺  →  [Au-RC=CR']⁺  +  Nu-H  →  vinyl-Nu  +  Au reformed
```

The Au is regenerated; turn-over depends on quenching of
the vinyl-Au intermediate by H⁺ or another step.

## Substrate scope

Au(I) catalysis works best with:

- **Alkynes** (Markovnikov-Nu addition; hydration to
  ketone, hydroamination to imine, hydroalkoxylation to
  ketone).
- **Allenes** (cyclisation to ring; Larock indole synthesis
  variants).
- **Alkenes** (less reactive than alkynes; sometimes works
  with bulky Au + strongly nucleophilic partners).

Pi-acidity ranking: Au(I) > Pt(II) > Pd(II) >> Au(III).
Most reactions use Au(I) cation generated by Au-X / Ag-X.

## Famous Au-catalysed reactions

### Au-hydration of alkynes

```
RC≡CH + H₂O + AuCl + AgOTf → R-CO-CH₃ (methyl ketone)
```

Markovnikov-selective. Industrial alternative to Hg-
catalysed Markovnikov hydration (which has Hg toxicity
issues).

### Au-Conia ene

```
β-keto alkyne (intramolecular) + Au cat. → cyclised pentenone
```

Toste 2004 — first asymmetric Au catalysis with chiral
phosphine.

### Au-hydroamination

```
RC≡CH + R'NH₂ + Au cat. → R-C(NHR')=CH-R'' → enamine →
                          ketimine → ketone amine
```

Mild, atom-economical N-H functionalisation.

### Au-cycloisomerisation

A 1,5- or 1,6-enyne cyclises with Au catalyst → bicyclic
diene + cyclopropane mixtures, depending on Au coordination
+ substrate.

### Au-mediated furan synthesis

```
allenyl ketone + Au cat. → furan (5-exo-dig)
```

Hashmi's discovery (2000) — first big Au catalysis paper.

## Asymmetric Au chemistry

Au(I) is linear (2-coordinate), so chirality must come from
the ligand backbone. Chiral phosphines (BINAP, JosiPhos,
DTBM-SegPhos) + chiral N-heterocyclic carbenes give
enantioselective hydroamination / cycloaddition.

Toste's chiral counter-ion strategy: chiral phosphate
counterion (TRIP-O⁻) gives enantioselective Au-catalysed
hydroamination of allenes → enantioselective
heterocycle synthesis.

## Mechanism intricacies

- **Cation generation** — Au-X + AgX' → Au⁺ + AgX↓ (silver
  salts AgOTf, AgSbF₆, AgBARF activate).
- **π-coordination** — Au sits over the π-bond.
- **Nucleophile attack** — anti-Markovnikov for Au(I) on
  some alkenes; Markovnikov on alkynes (electronic).
- **Protodemetalation** — H⁺ quenches the vinyl-Au.

The Au is regenerated so loadings are ~ 0.1-5 mol %.

## Au vs Pt

Pt(II) is a sister π-acid (chemically similar but slightly
different). Often Pt + Au give complementary products on the
same substrate — Pt for some cycloisomerisations Au can't
do.

## Why Au never went big in industry

Despite the academic excitement (~ 5000 papers since 2000):

- **Cost** — Au is ~ $50/g; not a problem at 0.5 mol % but
  scales matter.
- **Substrate scope** — very narrow vs Pd; mostly π-rich.
- **Removal** — trace Au in API is a regulatory concern.

So Au remains a research workhorse + a route discovery
tool, less a process workhorse.
