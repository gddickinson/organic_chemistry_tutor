# Carbon capture chemistry

To meet 2050 net-zero targets, the world will need to
remove or recycle ~ 10 Gt CO₂/yr by mid-century. **Carbon
capture, utilisation, + storage (CCUS)** is the chemistry
that makes this possible.

## Three categories

### 1. Carbon capture from point sources

Industrial flue gas (4-15 % CO₂):

- **Power plants** (gas: 4-7 % CO₂; coal: 12-15 %).
- **Cement** kilns (~ 25 % CO₂).
- **Steel** blast furnaces (~ 20 %).
- **Refineries, ammonia plants, ethanol plants**.

### 2. Direct air capture (DAC)

Atmospheric air (~ 420 ppm = 0.042 % CO₂). Much harder
because dilute, but unbounded scale.

### 3. Bioenergy + CCS (BECCS)

Burn biomass (carbon-neutral fuel) + capture CO₂ → net
**negative** emission.

## Capture chemistries

### Amine scrubbing (mature)

```
2 R₂NH + CO₂ ⇌ R₂NCO₂⁻ R₂NH₂⁺ (carbamate + ammonium)
                         ↓ heat
                       2 R₂NH + CO₂↑
```

Aqueous monoethanolamine (MEA, 30 wt%) is the workhorse.
Energy penalty: ~ 3-4 GJ / t CO₂ for regeneration. ~ $50-
100 / t CO₂ captured.

Modern: piperazine-promoted amines, activated MDEA, mixed
amines lower regeneration energy.

### Membrane separation

CO₂-selective polymeric membranes:

- Polyimides + polysulfones — mature.
- ZIF-8, mixed-matrix membranes — emerging.
- 5× cheaper than amine but lower capture rate.

### Pressure / temperature swing adsorption (PSA, TSA)

Solid sorbent (zeolites, MOFs, amine-on-silica):

- Pressurise gas → CO₂ adsorbed; depressurise → CO₂
  released.
- Or temperature cycle.
- Used for separating O₂, H₂, methane; CO₂ scaling
  improving.

### MOFs + amine-functionalised sorbents

- **Mg₂(dobpdc)** + appended amines — 4 mmol CO₂/g
  capacity; cycle stability > 1000 cycles.
- **CALF-20** (Calgary) — used by Svante for industrial
  capture.
- **mmen-Mg₂(dobpdc)** — first reversible amine-on-MOF
  with gas-phase amine cooperativity.

### Cryogenic separation

Cool flue gas → CO₂ liquefies / freezes (-78 °C). High
energy cost; only at small scale or for high-purity
applications.

## DAC technologies

### Climeworks (CO₂-amine on filter)

```
air pulled through amine-functionalised sorbent fabric
(low T) → CO₂ binds → heat to 100 °C → CO₂ released
```

Iceland Mammoth plant: 36 kt CO₂/yr (2024). Cost ~ $600 /
t today; target $200 / t by 2030.

### Carbon Engineering (KOH-Ca cycle)

```
KOH(aq) + CO₂ → K₂CO₃; Ca(OH)₂ + K₂CO₃ → CaCO₃; CaCO₃ heat → CaO + CO₂
```

Strong base + multi-step regeneration. Pilot plant in
Squamish, BC. Cost target: $100-150 / t CO₂.

### Heirloom (mineralisation)

Spread Ca(OH)₂ + Mg(OH)₂ on trays in air → CO₂
mineralised to CaCO₃, MgCO₃ → grind + heat → CO₂
released; CaO + MgO recycled.

### Solid sorbent + photoredox

Lab-scale: Cu-MOF + visible light directly reduces
captured CO₂ → CO + formate. Skip the regeneration step.

## Storage

After capture, where does the CO₂ go?

### Geologic sequestration

- Inject into depleted oil + gas reservoirs.
- Saline aquifers.
- Norway's Sleipner field — > 25 yr operation, > 25
  Mt CO₂ stored.

### Mineralisation

- React CO₂ with Mg / Ca silicate rocks → MgCO₃ / CaCO₃
  permanently bound.
- Iceland CarbFix project — inject CO₂ into basalt; ~ 95
  % mineralised within 2 years.

### Ocean

Inject deep ocean (> 1000 m). Controversial; affects ocean
chemistry; mostly research.

## Utilisation (CCU)

Convert CO₂ to useful products:

| Product | Pathway |
|---------|---------|
| **Methanol** | CO₂ + H₂ → CH₃OH (Cu/ZnO/Al₂O₃ cat.) |
| **Methane** | Sabatier; CO₂ + 4 H₂ → CH₄ + 2 H₂O |
| **Formic acid** | electroreduction CO₂ + 2 e⁻ + 2 H⁺ → HCOOH |
| **Ethylene** | electroreduction over Cu nanoparticles |
| **Carbonates / urea** | CO₂ + amine / phenol → carbamate / carbonate |
| **Polycarbonate plastics** | CO₂ + epoxide → poly(carbonate) |
| **Synthetic fuels** | CO₂ + H₂O + electricity → e-fuels |

Companies: **Lanzajet, Lanzatech, Twelve, Carbon Engineering,
Sunfire, Climeworks, Mosaic Materials**.

## Challenges

- **Cost** — DAC currently ~ $600 / t; needs to reach
  ~ $100 / t to be competitive at gigaton scale.
- **Energy** — capture + compression cost 10-30 % of
  emitted-source energy.
- **Sorbent cycle stability** — many sorbents degrade
  after 1000-10000 cycles.
- **Storage permanence + monitoring** — need to verify
  CO₂ stays underground for 1000+ years.
