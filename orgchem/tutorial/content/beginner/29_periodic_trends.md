# Periodic trends in organic chemistry

The periodic table isn't just a chart for inorganic-chem
exams — its trends drive every reactivity prediction in
organic chemistry. Three trends do most of the work.

## Atomic radius

Down a group, atoms get bigger (more shells). Across a period,
they shrink (more nuclear charge pulls the same shell tighter).

Why it matters:
- Iodine (large) is **softer + more polarisable** than
  fluorine — better leaving group, better SN2 nucleophile.
- Sulfur (large) makes a great soft nucleophile (thiols,
  thiolates) where oxygen wouldn't reach.

## Electronegativity

Up + right is more electronegative. Linus Pauling's scale:
F (4.0) > O (3.4) > N (3.0) > Cl (3.2) > C (2.6) > H (2.2).

Why it matters:
- The C–F bond (Δχ ~ 1.4) is much more polar than the C–H
  bond (Δχ ~ 0.4). Halogenated solvents (CHCl₃, DCM) have
  measurable dipoles.
- The acidic α-H of a ketone (pKa ~ 20) is acidic because
  the resulting enolate puts the negative charge partially
  on the very-EN oxygen.

## Bond strength + length

Down a group, bonds get longer + weaker. Across a period,
similar — shorter + stronger.

Bond | Length (Å) | BDE (kcal/mol)
---|---|---
C–F | 1.4 | 116
C–Cl | 1.8 | 81
C–Br | 1.9 | 68
C–I | 2.1 | 57

Iodides are the best leaving groups in SN2 partly because
the C–I bond is the weakest to break.
