# Polar bonds and electronegativity

A bond is **polar** when the two atoms sharing it pull on the
electrons unequally. The pulling-strength of an atom is its
**electronegativity (χ)** — a single number that summarises how
much an atom wants electrons.

## The Pauling scale at a glance

Linus Pauling's 0-4 electronegativity scale (1932) is the
universal reference:

| Atom | χ (Pauling) | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|
| F  | 4.0 | The most electronegative; pulls hardest |
| O  | 3.4 | The chemistry of carbonyls + alcohols |
| N  | 3.0 | Amines, amides |
| Cl | 3.2 | Polar bonds in alkyl halides |
| C  | 2.6 | The reference |
| H  | 2.2 | Slightly less EN than C |
| Si | 1.9 | Silyl groups push electrons toward C |
| Na | 0.9 | Strongly electropositive |

A bond's **dipole** points from the less-EN to the more-EN
atom, with magnitude ~ proportional to Δχ.

## Three bond types by Δχ

- **Δχ < 0.5** → **non-polar covalent**.  C–C, C–H, C–S all
  fall here.  The classic "non-polar" alkane chains.
- **0.5 ≤ Δχ < 2.0** → **polar covalent**.  C–O (Δχ = 0.8),
  C–N (Δχ = 0.4 — borderline polar), C=O (Δχ = 0.8 + extra π
  contribution), C–F (Δχ = 1.4 — very polar).
- **Δχ ≥ 2.0** → **ionic**.  Na–Cl (Δχ = 2.3) is the textbook
  case.  Most organic chemistry doesn't have ionic bonds in
  the traditional sense, but salts (carboxylates, ammoniums)
  involve ionic interactions between organic ions + small
  inorganic counterions.

## Electronegativity drives reactivity

- **Carbonyl chemistry** — C=O carbon is δ⁺ (electrophilic)
  because O pulls density away; nucleophiles attack the
  carbon.  Nearly every named reaction in the *Reactions* tab
  with a C=O substrate (aldol, Claisen, Wittig, Grignard
  addition, Cannizzaro) hinges on this polarity.
- **Alkyl halides** — C–X is polarised δ⁺-C ... δ⁻-X.  SN1 +
  SN2 reactions exploit it: nucleophile attacks the δ⁺ C, X⁻
  leaves.
- **Carboxylic acid α-protons** — α to a carbonyl, doubly
  polarised + acidic (pKa ~ 25-26 for ketones, ~ 4.8 for
  carboxylic-acid α-H thanks to the second polar bond + the
  resonance stabilisation of the conjugate base).

## Inductive effect

Electronegative atoms transmit their pull through σ bonds — an
**inductive effect** that drops off with distance (~ 1 / r³).
Trifluoroacetic acid (CF₃COOH) is ~ 4 pKa units more acidic
than acetic acid (CH₃COOH) because the three F atoms
inductively withdraw electron density from the carboxylate,
stabilising the conjugate base.
