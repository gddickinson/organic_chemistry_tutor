# Vapour pressure + boiling point

A liquid's **vapour pressure** is the pressure exerted by its
vapour at equilibrium with the liquid. Boiling occurs when
vapour pressure equals atmospheric pressure (1 atm at sea
level).

## Trends

Vapour pressure depends on:

- **Temperature** — exponentially via Clausius-Clapeyron:
  ```
  ln(P₂/P₁) = -ΔH_vap/R × (1/T₂ - 1/T₁)
  ```
  Doubles every ~ 20 °C for typical organic liquids.
- **Intermolecular forces** — strong forces (H-bonds, dipole-
  dipole) → low vapour pressure → high boiling point.
- **Molecular weight** — heavier molecules tend toward
  higher bp at given IMF strength (more London dispersion).

## Reduced pressure (vacuum) distillation

If a compound's bp at 1 atm is too high (decomposition
above 200 °C), reduce pressure → bp drops:

| Pressure | Effective bp drop |
|----------|-------------------|
| 100 mmHg | -30 °C |
| 10 mmHg | -50 °C |
| 1 mmHg | -70 °C |
| 0.1 mmHg | -90 °C |

Useful for natural products (sesquiterpenes, alkaloids),
prostaglandins, late-stage advanced intermediates.

## Boiling point and structure

| Pair | Higher bp | Why |
|------|-----------|-----|
| pentane vs n-pentanol | n-pentanol | OH H-bonds |
| ethanol vs propane | ethanol | OH H-bonds |
| DMF vs n-pentane | DMF | dipole-dipole |
| n-octane vs 2,2,4-tri-Me-pentane | n-octane | linear, more contact |

## Application: rotovap

The rotary evaporator removes a solvent at reduced pressure
+ moderate temperature. For DCM: water bath at 30 °C +
reduced pressure (300 mmHg) is fast. For DMF: 60 °C bath +
50 mmHg → still slow; better to dilute + extract first.

Rotovap rule of thumb: bp of solvent at vacuum should be
~ 20 °C below water-bath temperature.
