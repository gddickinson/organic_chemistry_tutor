# Alkanes — the simplest hydrocarbons

**Alkanes** are saturated hydrocarbons — only C and H, only
single bonds. Formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ for the open-chain (acyclic)
ones. They sit at the bottom of the organic-chemistry ladder
because they're **chemically boring** — and that's the point.

## The first 10 alkanes

| n | Name | Formula | bp (°C) | Notes |
|---|------|---------|---------|-------|
| 1 | Methane | CH₄ | −162 | Natural gas |
| 2 | Ethane | C₂H₆ | −89 | Natural gas component |
| 3 | Propane | C₃H₈ | −42 | LPG; barbecues |
| 4 | Butane | C₄H₁₀ | 0 | Lighter fluid |
| 5 | Pentane | C₅H₁₂ | 36 | Solvent |
| 6 | Hexane | C₆H₁₄ | 69 | Workhorse non-polar solvent |
| 7 | Heptane | C₇H₁₆ | 98 | Octane-rating reference fuel |
| 8 | Octane | C₈H₁₈ | 126 | Petroleum |
| 9 | Nonane | C₉H₂₀ | 151 | |
| 10 | Decane | C₁₀H₂₂ | 174 | |

The boiling-point trend (~ +20-30 °C per CH₂) is pure
**dispersion-force** scaling — alkanes have no polar bonds, no
H-bond donors. Bigger surface area = stronger dispersion =
higher bp.

## Acyclic vs cyclic vs branched

The same molecular formula maps to several **constitutional
isomers**:

- **n-Butane** (CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃) and **isobutane** (CH₃)₃CH share
  C₄H₁₀.
- C₅H₁₂ has 3 isomers: n-pentane, isopentane, neopentane.
- C₈H₁₈ has 18 isomers.
- The number of isomers explodes combinatorially with n.

**Cycloalkanes** (CₙH₂ₙ — one ring lowers H count by 2)
introduce ring strain considerations. Cyclohexane (the most
stable size, no strain) sits at one end; cyclopropane (huge
ring strain, ~ 27 kcal/mol) at the other. Lesson 19 on
cycloalkanes covers ring strain in detail.

## What chemistry alkanes do

- **Combustion** — C + H + lots of O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + heat.
  The energy basis of fossil fuels.
- **Radical halogenation** — Cl₂ or Br₂ + UV light substitute
  H for halogen. The seeded *Radical halogenation of methane*
  entry in the **Reactions** tab walks the chain mechanism
  (initiation → propagation → termination).
- **Catalytic cracking** — long alkanes break into shorter
  alkanes + alkenes at high temperature on zeolite catalysts;
  the basis of petroleum refining.
- **Almost everything else needs activation**. You can't easily
  do an SN2 on an alkane (no leaving group), can't do
  electrophilic addition (no π bond), can't deprotonate
  (pKa ~ 50). Alkanes are inert.

## Why the inertness matters

The C–H bonds in alkanes are very strong (~ 100 kcal/mol) +
non-polar. That's why we use hexane as a **solvent for
non-polar organic chemistry** — it dissolves your reactants
without participating in the reaction.

It also drives the **C–H functionalisation** branch of modern
organic chemistry: developing catalysts that can break unactivated
alkane C–H bonds is one of the field's biggest active areas
(Sames, Sanford, Hartwig — see the seeded *Heck reaction* +
related cross-coupling entries).
