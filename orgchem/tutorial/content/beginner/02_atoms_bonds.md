# Atoms, Bonds, and Hybridisation

Organic chemistry sits on three ideas that every later lesson pulls from:
atoms want a **stable electron count**, they reach it by **sharing
electrons** in covalent bonds, and the geometry of those bonds is set by
**hybridisation** of orbitals.

## The organic subset

Most of organic chemistry happens with a handful of elements:

| Element | Typical valence | Why it matters |
|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| **C**   | 4  | Can form long chains, rings, and every geometry — the skeleton of all organic molecules. |
| **H**   | 1  | Caps the ends. Not drawn in skeletal structures. |
| **O**   | 2  | Alcohols, ethers, carbonyls — the electron-rich electronegative partner. |
| **N**   | 3  | Amines, amides, aromatic heterocycles — can also be positively charged (ammonium). |
| **Hal (F, Cl, Br, I)** | 1 | Electronegative; classic leaving groups. |
| **S, P** | 2 / 3 (variable) | Sulfur in thiols / sulfides; phosphorus in phosphates. |

Open **Methane (CH₄)** in the molecule browser on the left — it's the
simplest possible organic molecule. Rotate the 3D view; note that the
four C–H bonds point to the corners of a tetrahedron.

## Covalent bonds

Two atoms form a bond by sharing a pair of electrons. The number of
pairs shared gives the bond's **order**:

- **Single bond** (one pair) — e.g. the C–C bond in ethane, free rotation.
- **Double bond** (two pairs) — e.g. the C=C bond in ethene. One σ + one π. No rotation.
- **Triple bond** (three pairs) — e.g. the C≡C bond in ethyne. One σ + two π.

In our 2D viewer, bond order is shown by the number of parallel lines; in
the 3D viewer, use the stick style and look for the parallel secondary
lines alongside double bonds.

## Hybridisation — why geometry is what it is

| Hybridisation | Bond partners | Geometry | Angle |
|---------------|---------------|----------|-------|
| **sp³** | 4 single bonds | Tetrahedral | 109.5° |
| **sp²** | One double + two single | Trigonal planar | 120° |
| **sp**  | One triple + one single, or two doubles | Linear | 180° |

Every carbon you'll meet is one of these three. The hybridisation you
see in a molecule explains:

- its shape (tetrahedral? flat? linear?),
- its reactivity (sp³ C's are saturated; sp² C's in an alkene are
  nucleophilic π systems; sp C's in an alkyne are more acidic),
- and for π-systems, whether they can overlap into an aromatic ring.

## Try it

1. Open **Caffeine** in the browser — every ring atom in the purine system
   is sp² and the rings are planar. Switch to **Ball-and-stick** in the 3D
   view and convince yourself.
2. Open **Cholesterol** — the fused four-ring steroid backbone is almost
   entirely sp³ (saturated), with one sp² C=C in ring B. Rotate it.
3. Open **Ethanol (CCO)** — two sp³ carbons plus an sp³ oxygen with two
   lone pairs.
