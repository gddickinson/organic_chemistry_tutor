# Recrystallisation for beginners

**Recrystallisation** is the most common purification method
for solid organic compounds. It exploits a simple solubility
trick: dissolve the impure crude in just enough HOT solvent,
then cool slowly so pure crystals grow + impurities stay
dissolved in the cold mother liquor.

## The single principle

Most solids are **more soluble hot than cold** (positive
temperature coefficient of solubility). When you cool a
saturated hot solution:
- The product, present at high concentration, **drops out**
  as crystals.
- Trace impurities, present at low concentration, **stay in
  solution** because they're nowhere near saturation.

## Picking the solvent

You want a solvent that:

- **Dissolves the product well at the boiling point**.
- **Doesn't dissolve the product at room temperature** (or
  at ice-bath temperature).
- **Dissolves the impurities at all temperatures** (so they
  stay in the mother liquor).
- Boils below the product's melting point (so you can
  evaporate without melting the product).
- Is volatile enough to remove easily from the crystals
  during drying.
- Doesn't react with the product.

Common recrystallisation solvents:
- **Ethanol** — good general workhorse for medium-polarity
  organics.
- **Methanol** — for polar / H-bonding solids.
- **Water** — for very polar solids (sugars, salts).
- **EtOAc** — for medium-polarity compounds.
- **Hexane** — for non-polar compounds.
- **EtOAc / hexane mixtures** — tunable polarity, very
  common.
- **Ethanol / water mixtures** — tunable + most-common
  natural-product solvent.

## The procedure

1. **Charge** the impure solid into an Erlenmeyer flask + a
   stir bar.
2. **Add solvent in small portions** while warming on a hot
   plate. Add only enough to dissolve the solid completely
   while hot — usually 5-20 mL/g of crude.
3. **Hot-filter** if the solution has insoluble impurities
   (dust, charcoal-decolorised residues). Pour through a hot
   fluted filter to keep the product in solution.
4. **Cool slowly** to room temperature uninterrupted. Don't
   blast it with ice — slow cooling = bigger, purer crystals;
   fast cooling = small crystals that occlude impurities.
5. **Cool further** in an ice-water bath to maximise yield
   (cold solubility is even lower).
6. **Vacuum-filter** the crystals on a Büchner funnel; rinse
   with a few mL of **COLD** solvent (so the crystals don't
   redissolve).
7. **Air-dry** under vacuum a few minutes.

## What can go wrong

- **No crystals form** — too dilute. Boil off some solvent +
  cool again. Or seed with a previous crystal.
- **Oil out instead of crystals** — the product melted
  before crystallising. Reheat + add more solvent + cool
  more slowly. Or switch to a higher-boiling solvent.
- **Crystals are coloured / impure** — the impurity may
  co-crystallise. Try a different solvent or repeat the
  recrystallisation.
- **Yield too low** — solvent is dissolving too much product
  cold. Switch to a less-polar solvent (or solvent mixture).

## Yield calculation

Recovery = (mass of dry crystals) / (mass of crude) × 100 %.

Typical good recrystallisation gives 70-90 % recovery. The
remaining 10-30 % goes into the **mother liquor** (the cold
filtrate); concentrate it + recrystallise again to get a
**second crop** of slightly less-pure crystals.
