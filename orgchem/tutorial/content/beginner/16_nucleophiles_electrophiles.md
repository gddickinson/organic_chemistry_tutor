# Nucleophiles vs electrophiles

Every polar organic reaction is a meeting between a
**nucleophile** (electron-rich, "loves the nucleus") and an
**electrophile** (electron-poor, "loves electrons"). Recognising
which is which makes mechanism prediction tractable.

## Quick definitions

- **Nucleophile** — has electrons to donate. Either a lone
  pair or a π bond. Often anionic or neutral with a
  heteroatom lone pair.
- **Electrophile** — wants electrons. Either an empty orbital
  (cation) or a partially-empty orbital (δ⁺ C of a polar
  bond, antibonding π* of an electron-poor C=C).

## Common nucleophiles

| Type | Example | Why nucleophilic |
|------|---------|------------------|
| Anionic Y | HO⁻, MeO⁻, NH₂⁻, CN⁻ | Lone pair on a charged atom |
| Lone-pair Y | NH₃, H₂O, EtOH, R₃P | Lone pair on neutral atom |
| Carbanion | RMgBr, RLi, enolate, ylide | C-bonded to electropositive metal |
| π bond | C=C, C=O, aromatics | π electrons donated |
| σ bond | R-H (SN2', radical), R-B (Suzuki) | σ electrons donated |

## Common electrophiles

| Type | Example | Why electrophilic |
|------|---------|-------------------|
| Cation | R⁺, H⁺, NO₂⁺ (nitration) | Empty orbital |
| δ⁺ carbon | C=O carbonyl, R-Cl, R-Br, R-OTs | Polar bond to electronegative atom |
| Empty p | Carbocation, BF₃, AlCl₃ | Less than octet |
| Polarised π | Michael acceptor (α,β-unsat. carbonyl) | EWG lowers π* energy |

## Which atom is which?

In aldehydes / ketones (e.g. acetone CH₃COCH₃):
- The **carbonyl C** is electrophilic (δ⁺ from C=O polarity).
- The **carbonyl O** is nucleophilic (two lone pairs).
- The **α-C** can be made nucleophilic by deprotonation
  (enolate formation), or stays slightly nucleophilic via
  hyperconjugation.

## HSAB — Hard / Soft Acid / Base

Pearson's **Hard / Soft Acid / Base** rule (1963) refines
nucleophile-electrophile pairing:

- **Hard** = small, charged, low-polarisability — Li⁺, H⁺,
  HO⁻, F⁻, Mg²⁺. Like to react with hard partners via fast,
  reversible, charge-driven bond formation.
- **Soft** = large, neutral or low-charge, high-polarisability
  — Cu(I), I⁻, RS⁻, alkenes. Like to react with soft partners
  via covalent-bond-formation.

Practical consequence:
- Hydroxide (HO⁻, hard) prefers acyl substitution at C=O over
  SN2 on a primary alkyl bromide.
- Iodide (I⁻, soft) does SN2 efficiently on primary alkyl
  halides.
- Cuprates (R₂CuLi, soft) do 1,4-conjugate addition to enones
  (soft electrophile site at β-C); Grignards (RMgBr, hard) do
  1,2-addition to the carbonyl C.

## Predicting reactivity

A 3-step mental algorithm:

1. **Find the electrophile** — look for δ⁺ carbons + empty
   orbitals.
2. **Find the nucleophile** — look for lone pairs + π bonds +
   anionic atoms.
3. **Draw the arrow** from nucleophile to electrophile + see
   what bond forms / breaks.
