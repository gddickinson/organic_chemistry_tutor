# Naming heterocycles

A **heterocycle** has at least one ring atom that's not
carbon. Most have well-established trivial names that
override IUPAC.

## 5-membered, 1 heteroatom

| Heteroatom | Aromatic | Saturated |
|------------|----------|-----------|
| O | furan | tetrahydrofuran (THF) |
| N | pyrrole | pyrrolidine |
| S | thiophene | thiolane (tetrahydrothiophene) |
| P | phosphole | phospholane |

## Substitution numbering

For pyridine-like N: number the ring with N as 1, going
toward the lowest substituent locant.

```
3-aminopyridine: N=1, the NH₂ at C3
2-bromothiophene: S=1, the Br at C2
```

For indole: N is position 1; the C adjacent to N in the
5-ring is position 2; the C in the 6-ring nearest the N
is position 7a (fusion atom).

## Why heterocycles dominate drugs

Of FDA-approved small-molecule drugs:

- ~ 70 % contain at least one nitrogen heterocycle.
- The 25 most-common scaffolds include: pyridine,
  piperidine, indole, imidazole, pyrazole, quinoline,
  thiazole, pyrimidine, morpholine, benzimidazole.

Why?

- N atoms bind protein H-bond donors / acceptors.
- Aromatic heterocycles tune pKa + lipophilicity finely.
- Bioisosteric replacement of a phenyl with a pyridine /
  pyrimidine often improves solubility + reduces CYP
  metabolism.
