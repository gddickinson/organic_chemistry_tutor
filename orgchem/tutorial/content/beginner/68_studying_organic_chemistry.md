# Studying organic chemistry effectively

Organic chemistry has a reputation as a "weeder" course.
That's only true if you study it like other sciences. With
the right tactics it's tractable + intellectually beautiful.

## Don't memorise — generalise

There ARE facts to learn (functional groups, common
reactions, NMR shifts). But memorising 200 reactions in
isolation is a recipe for failure.

Instead, focus on:

- **Reactivity patterns** — what kinds of bonds form vs
  break in nucleophilic substitution / electrophilic
  addition / pericyclic / radical mechanisms.
- **Functional-group reactivity** — how each FG behaves
  with each class of reagent.
- **Mechanism templates** — once you know SN1, SN2, E1,
  E2, EAS, addition, you can predict thousands of
  individual reactions.

## Draw mechanisms by hand

The **flow of electrons** is the language of organic
chemistry. Drawing curly arrows for every step:

- Forces you to track who's the nucleophile + electrophile.
- Reveals the rate-determining step.
- Predicts side products + selectivity.

If you can't draw the mechanism, you don't know the
reaction.

## Models + 3D

Organic molecules are 3D. Chair flips, axial vs equatorial,
endo vs exo, R vs S — all spatial. Use:

- **3D viewer in this app** — rotate to confirm
  stereochemistry intuition.
- **Physical model kits** — Molymod, Maruzen Kits.
- **Drawing your own** — sketch a chair, mark the wedges,
  then look at the 3D model + iterate.

## Practice problems > reading

Reading a chapter feels like learning. Doing 30 SN1 / SN2
predictions reveals what you actually understand vs what
you can recite.

Sources:

- **Textbook end-of-chapter problems** — typically rated
  by difficulty.
- **Klein, Bruice, Carey, Clayden, Smith** — major
  textbooks; the explanatory style varies.
- **Old exams + study guides** — many universities post
  PDFs.
- **Reaxys + SciFinder** practice — search a substrate +
  see what published chemistry it's been through.
- **OrgChem Studio** — every seeded reaction is a
  worked-through mechanism.

## Five tactical study methods

### 1. Start each session by drawing the previous
reaction class

Before tackling a new chapter, draw the SN1 mechanism
from memory in 30 seconds. Then SN2. Then E1, E2. If you
can't, your foundation is shaky — back up.

### 2. Make a personal "reagents I trust" sheet

Every time you encounter a new reagent in a problem set,
add a row: *what does it do, what survives, when do I use
it?* By end of semester you've built your own reference.

### 3. Spaced repetition for vocabulary + structures

For the 200 functional groups + 100 reagent abbreviations
+ 60 named reactions you'll encounter:

- Anki, Quizlet, RemNote — flashcards with structure
  images.
- Quick daily review of yesterday's lesson.

### 4. Teach a peer

The "rubber duck" method — explaining a mechanism aloud to
a classmate (or the wall) reveals exactly where your
understanding breaks.

### 5. Map relationships

When you learn a new reaction, ask:

- What's the analogous reaction with the next-period
  element (S instead of O, P instead of N)?
- What's the reverse?
- What's the photochemical version?
- What's the biocatalytic version?

This builds web of connections instead of isolated facts.

## Common pitfalls + fixes

- **"I'll memorise this exam, then move on"** — gaps grow
  cumulatively. Spend 30 min/week reviewing earlier
  material.
- **"I don't have time to draw mechanisms"** — drawing is
  10 % of the time, 90 % of the learning. Skip the writing
  if you must, but DRAW.
- **"I'll just look at Wikipedia for the structures"** —
  Wikipedia is great, but draw structures yourself; your
  hand-eye coordination is part of how you learn structures.
- **"I'll start memorising reactions a week before the
  test"** — too late. Three sessions of 30 min in week 1
  beat 4 hours the night before.

## Mistakes are the curriculum

Every wrong answer is a clue. Wrong predictions reveal
which mental model needs adjusting:

- Predicted SN2 but got SN1 → you ignored the substrate
  hindrance / leaving group / solvent effect.
- Predicted Markov but got anti-Markov → you missed
  peroxides / radical conditions.
- Predicted cis but got trans → you forgot the bromonium
  intermediate's anti-opening.

Keep a "gotchas" notebook. By exam time it's a personal
study guide.
