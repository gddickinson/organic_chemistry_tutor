# Resonance and electron delocalization

A single Lewis structure sometimes can't capture the truth.
**Resonance** is the convention chemists use when **two or more
Lewis structures with different electron arrangements** are
better thought of as **one molecule whose electrons are
delocalized** across all of them.

## The carboxylate anion

Acetate (CH₃COO⁻) — the conjugate base of acetic acid — can be
drawn two ways:

```
   O               O⁻
   ‖               |
H₃C–C–O⁻   ↔   H₃C–C=O
```

The two structures are **resonance forms**. The double-headed
arrow `↔` is **the resonance arrow** — NOT an equilibrium arrow
(use `⇌` for that). The molecule isn't flipping back and forth;
it's a **hybrid** of both forms simultaneously.

Reality: both C–O bonds in acetate have the **same length** +
**same partial-negative charge** (~ ½ on each O). Crystallography
confirms it.

## Three rules for drawing resonance forms

1. **Atoms can't move** — only π electrons + lone pairs move.
2. **The total number of electrons stays constant** — including
   formal charge totals.
3. **Better resonance forms** have:
   - More bonds (especially π bonds in conjugation),
   - Negative charge on the more-electronegative atom,
   - Positive charge on the less-electronegative atom,
   - All atoms with octets (no incomplete shells if avoidable).

## Hyperconjugation vs full resonance

**Hyperconjugation** is a weaker form: a σ bond (typically C–H
or C–C) donates electron density into an empty / partially-empty
adjacent p orbital. It explains why a **3° carbocation** (3
adjacent C–H σ donors) is much more stable than a **methyl
cation** (no donors).

The Phase-29 Saytzeff vs Hofmann elimination lesson and the
Phase-31 SN1 mechanism BOTH lean on hyperconjugation to explain
the thermodynamic / kinetic outcome.

## Where resonance shows up

- **Allyl + benzyl cations** — π electrons spread across
  multiple carbons → much more stable than the equivalent
  isolated cation.
- **Aromatic rings** — benzene's 6 π electrons are delocalized
  around the ring; that's what makes it aromatic.
- **Amide bonds** — the C=O ↔ C–O⁻ + N⁺ resonance gives the
  C–N bond ~ 40 % double-bond character + restricts rotation
  (this is why proteins fold the way they do).
- **Enolates** — C=C–O⁻ ↔ C⁻–C=O.  The α-carbon is nucleophilic
  in aldol + Claisen condensation.
