# Lab safety basics

The single most important habit in an organic-chemistry lab is
**knowing what you're handling before you touch it**. Most lab
accidents come not from exotic reagents but from simple
ignorance — the wrong glassware, the wrong PPE, the wrong
solvent for a fire.

## The minimum kit

Always wear, always:
- **Safety glasses or goggles** — every time you walk into the
  lab, even for a 30-second errand. Splash protection is
  non-negotiable.
- **Lab coat** — buttons up the front, sleeves down. Cotton
  flames out faster than synthetics; either is acceptable.
- **Closed-toe shoes** — nothing between your foot and a
  spilled solvent.
- **Long pants** — no shorts.
- **Hair tied back** if long.
- **Gloves** matched to the chemistry — nitrile for most
  organics, butyl for chlorinated solvents, neoprene for
  acids. Latex is NOT a chemical-resistant glove.

Optional / situational:
- **Face shield** — over goggles, when working with anything
  pyrophoric (BuLi, NaH, t-BuLi) or large quantities of
  acid / base.
- **Aprons** — over the lab coat, for concentrated acid /
  base work.

## The fume hood

Every reaction with a volatile or hazardous component runs in
a hood. Three hood rules:

1. **Sash low** — a hood with the sash all the way up doesn't
   contain anything. The 18-inch (~ 45 cm) marker on most
   modern hoods is the highest you should work.
2. **Don't store stuff in the working area** — a hood full of
   bottles can't pull air properly. Move bottles out
   when you're done with them.
3. **Don't lean in** — keep your face above the sash level.
   The air should be flowing INTO the hood, not out at you.

Many high-pKa solvents (DMSO, DMF) penetrate skin readily +
carry their solutes with them, and **hydrogen bonding**
between solvent + skin moisture is part of why some chemicals
are absorbed faster than others.

## The big four hazards

1. **Fire** — most organic solvents are flammable. Watch for
   open flames near ether, hexane, alcohols. Bunsen burners
   are mostly historical now in organic labs.
2. **Splash / corrosive** — concentrated H₂SO₄, HNO₃,
   bromine, NaOH. Always add **acid to water**, never water
   to acid (otherwise the local heating boils the small
   amount of water + spits acid back at you).
3. **Toxic exposure** — many organics are absorbed through
   skin. DMSO is the famous case: it's not very toxic itself,
   but it dissolves anything you touch into your skin
   instantly. Don't get DMSO + a soluble toxin on your hand.
4. **Pressure** — sealed systems with heat can explode. Always
   leave a vent (the condenser top stays open to atmosphere
   in a normal reflux). Vacuum work needs vacuum-rated
   glassware (no flat-bottom Erlenmeyers under vacuum).

## Spills

Small (< 50 mL) organic spill: spread vermiculite or sand on
it, scoop into a waste container, dispose as flammable solid
waste. Don't use paper towels — solvent-soaked towels are a
fire risk.

Acid / base spill: neutralise small ones with sodium
bicarbonate (acids) or vinegar / dilute citric acid (bases).
Anything significant — get out, alert your supervisor + the
safety officer.

## Disposal

- **Halogenated waste** (CHCl₃, DCM, CHBr₃) — separate bottle.
- **Non-halogenated organic waste** (EtOAc, hexane, MeOH) —
  separate bottle.
- **Aqueous heavy-metal waste** — separate bottle (Cr, Hg,
  As, Pb, Cd).
- **Solid waste with reagent residue** — flammable solids
  bin.
- **Sharps** — broken glass / needles in a rigid container.

Never tip organic solvent down the sink.

## The buddy system

Never run pyrophoric or extremely toxic chemistry alone in the
lab — t-BuLi, OsO₄, large peroxide solutions, HF. Always have
someone who knows where you are + can call for help.

## Try it in the app

- **Tools → Lab reagents…** — every reagent reference card has
  a *Hazards* section that names specific dangers + handling
  notes (DMSO skin penetration, EtBr mutagenicity, picric
  acid shock-sensitivity, etc.).
- **Tools → Lab equipment…** — the *Safety* category has
  fume-hood + safety-shower reference cards.
- **Glossary tab** → search for *Pyrophoric*, *PPE*,
  *Hood etiquette* (if seeded; otherwise these terms come
  from your local lab manual).
