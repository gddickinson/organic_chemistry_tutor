# Aromatic, antiaromatic, and non-aromatic

The aromaticity-intro lesson (20) introduced benzene's
special stability. This lesson sharpens the criteria + adds
the antiaromatic and non-aromatic categories — three
distinct outcomes for cyclic π systems.

## The three outcomes

A planar cyclic conjugated π system has 4n + something π
electrons, and the count determines stability.

| Class | π electrons | Stability vs reference |
|-------|-------------|-----------------------|
| **Aromatic** | 4n + 2 (n = 0, 1, 2, ...) | More stable |
| **Antiaromatic** | 4n (n = 1, 2, 3, ...) | Less stable |
| **Non-aromatic** | doesn't satisfy planarity / continuous overlap | normal |

## Hückel's criteria — the four conditions

For a molecule to be aromatic OR antiaromatic, ALL must hold:

1. **Cyclic** — π system forms a ring.
2. **Planar** — flat, so p orbitals overlap continuously
   around the ring.
3. **Continuously conjugated** — every atom in the ring
   contributes a p orbital to the π system.
4. **Closed-shell** π count — 4n + 2 for aromatic, 4n for
   antiaromatic.

If any of (1)-(3) fails, the molecule is non-aromatic.

## Aromatic examples

- **Benzene** (n = 1, 6 π e⁻) — the prototype.
- **Cyclopentadienyl anion (Cp⁻)** (n = 1, 6 π e⁻) — a
  planar pentagon with a delocalised negative charge.
- **Cycloheptatrienyl cation (tropylium, C₇H₇⁺)** (n = 1, 6
  π e⁻) — Sven-Karol Tropsch's discovery; remarkably
  stable + isolable as a salt.
- **Naphthalene** (10 π e⁻ over 2 fused rings).
- **Pyridine** (6 π e⁻; lone pair on N is in an sp² orbital
  in the plane, NOT in the π system).
- **Pyrrole, furan, thiophene** (6 π e⁻ each; one lone pair
  on N/O/S is donated into the π system).
- **Imidazole, pyrimidine, oxazole** — heteroaromatics.

## Antiaromatic examples

- **Cyclobutadiene** (4 π e⁻) — so unstable it dimerises +
  rectangulises (loses planarity) below 35 K.
- **Cyclopentadienyl cation** (4 π e⁻) — extremely unstable.
- **Cycloheptatrienyl anion** (8 π e⁻) — also unstable.
- **Cyclooctatetraene CATION (C₈H₈²⁺)** — 6 π e⁻ → aromatic;
  neutral cyclooctatetraene is non-aromatic (puckers into
  a tub shape).

## Non-aromatic — escapes by puckering

- **Cyclooctatetraene (COT, C₈H₈)** — would have 8 π e⁻ if
  planar (antiaromatic); instead puckers into a tub. Now
  non-aromatic, normal alkene reactivity.
- **[18]-Annulene** (planar 18-membered ring, 18 π e⁻ = 4·4
  + 2) — aromatic; has actual aromatic NMR shifts.

## Diagnostic tests

### 1. Spectroscopic — NMR ring current

An aromatic ring, in a magnetic field, sets up a **ring
current** that:

- **Deshields** outside-ring protons (benzene H at δ 7.3,
  while alkene H is at δ 5-6).
- **Shields** inside-ring protons (the inside H's of [18]-
  annulene are at δ −3, weirdly upfield).

Antiaromatic rings reverse this pattern (paratropic ring
current).

### 2. Energetic — heat of hydrogenation

Compare cyclohexene (-28 kcal/mol per π) → expect benzene
to release ~ 84 kcal/mol on full hydrogenation. **Actual:
~ 49 kcal/mol** — 35 kcal/mol stabilisation, the **aromatic
stabilisation energy**.

### 3. Frontier-orbital — Hückel MO theory

For a (4n+2) system, the Hückel MOs fill in pairs giving a
stable closed-shell ground state. For a 4n system, the
HOMO is degenerate + half-filled → triplet diradical
character + Jahn-Teller distortion.

## Why does it matter?

- **Predicts reactivity** — aromatic rings undergo
  electrophilic *substitution* (preserving aromaticity),
  not addition. Cyclohexene undergoes addition.
- **Drug design** — aromatic rings are common
  pharmacophores (~ 80 % of marketed small-molecule drugs
  contain ≥ 1 aromatic ring) but excessive flat aromatics
  → poor solubility ("flat-land" problem).
- **Materials** — aromatic conjugation enables conducting
  polymers (polyacetylene, polyaniline, PEDOT).
