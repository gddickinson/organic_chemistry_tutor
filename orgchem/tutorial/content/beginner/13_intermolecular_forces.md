# Intermolecular forces

Atoms inside a molecule are bonded.  Molecules in a liquid or
solid stick to each other through **intermolecular forces
(IMFs)** — much weaker than covalent bonds (typically 1-40
kJ/mol vs 200-400 kJ/mol for a single bond) but the **defining
factor** for boiling points, solubility, viscosity, melting
points, vapour pressure, and protein folding.

## The four main IMFs (weakest → strongest)

1. **London dispersion** (~ 1-10 kJ/mol).  Universal — every
   molecule has them.  Arise from instantaneous induced
   dipoles when an electron cloud's transient asymmetry
   polarises a neighbour.  Magnitude ~ molecular surface area.
   The reason long alkanes have higher boiling points than
   short ones (n-octane bp 126 °C vs methane bp −162 °C).
2. **Dipole-dipole** (~ 5-25 kJ/mol).  Between molecules with
   permanent dipoles (acetone, DMSO).  More aligned + more
   polarised → stronger.
3. **Hydrogen bonds** (~ 10-40 kJ/mol).  A special, especially
   strong dipole-dipole interaction when H is bound to N, O,
   or F + sees a lone pair on another N, O, or F.  Water,
   alcohols, amines, amides, nucleic-acid base pairing —
   biology's universal tape.
4. **Ion-ion** (~ 100-300 kJ/mol — almost a covalent bond).
   Between full charges.  Salts, carboxylates + ammoniums in
   solution.

## Why this drives bulk properties

- **Boiling point**: methane (−162 °C, only dispersion) vs
  water (100 °C, H-bonds) — same number of heavy atoms,
  > 250 °C apart.
- **Solubility**: "like dissolves like" is the IMF principle.
  Polar / H-bonding solutes dissolve in polar / H-bonding
  solvents (sugar in water).  Non-polar solutes need
  non-polar solvents (cholesterol in hexane).
- **Protein folding**: the hydrophobic effect (clustering of
  non-polar amino-acid side chains away from water) is what
  drives proteins to fold into compact globules.
- **Drug binding**: a drug fits into its target protein via
  ~ 5-15 H-bonds + π-stacking + hydrophobic contacts.
  Lipinski's rule-of-5 caps H-bond donor / acceptor count
  partly because excess polarity hurts membrane permeability.

## Hydrogen-bonding patterns to recognise

- **Water lattice** — each water has 2 H-bond donors + 2 H-bond
  acceptors → tetrahedral lattice in ice.
- **Carboxylic acid dimers** — two −COOH groups H-bond
  back-to-back via the −OH ↔ C=O contact, doubling the
  effective molecular weight in the gas phase.
- **DNA base pairing** — A:T = 2 H-bonds, G:C = 3 H-bonds
  (the extra H-bond is what makes G:C-rich sequences denature
  at higher temperature than A:T-rich).
- **Protein α-helix** — backbone amide N-H donates to
  backbone amide C=O four residues away.
- **β-sheet** — backbone amide N-H ↔ C=O across strands.
