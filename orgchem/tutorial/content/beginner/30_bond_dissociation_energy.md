# Bond dissociation energies (BDE)

The **bond dissociation energy (BDE)** is the energy required to
homolytically break a bond — splitting the two shared electrons
one to each fragment, giving two radicals. BDE is the
fundamental thermodynamic ruler for every reaction that
involves bond making or breaking.

## A reference table

Bond | BDE (kcal/mol)
---|---
H–H | 104
C–H (CH₄, primary) | 105
C–H (3°, isobutane) | 96
C–H (allylic) | 88
C–H (benzylic) | 90
C–C (ethane) | 89
C–C (in cyclopropane) | 65 (ring strain reduces it)
O–H (water) | 119
O–H (alcohol) | 105
N–H (ammonia) | 107
C=C (ethene) | 174 (σ + π)
C=O (acetone) | 179
C–F | 116
C–Cl | 81
C–Br | 68
C–I | 57

## Three lessons from the table

1. **3° C–H < 2° C–H < 1° C–H < methyl C–H** — explains why
   radical halogenation is more selective for 3° H's.
2. **Allylic + benzylic C–H bonds are weakest** — the radical
   formed is resonance-stabilised. This is the basis of
   allylic bromination (NBS) + benzylic oxidation.
3. **C–X bond strength decreases F > Cl > Br > I** — iodides
   are the best leaving groups; fluorides are nearly inert.

## Calculating reaction enthalpy

For any reaction:

```
ΔH ≈ Σ(BDE bonds broken) − Σ(BDE bonds formed)
```

Worked example — chlorination of methane:
```
CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl
broken: C–H (105) + Cl–Cl (58)  = 163 kcal/mol
formed: C–Cl (81) + H–Cl (103)  = 184 kcal/mol
ΔH = 163 − 184 = −21 kcal/mol  (exothermic — favorable)
```
