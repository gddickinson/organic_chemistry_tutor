# Alkenes and alkynes

Add a **π bond** to an alkane and you unlock the central
chemistry of synthesis. **Alkenes** (C=C, formula CₙH₂ₙ) and
**alkynes** (C≡C, formula CₙH₂ₙ₋₂) are the substrate class for
addition reactions, polymerisation, metathesis, and the dozens
of named reactions that built modern organic chemistry.

## Bonding: σ + π

A C=C consists of one **σ bond** (head-on overlap of sp² hybrid
orbitals — strong, ~ 80 kcal/mol) plus one **π bond** (side-by-
side overlap of unhybridised p orbitals — weaker, ~ 65 kcal/mol).
Together: ~ 145 kcal/mol, vs ~ 90 kcal/mol for a C–C single bond.

A C≡C adds a second π bond. The two π bonds are **perpendicular
to each other**, so the alkyne carbon is sp-hybridised with
linear (180°) geometry.

## Geometry constraints

- **Alkenes** are **planar at the C=C** with ~ 120° angles.
  Restricted rotation around the double bond → **cis/trans
  (E/Z) isomerism** for substituted alkenes.
- **Alkynes** are **linear** at the C≡C. No cis/trans isomerism.
  Terminal alkynes (R–C≡C–H) have an acidic terminal H (pKa
  ~ 25) — the only sp C–H acidic enough for routine
  deprotonation.

## Why π bonds are reactive

The π bond's electrons sit **above + below** the σ-bond axis,
relatively exposed. They:
- **Donate to electrophiles** (alkene + Br₂ → 1,2-dibromide
  via bromonium intermediate).
- **Accept from nucleophiles when activated** (Michael
  addition: nucleophile + α,β-unsat. carbonyl → 1,4-adduct).
- **Form metallacyclobutane intermediates** (olefin metathesis
  with Grubbs catalyst).
- **Undergo cycloadditions** (Diels-Alder needs an alkene
  dienophile).

- **CuAAC (Click chemistry)** (terminal alkyne + azide →
  1,4-triazole).

## Key terminology

- **Markovnikov's rule** — in HX addition to an unsymmetrical
  alkene, the H goes to the C with more H's (cation forms on
  the more-substituted C, since 3° > 2° > 1° cation stability).
- **Anti-Markovnikov** — the opposite regiochemistry. Free
  radical HBr (with peroxides) + hydroboration both give it.
- **Syn vs anti addition** — both groups on the same face vs
  opposite faces of the alkene.
- **Zaitsev product** — the more-substituted alkene from
  elimination (the thermodynamic product).
