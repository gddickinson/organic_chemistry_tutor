# Conformational analysis of butane — Newman projections

A **conformation** is one specific 3D shape a flexible molecule
can adopt by rotating around single bonds. **Newman projections**
are the standard way to draw + analyse conformations — viewing
the molecule down a single C–C bond.

## How to draw a Newman projection

Look down the C–C bond axis. The **front carbon** is drawn as
a dot (or implied centre); its three substituents are lines
radiating out at 120°. The **back carbon** is drawn as a
larger circle behind the front; its three substituents come
out of the circle's perimeter (also at 120°).

The **dihedral angle** (also called *torsion angle*) between
front and back substituents is what changes during rotation.

## Ethane (CH₃CH₃) — the simplest case

Two extreme conformations:

- **Eclipsed (0° dihedral)** — front + back H's overlap.
  ~ 3 kcal/mol higher energy due to electron-cloud repulsion
  between the eclipsed C–H bonds.
- **Staggered (60° dihedral)** — back H's bisect the angles
  between front H's. Energy minimum.

Rotation barrier in ethane: ~ 3 kcal/mol. At room temperature,
ethane spends most of its time in staggered conformations but
flips through eclipsed with timescales of picoseconds.

## Butane (CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃) — the rich case

Look down the central C2–C3 bond. The two methyl groups (one
on each carbon) can sit at 0°, 60°, 120°, 180° relative to
each other. Four named conformations:

- **0° = eclipsed (syn)** — Me + Me overlap. ~ 6 kcal/mol
  above minimum. Strongly disfavoured.
- **60° = gauche** — Me + Me at 60° angle. ~ 0.9 kcal/mol
  above minimum. Two energetically equivalent gauche forms
  (60° and 300°).
- **120° = eclipsed** — H + Me and Me + H overlap. ~ 3.5
  kcal/mol above minimum.
- **180° = anti** — Me + Me at maximum separation. **Energy
  minimum**.

The ~ 0.9 kcal/mol "gauche penalty" between two methyl groups
is called the **gauche interaction**. It scales with
substituent size — two t-butyls have an enormous gauche
penalty (~ 5 kcal/mol).

## Why anti is the most stable

Steric repulsion between substituents is minimised when they
are as far apart as possible. The anti (180°) conformation
puts the two largest groups exactly opposite each other.

## Why staggered always beats eclipsed

Three contributions:
1. **Steric** — eclipsed atoms compete for the same space.
2. **Torsional** — eclipsed C–H σ bonds repel via filled-
   filled orbital overlap.
3. **Hyperconjugation** — staggered conformations enable
   donation from one C–H σ bond into the antibonding C–H σ*
   on the adjacent carbon (small but real).

## Connection to cyclohexane chairs

Cyclohexane's preference for the **chair conformation** (lesson
19) is essentially the same physics writ on a ring: every C–C
bond in the chair is in a perfect staggered (60°-anti)
conformation around the ring.
