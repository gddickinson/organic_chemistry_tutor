# Acid-base titrations + indicators

A **titration** measures concentration by reacting a
solution of unknown concentration with a standardised
solution. For acid-base, the equivalence point is detected
by an **indicator** that changes colour at a specific pH.

## The titration curve

For strong acid + strong base (HCl + NaOH):

```
pH
 14 |                       _______
    |                     /
  9 |             jump
  7 |- - - - - - + - - - - eq. point
  5 |             jump
    |          /
  1 |________/
    +-------+----------+----+--→ Vbase added
```

- Initial pH from acid concentration.
- pH rises slowly as base added.
- Sharp jump (~ 6 pH units) at equivalence.
- Plateau at high pH after equivalence.

For weak acid + strong base (acetic + NaOH): half-equivalence
point pH = pKa (Henderson-Hasselbalch).

## Equivalence point vs end point

- **Equivalence point** — moles acid = moles base
  (theoretical).
- **End point** — when the indicator changes colour
  (experimental, ideally close to equivalence).

Choose the indicator so its pKa-of-colour-change is near
the equivalence pH.

## Common indicators

| Indicator | pH range | Acid colour | Base colour |
|-----------|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Methyl violet | 0-2 | yellow | violet |
| Methyl orange | 3.1-4.4 | red | yellow |
| Methyl red | 4.4-6.2 | red | yellow |
| Litmus | 5-8 | red | blue |
| Bromothymol blue | 6.0-7.6 | yellow | blue |
| Phenol red | 6.8-8.2 | yellow | red |
| Phenolphthalein | 8.3-10.0 | colourless | pink |
| Thymolphthalein | 9.3-10.5 | colourless | blue |

For strong acid + strong base: any indicator works
(equivalence at pH 7, jump from ~ 4 to ~ 10).

For weak acid + strong base (e.g. acetic): equivalence at
pH > 7 → use phenolphthalein.

For strong acid + weak base (e.g. HCl + NH₃): equivalence
at pH < 7 → use methyl orange or methyl red.

## pH meters vs indicators

- **Indicators** — cheap, visible, but ± 0.5 pH unit accuracy.
- **pH meters** — ± 0.01 pH; auto-titrators do entire curve.
- **Conductometric titration** — measures conductivity vs
  volume; flat segments + sharp inflection at equivalence.
  Good for very dilute or coloured samples.

## Standard solutions

For accurate analysis, the titrant must be **standardised**
against a primary standard:

- **Acid**: KHP (potassium hydrogen phthalate) for NaOH
  standardisation.
- **Base**: weighed Na₂CO₃ for HCl standardisation.

Stock NaOH absorbs CO₂ from air → drifts; standardise
weekly.

## Modern variants

- **Karl Fischer titration** — water content in organic
  solvents; uses I₂ + SO₂ + base in MeOH/pyridine.
- **Argentometric titration** — Ag⁺ for halide
  determination (Mohr, Volhard, Fajans methods).
- **Redox titration** — KMnO₄ self-indicating; iodometric
  (I₂/S₂O₃²⁻) for many oxidants.
