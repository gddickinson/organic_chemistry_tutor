# Hydrogen bonding — deeper

The intermolecular-forces lesson (13) introduced hydrogen
bonding as one of three IMFs. This lesson focuses on it
specifically — H-bonding shapes water, biology, drug binding,
and crystal packing.

## What counts as a hydrogen bond

A hydrogen bond is a directional electrostatic + partially-
covalent interaction:

```
D-H ··· A
```

- **D** (donor) — electronegative atom (N, O, F) covalently
  bonded to H.
- **A** (acceptor) — electronegative atom with a lone pair
  (N, O, F most common).

Strength: ~ 2-10 kcal/mol — much weaker than a covalent bond
(~ 80-100 kcal/mol) but much stronger than a typical van der
Waals interaction (< 1 kcal/mol).

## Geometry

Strong H-bonds are nearly linear: D-H···A angle near 180°,
H···A distance ~ 1.8-2.5 Å. Bent H-bonds (< 150°) are
weaker.

## H-bond strength order (in water)

```
F-H ··· F⁻    > O-H ··· O    > N-H ··· N    > N-H ··· O
~ 23 kcal/mol   ~ 5 kcal/mol   ~ 3 kcal/mol   ~ 2 kcal/mol
```

Ionic H-bonds (e.g. F-H···F⁻ in HF₂⁻) are anomalously strong;
neutral N-H···O is the workhorse of biology.

## Water — the H-bond extreme

A water molecule donates **2** H-bonds (two O-H bonds) and
accepts **2** (two lone pairs on O). On average it forms ~
3.5 H-bonds at any moment in liquid water.

Consequences:

- **Anomalously high boiling point** (100 °C vs ~ −60 °C
  predicted by mass alone).
- **Density anomaly** — ice is less dense than liquid water
  because the open H-bonded lattice has more empty space
  than the dynamic liquid.
- **High specific heat** — a lot of H-bonds to break per
  unit warming.
- **High surface tension** — strong cohesion.

## H-bonding in biology

- **Protein secondary structure** — α-helix + β-sheet held
  by N-H···O=C backbone H-bonds.
- **DNA double helix** — A-T pair has 2 H-bonds, G-C pair
  has 3 H-bonds. G-C-rich DNA melts at higher temperature.
- **Enzyme-substrate recognition** — many catalytic-residue
  contacts are H-bonds.
- **Solvation of polar substrates** — water + small
  molecules.

## Intramolecular H-bonds

A H-bond within the same molecule (a 5- or 6-membered ring
forms easily):

- **Salicylic acid** — intramolecular O-H···O=C. Boiling
  point + acidity + IR signature shifted relative to
  m-hydroxybenzoic acid.
- **Acetylacetone (penta-2,4-dione)** — its enol form has a
  6-membered intramolecular H-bond, stabilising the enol so
  much that the keto / enol equilibrium is ~ 85 % enol in
  CCl₄.
- **Drug pharmacology** — intramolecular H-bonds reduce
  effective polar surface area + improve membrane
  permeability ("hidden polar surface area" trick).

## Cooperativity

H-bonds reinforce each other when chained:

```
D-H ··· O-H ··· O-H ··· A
```

The middle O-H is a stronger donor than it would be alone
because polarisation builds up along the chain. Water + ice
+ α-helix benefit from cooperativity.

## Spectroscopic signatures

- **IR** — H-bonded O-H + N-H stretches shift to **lower**
  wavenumbers (weaker effective force constant) AND broaden
  enormously (~ 100-300 cm⁻¹ FWHM vs ~ 30 cm⁻¹ for free).
- **NMR** — H-bonded protons shift downfield (deshielded);
  exchangeable so the chemical shift is concentration-,
  solvent-, and temperature-dependent.
- **X-ray crystallography** — H-bonds visible at high
  resolution from the donor-acceptor distances + angles.

## Beyond conventional H-bonds

- **C-H···O / C-H···N** — weak (< 1 kcal/mol) but real;
  matters in dense crystal packing + protein interiors.
- **π H-bonds** — D-H···π (the π cloud accepting) — weak
  but detectable in benzene + indole rings.
- **Halogen bond** (related concept) — D···X-R where the
  C-X bond's σ-hole acts like an H-bond donor; strongest
  for I and Br.
