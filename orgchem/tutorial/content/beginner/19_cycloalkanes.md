# Cycloalkanes — ring strain + chair conformations

A cycloalkane is just an alkane with the chain ends joined into
a ring. The formula drops by 2 H (CₙH₂ₙ instead of CₙH₂ₙ₊₂) and
the chemistry gains a new dimension: **ring strain**.

## Three sources of strain

1. **Angle strain** — the ideal sp³ tetrahedral angle is 109.5°.
   Cyclopropane forces 60°; cyclobutane forces 90°. The
   energetic cost shows up as a higher heat of combustion per
   CH₂.
2. **Torsional (eclipsing) strain** — in flat rings the C–H
   bonds on adjacent carbons can't stagger. Each pair of
   eclipsing H's costs ~ 1 kcal/mol.
3. **Steric (transannular) strain** — in medium rings (8-12
   atoms), atoms on opposite sides of the ring bump into each
   other across the empty centre.

## Strain by ring size

| Ring | Strain (kcal/mol) | Why |
|------|-------------------|-----|
| 3-membered (cyclopropane) | 27 | huge angle + torsional strain |
| 4 (cyclobutane) | 26 | angle + torsional |
| 5 (cyclopentane) | 6 | minor torsional |
| 6 (cyclohexane) | **0** | chair conformation = zero strain |
| 7 | 6 | minor strain |
| 8-12 | 8-12 | transannular strain |
| > 14 | ≈ 0 | ring big enough to relax |

The zero-strain status of cyclohexane is why it's the most
common ring in nature — the steroid backbone (cholesterol,
testosterone, cortisol), most sugar pyranoses (glucose,
galactose), inositol — all built on cyclohexane chairs.

## The chair conformation

Cyclohexane adopts a **chair** shape that simultaneously:
- Keeps every C–C–C angle exactly 109.5° (no angle strain).
- Staggers every C–H bond pair (no torsional strain).

In the chair, each carbon has one **axial** H (pointing
straight up or straight down) and one **equatorial** H
(pointing roughly outward in the equatorial plane). Substituents
strongly prefer **equatorial** — axial substituents have
unfavourable **1,3-diaxial interactions** with the H's three
atoms away.

The energy cost of axial vs equatorial is the **A-value** —
~ 1.7 kcal/mol for methyl, ~ 5 kcal/mol for *t*-butyl. *t*-Butyl
substituents lock cyclohexane chairs because flipping the chair
would force the *t*-butyl axial at huge energy cost.

## Ring flip

A cyclohexane chair flips into its **mirror chair** by passing
through a higher-energy half-chair / twist-boat. The flip
exchanges all axial ↔ equatorial positions. At room temperature
the flip is fast (~ 10⁵ s⁻¹) — substituents that were axial
become equatorial in a microsecond.
