# Free-radical chemistry — going deeper

You met radical chains in the beginner radical-halogenation
lesson. This lesson digs into the regio- + stereoselectivity
patterns that make modern radical chemistry a strategic tool
in synthesis (rather than the "explosive disaster avoided
when possible" view of the 1990s).

## Radical stability ladder

Like carbocations:

```
3° > 2° > 1° > methyl  (alkyl)
allylic + benzylic > 3° (resonance)
captodative (EWG + EDG flanking) > allylic
acyl + α-carbonyl ~ 2° (special — see below)
```

Captodative effect: a radical flanked by both an electron
donor (e.g. NH₂) + an electron acceptor (e.g. CO₂R) is
stabilised by both. Classic in α-amino-acid radicals.

## Selectivity in radical halogenation

The classical Cl vs Br selectivity contrast:

- **Cl₂** + alkane: Cl• is **highly reactive** → not very
  selective. Statistical product distribution dominates.
  Example: 2-methylbutane + Cl₂ → mixture of all four mono-
  chloro isomers in ~ statistical ratios.
- **Br₂** + alkane: Br• is **less reactive + more selective**.
  Reactivity ratios 3° : 2° : 1° ≈ 1600 : 80 : 1. Selectively
  halogenates the most-substituted C–H.
- **NBS** (N-bromosuccinimide) + alkene: low [Br₂] generated
  in situ → selective allylic bromination, not addition to
  the C=C.

## Radical chain mechanism

Three phases:

1. **Initiation** — generate the first radical. UV light, a
   peroxide initiator (BPO, AIBN), or thermolysis at high
   temperature.
2. **Propagation** — the radical does work, generating
   another radical that continues the chain. The two-step
   pattern:
   ```
   X• + R-H → HX + R•
   R• + X-X → R-X + X•
   ```
3. **Termination** — two radicals combine, ending the chain.
   Gives byproducts (R-R, X-X, R-X minor).

Chain length (# of propagation cycles per initiation event)
can reach 10⁴-10⁶ for clean radical chains.

## Modern radical methods

The 2000s + 2010s revival of radical chemistry — driven by:

- **Photoredox catalysis** (lesson on photoredox in the
  graduate tier) — visible light + Ru/Ir or organic dyes
  generate radicals from carboxylates, amines, or even C-H
  bonds.
- **HAT (hydrogen-atom transfer)** catalysis — quinuclidinium
  + visible light removes specific C-H atoms from
  unactivated positions.
- **Hydrogen-borrowing** + **C-H functionalisation** —
  catalyst removes a CH bond, swaps in a CC bond, then
  returns the H elsewhere.

## Stereoselectivity in radical reactions

Historically: radicals are flat (sp²) → racemic products.
Modern asymmetric radical chemistry uses chiral catalysts
(Lewis acids, photoredox, hydrogen-bond catalysts) to bias
which face is attacked.

Recent example: visible-light photoredox + a chiral Brønsted
acid catalyst can give > 95% ee on radical α-amination.

## Radical polymerisation

- **Free-radical polymerisation** (initiator + monomer) —
  styrene, MMA, vinyl acetate. Gives polydisperse polymer.
- **Controlled radical polymerisation** (RAFT, ATRP, NMP) —
  reversible chain-end deactivation gives narrow molecular-
  weight distributions. Used industrially for high-end
  polymers (e.g. block copolymer drug-delivery vehicles).
