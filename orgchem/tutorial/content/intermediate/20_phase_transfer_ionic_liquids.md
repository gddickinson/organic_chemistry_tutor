# Phase-transfer catalysis + ionic liquids

Two solutions to a single recurring problem in organic
synthesis: **how do you get an ionic reactant to react with a
non-polar substrate?**

## The problem

A typical reaction wants:

- An **anionic nucleophile** (CN⁻, OH⁻, RS⁻, F⁻ from KF) —
  prefers water + polar protic solvents.
- A **non-polar substrate** (alkyl halide, aromatic C–X) —
  prefers DCM, hexane, toluene.

In a biphasic mixture (water layer + organic layer), the two
sit on opposite sides of the interface + barely meet. The
reaction crawls.

## Phase-transfer catalysis (PTC)

Add a **phase-transfer catalyst** — a quaternary ammonium
(R₄N⁺) or phosphonium (R₄P⁺) salt with a lipophilic counter-
cation. It works by swapping its native counter-ion (Cl⁻, Br⁻)
for the anionic nucleophile + carrying it INTO the organic
phase as a soluble ion pair:

```
(Bu₄N)⁺ Br⁻ + Na⁺ CN⁻  ⇌  (Bu₄N)⁺ CN⁻ + Na⁺ Br⁻
                              (organic-soluble)
                              ↓
                          attacks RX in organic phase
                              ↓
                          R-CN + (Bu₄N)⁺ X⁻  → cycle continues
```

Effective rate enhancements: 100-1000 ×. Reagents like
**tetrabutylammonium bromide (TBAB)**, **benzyltriethylammonium
chloride (BTEAC)**, **18-crown-6** (chelates K⁺) are the
workhorses.

### When to use PTC

- Cyanide displacements (R-X → R-CN).
- Ester saponifications under mild conditions.
- Williamson ether syntheses with hindered alkoxides.
- Asymmetric alkylation with chiral PTCs (Maruoka catalyst,
  cinchonidinium quaternary salts) — competes with enzyme
  + photoredox for asymmetric C–C bond formation.

## Ionic liquids

A salt with a **melting point below 100 °C** (often well below
room temperature) — counts as a "liquid" by definition. The
trick: pair a bulky organic cation (imidazolium, pyridinium,
ammonium) with a non-coordinating anion (BF₄⁻, PF₆⁻, NTf₂⁻,
OTf⁻).

Properties:

- Negligible vapour pressure — non-flammable, no inhalation
  hazard, easy to recover.
- Tunable polarity by varying cation + anion combinations
  (~ 10⁶ possible ILs).
- Dissolve a wide range of polar + non-polar solutes.
- High thermal stability (~ 300 °C decomposition).

Common ILs:

- **[BMIM][BF₄]** — 1-butyl-3-methylimidazolium tetrafluoroborate.
- **[BMIM][PF₆]** — water-immiscible variant.
- **[EMIM][NTf₂]** — 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium
  bis(triflimide). Most popular for electrochemistry.

Used as:

- Solvents for organic reactions.
- Catalysts (acidic ILs as Brønsted acid catalysts).
- Electrolytes in batteries + supercapacitors.
- Cellulose dissolvers (room-temperature dissolution of
  wood-derived polymers).

## Deep eutectic solvents (DES) — modern alternative

A simpler + cheaper relative of ILs: mix two solid
components (e.g. choline chloride + urea) that form a liquid
eutectic mixture at room temperature. Cheaper, more
biodegradable than full ionic liquids; the "**reline**"
solvent (1:2 choline chloride : urea) is the textbook
example.
