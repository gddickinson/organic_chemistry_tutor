# Heterocyclic chemistry intro

A **heterocycle** is a ring containing at least one non-carbon
atom (most often N, O, or S). Heterocycles are
**overwhelmingly dominant** in pharmaceuticals — > 85 % of
small-molecule drugs contain at least one heterocyclic ring.
Recognising the common ring systems + their reactivity patterns
is non-optional for medicinal chemistry.

## The 5-membered aromatic heterocycles

Each is aromatic by Hückel's rule (6 π electrons in the ring),
with the heteroatom contributing a lone pair to the π system:

Ring | Hetero | Example drugs
---|---|---
Furan | 1 O | nitrofurantoin, furosemide
Thiophene | 1 S | duloxetine, ticlopidine
Pyrrole | 1 N (NH) | sumatriptan (indole subtype), atorvastatin
Imidazole | 2 N | cimetidine, ketoconazole, losartan
Pyrazole | 2 N adjacent | celecoxib (Celebrex)
Oxazole | 1 N + 1 O | sulfamethoxazole
Thiazole | 1 N + 1 S | ritonavir, sulfathiazole

## The 6-membered aromatic heterocycles

Ring | Hetero | Example drugs
---|---|---
Pyridine | 1 N | nicotine, isoniazid, omeprazole
Pyrimidine | 2 N (1,3) | DNA bases (cytosine, uracil, thymine), trimethoprim
Pyrazine | 2 N (1,4) | pyrazinamide
Pyridazine | 2 N (1,2) | minaprine
Triazine | 3 N | melamine, lamotrigine

## Reactivity vs benzene

The heteroatom changes electron density:

- **π-Excessive** (5-membered with NH/O/S; pyrrole, furan,
  thiophene): the heteroatom lone pair pushes electron density
  into the ring, making the ring **more nucleophilic** than
  benzene. They undergo electrophilic aromatic substitution
  (EAS) much faster than benzene. EAS goes preferentially at
  the **2-position** (alpha to the heteroatom).
- **π-Deficient** (pyridine, pyrimidine): the heteroatom
  withdraws electron density (it has no lone pair in the
  ring; its lone pair sits in an sp² orbital in the plane),
  so the ring is **less nucleophilic** than benzene. EAS is
  slow + goes at **3-position**. Nucleophilic aromatic
  substitution (SNAr) at 2- or 4-position is the preferred
  manipulation.

## Fused heterocycles — the medicinal chemistry zoo

- **Indole** = benzene + pyrrole. Found in tryptophan,
  serotonin, sumatriptan.
- **Quinoline** = benzene + pyridine (sharing a C=C).
  Found in chloroquine (anti-malarial), atovaquone,
  dibucaine.
- **Isoquinoline** = pyridine fused at a different position.
  Found in papaverine, dopamine antagonists.
- **Purine** = pyrimidine + imidazole. Adenine, guanine —
  the two purine DNA bases. Caffeine, theophylline,
  theobromine, allopurinol.
- **Benzofuran**, **Benzothiophene**, **Benzimidazole**,
  **Benzoxazole**, **Benzothiazole** — analogous fusions
  with various pharmacological targets.

## Saturated heterocycles

Equally important + often more drug-like:

- **Pyrrolidine** (5-mem N) — found in nicotine, hygrine.
- **Piperidine** (6-mem N) — extremely common in pharma
  (Nepenthes alkaloids, fentanyl, donepezil). Often called
  the **"Mona Lisa"** of medicinal-chemistry rings — > 5%
  of all drugs contain it.
- **Piperazine** (6-mem 2N) — quetiapine, sildenafil
  (Viagra), trazodone.
- **Morpholine** (6-mem N + O) — moclobemide, gefitinib.
- **Tetrahydrofuran (THF)** (5-mem O) — solvent + drug
  scaffold (paclitaxel taxane core).

## Synthesis routes

Three big general methods:

- **Hantzsch synthesis** — pyridines + pyrroles via
  multi-component condensation of aldehyde + β-ketoester +
  amine. The seeded *Hantzsch dihydropyridine synthesis*
  entry walks one variant.
- **Paal-Knorr** — furans + pyrroles + thiophenes from 1,4-
  diketones + the appropriate "X" source.
- **Modern transition-metal cyclisations** — Larock indole
  synthesis (Pd-catalysed), Buchwald-Hartwig N-arylation,
  Cu-catalysed N-alkylations.
