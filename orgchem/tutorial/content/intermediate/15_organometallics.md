# Organometallic reagents — Grignards, organolithiums, cuprates

A C–metal bond polarises **opposite** to the typical C–H or
C–O bonds: the carbon becomes **δ⁻ + nucleophilic** because
metals are less electronegative than carbon. This is the trick
that gives synthetic chemists a "**carbanion equivalent**" — a
nucleophilic carbon that can attack any electrophile.

## The polarity reversal

Comparison:

```
H₃C–H        normal: C is δ−, H is δ+
H₃C–OH       normal: C is δ+, O is δ−
H₃C–MgBr     reversed: C is δ−, Mg is δ+   (a "umpolung")
```

The carbon is now **electron-rich**; it attacks the C of any
C=O like an enolate would.

## The three main organometallic classes

### Grignards (RMgBr / RMgI / RMgCl)

Discovered by Grignard (1900 Nobel, 1912). Made from R–X +
Mg(0) in dry THF or Et₂O. Strong nucleophile, moderate base.

Reactions:
- + aldehyde → 2° alcohol
- + ketone → 3° alcohol
- + ester → 3° alcohol (with two R groups added)
- + CO₂ → carboxylic acid
- + epoxide → β-substituted alcohol
- + nitrile → ketone (after hydrolysis)

The seeded **Grignard addition to acetone** entry walks the
mechanism.

### Organolithiums (n-BuLi, MeLi, t-BuLi, PhLi)

Stronger + more basic than Grignards. Used for:

- Deprotonation of acidic positions (pKa < 40).
- Halogen-lithium exchange (R–Br + n-BuLi → R–Li + n-BuBr).
- Directed ortho-metalation (DoM): an aromatic with a
  directing group (CONEt₂, OMOM) lithiates ortho to the DG.
- Same nucleophilic-addition repertoire as Grignards, often
  faster + with broader functional-group scope.

**t-BuLi is pyrophoric** — must be handled under inert
atmosphere with extreme care.

### Cuprates (R₂CuLi, Gilman reagents)

Made from R–Li + CuI. Less basic + softer than Grignards.
Distinct selectivity:

- + α,β-unsaturated carbonyl → **1,4-conjugate addition**
  (NOT 1,2 like Grignards).
- + alkyl halide → SN2 product (cross-coupling without Pd!).
- + acid chloride → ketone (one transfer, doesn't go to 3°
  alcohol like Grignards do).

The HSAB principle (lesson 16 of beginner) explains:
Grignards (hard nucleophile) prefer hard 1,2-addition;
cuprates (soft) prefer soft 1,4-addition.

## Functional-group tolerance

Grignards + organolithiums are **incompatible** with:

- Acidic protons (water, alcohols, NH, terminal alkynes,
  carboxylic acids) — they'll just deprotonate.
- Other electrophilic groups (esters, ketones — unless that's
  the substrate).

This drives **protecting-group** strategy: protect the
sensitive group (e.g. silyl ether instead of free alcohol),
do the Grignard, deprotect at the end.

## Modern alternatives — Knochel's iPrMgCl·LiCl

The classical "Grignard goes only on activated halides" rule
breaks with **turbo-Grignard** (iPrMgCl·LiCl, "Knochel's
reagent"): it does Mg-halogen exchange on aryl chlorides,
heteroaryl bromides, and even some allylic systems. Modern
total syntheses lean on it for selectivity.
