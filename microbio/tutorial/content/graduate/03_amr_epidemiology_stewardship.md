# Antimicrobial resistance epidemiology + stewardship

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is one of the
defining global-health crises of the 21st century.
This lesson layers epidemiology + surveillance + drug-
development pipeline + stewardship policy on top of
the mechanism + treatment basics covered in the MB-3.0
intermediate "Antibiotic resistance" lesson.

## The global burden

Landmark numbers:
- **Lancet 2022 GBD on AMR (Murray et al.)** —
  estimated **1.27 million deaths in 2019 directly
  attributable** to bacterial AMR + ~ 4.95 million
  associated.
- **O'Neill review 2016 (UK government / Wellcome)** —
  projected **10 million deaths / year by 2050** if
  trajectories don't change + ~ $100 trillion
  cumulative GDP loss.
- **WHO 2020-2024** — AMR among top 10 global health
  threats; declared one of the WHO Bacterial Priority
  Pathogens List 2024 update.

Geographic pattern:
- Highest AMR burden — sub-Saharan Africa, south Asia.
- Highest resistance prevalence — south + east Asia
  (carbapenems), sub-Saharan Africa (Enterobacterales).
- Lowest — northern Europe (long-standing low antibiotic
  use + good infection control).

Pathogen pattern (Lancet 2022 attributable deaths):
1. *E. coli* (~ 220 K).
2. *S. aureus* (~ 180 K).
3. *Klebsiella pneumoniae* (~ 130 K).
4. *S. pneumoniae* (~ 110 K).
5. *A. baumannii* (~ 90 K).
6. *P. aeruginosa* (~ 75 K).
7. *M. tuberculosis* (~ 200 K MDR).

## Drivers of AMR

### Antibiotic use

Global antibiotic consumption:
- Total ~ 40 B daily doses / year human + livestock.
- Human consumption rose ~ 65 % from 2000 to 2015 +
  is still rising (mostly LMIC).
- Livestock: ~ 70 % of US + ~ 70 % of EU antibiotic
  tonnage; growth-promoter use phased out in EU
  (2006), still allowed in many countries.
- Aquaculture: increasing; little surveillance.
- Crop spraying: streptomycin + tetracyclines on
  pears + apples + citrus; AMR cross-selection
  documented.

### Healthcare-associated transmission

- Hospital outbreaks of carbapenem-resistant
  Enterobacterales (CRE), MRSA, VRE, *C. difficile*,
  *C. auris*.
- Long-term care facilities are reservoirs +
  amplifiers.
- Inter-facility patient transfer drives spread.

### Community + travel

- International travel disseminates resistant
  organisms (NDM-1 spread from south Asia ~ 2008+).
- Community ESBL acquisition rising.
- Recreational + occupational exposure (livestock-
  associated MRSA — LA-MRSA CC398 in pig farmers).

### Environmental

- WWTP effluent harbours resistant bacteria + ARGs +
  antibiotic residues at sub-MIC concentrations.
- River + ocean reservoirs.
- Soil resistomes — natural AMR genes in soil
  microbiomes are the ancestral source for many
  clinical resistance mechanisms.

## Surveillance systems

### Global

- **WHO GLASS (Global AMR Surveillance System)** —
  > 100 countries reporting; > 60 species-drug
  combinations.
- **WHO Tricycle protocol** — standardised
  surveillance of *E. coli* across human + animal +
  environment.
- **Global Burden of Disease (GBD) AMR study** — IHME-
  led; comprehensive country-level estimates.
- **WHO BPPL (Bacterial Priority Pathogens List)** —
  2017 + 2024 updates list pathogens by R&D priority.

### Regional / national

- **EU EARS-Net** (ECDC) — Europe's AMR surveillance
  + AMC (consumption).
- **CDC AR Bank + AR Lab Network + AR Threats
  Report** — US.
- **CIPARS** — Canada; integrated AMR + AMC
  + livestock.
- **JANIS** — Japan.
- **ResistanceMap (CDDEP)** — global open-data
  visualisation.
- **Wellcome Trust + JPIAMR + GARDP** — funder /
  enabler.

### Genomic surveillance

- **PulseNet** — US foodborne PFGE / WGS.
- **GenomeTrakr** — FDA pathogen WGS.
- **NCBI Pathogen Detection** — open WGS database +
  AR gene + plasmid analysis.
- **CARD (Comprehensive Antibiotic Resistance
  Database)** — curated AR gene + ontology.
- **ResFinder + AMRFinderPlus + RGI** — bioinformatic
  tools for AR prediction from genomes.

### Wastewater surveillance

- AMR genes + organisms in WWTP influent provide
  community-level signal.
- COVID-19 popularised wastewater approach for
  pathogens; AMR adoption growing.

## The drug-development gap

The market failure:
- Most novel antibiotics are reserved for last-line
  use → low volume → poor revenue.
- Resistance limits product life-cycle.
- High development cost (~ $1.5B per approved drug).
- Several recent companies (Achaogen, Aradigm,
  Melinta, Tetraphase) declared bankruptcy after
  FDA-approving novel antibiotics.

Push-pull incentives:
- **Push** — direct R&D funding (BARDA, JPIAMR,
  CARB-X, GARDP).
- **Pull** — market entry rewards (UK NICE
  subscription model — Pfizer cefiderocol +
  Shionogi sulbactam-durlobactam first contracts;
  proposed PASTEUR Act in US).
- **Combined market entry rewards (MERs)** + tax
  credits.

### Recent approvals

A selection from 2017+:
- Cefiderocol (Fetroja, 2019) — siderophore-cephalo-
  sporin.
- Sulbactam-durlobactam (Xacduro, 2023) — for CRAB.
- Imipenem-relebactam (Recarbrio, 2019).
- Meropenem-vaborbactam (Vabomere, 2017).
- Ceftazidime-avibactam (Avycaz, 2015).
- Ceftolozane-tazobactam (Zerbaxa, 2014).
- Plazomicin (Zemdri, 2018).
- Eravacycline (Xerava, 2018).
- Omadacycline (Nuzyra, 2018).
- Lefamulin (Xenleta, 2019).
- Tedizolid (Sivextro, 2014).
- Delafloxacin (Baxdela, 2017).
- Pretomanid (TB, 2019).
- Bedaquiline (TB, 2012).

### Pipeline

WHO 2023 review:
- ~ 27 antibiotics in clinical development (Phase 1-3
  + new application) targeting WHO priority
  pathogens.
- ~ 6 are first-in-class.
- ~ 10 are improvements on existing scaffolds.
- Coverage of WHO critical-priority pathogens
  uneven; *M. tuberculosis* over-represented vs
  gram-negative needs.

### Non-traditional approaches

Increasingly funded by CARB-X + others:
- Phage therapy (covered separately).
- Antibody adjunctives (e.g. *S. aureus* anti-
  toxin mAbs, *P. aeruginosa* anti-virulence mAbs).
- Microbiome-based prevention (live biotherapeutics).
- Vaccines for AMR pathogens (RSV, *S. aureus*,
  *C. difficile*, Group B *Streptococcus*, ETEC,
  *Klebsiella*, gonorrhoea).
- Anti-virulence drugs (quorum-sensing inhibitors,
  toxin neutralisers).
- Predatory bacteria (*Bdellovibrio*).
- CRISPR-Cas antimicrobials (Locus Biosciences).

## Stewardship in practice

Antimicrobial stewardship programs (ASPs) have
evidence-based core elements (CDC + IDSA + ISAP).

### Core elements

1. **Hospital leadership + commitment** —
   accountability + financial commitment.
2. **Stewardship leadership team** — physician +
   pharmacist + ID + microbiology.
3. **Pharmacy expertise** — ID-trained pharmacist.
4. **Action interventions**:
   - **Pre-authorisation** (formulary restriction +
     prescribing approval).
   - **Prospective audit + feedback** to prescribers.
   - **Facility-specific guidelines** based on local
     antibiogram.
   - **IV-to-PO switch**.
   - **Allergy assessment** (penicillin allergy de-
     labelling).
   - **Duration optimisation** (5 days for CAP, etc.).
   - **Diagnostic stewardship** (correct test
     ordering, proper interpretation).
5. **Tracking + reporting** of antibiotic use +
   resistance.
6. **Education** of clinicians + patients.
7. **Reporting to NHSN AUR module** (US) or
   equivalents.

### High-impact interventions

Evidence-based stewardship wins:
- **Penicillin allergy delabelling** — 90 % of
  reported allergies are not true allergies; testing
  + delabelling expands antibiotic options.
- **Procalcitonin guidance** — reduces duration in
  LRTI / sepsis (Procalcitonin trials mixed but
  positive on duration).
- **Rapid molecular diagnostics + ASP communication**
  — faster ID → earlier de-escalation.
- **Audit + feedback for surgical prophylaxis** —
  reduces duration without compromising efficacy.
- **Fluoroquinolone restriction** — reduces *C.
  difficile* + collateral damage.
- **Outpatient stewardship** — > 50 % of antibiotic
  prescribing is outpatient; opportunities in URI +
  bronchitis + acute sinusitis (most viral but get
  antibiotics).

### Outpatient + LMIC stewardship

Distinct challenges:
- Self-prescribing + over-the-counter availability
  in many LMIC.
- Quality of antimicrobials (substandard /
  falsified).
- Diagnostic capacity limited.
- Behavioural interventions, prescriber education,
  point-of-care diagnostics, regulatory
  enforcement.

## Infection prevention + control (IPC)

The other half of the equation:
- **Hand hygiene** — single most effective
  intervention; > 80 % evidence-based reduction in
  HAI when done well.
- **Environmental cleaning** + UV / hydrogen-peroxide
  vapour terminal cleaning.
- **Active surveillance** for MRSA / VRE / CRE +
  contact precautions.
- **Antibiotic stewardship** (above).
- **Vaccine strategies** for HCP + patient
  populations.
- **Bundles** — central-line, ventilator, surgical-
  site, urinary-catheter prevention.
- **Single-room isolation** for high-risk pathogens.

## Vaccine impact on AMR

Vaccines reduce AMR by:
- Directly reducing infection incidence → less
  antibiotic use.
- Reducing carriage of resistant strains.
- Pneumococcal conjugate vaccines reduced AMR-S.
  pneumoniae burden globally.
- HPV vaccines reduced cervical infections + cancer
  + secondary antibiotic use.
- RSV mAbs + vaccines reduce antibiotic
  prescribing for febrile infants.
- *S. typhi* TCV reduces typhoid + ciprofloxacin
  resistance pressure.

WHO + Gavi increasingly fund vaccines as AMR
intervention.

## The One Health approach

AMR transcends sectoral boundaries:
- **Quadripartite** (WHO + FAO + WOAH + UNEP)
  Joint Plan of Action 2022-2026.
- **National Action Plans (NAPs)** in most countries.
- **Codex Alimentarius** + **OIE** standards on
  veterinary antibiotic use.
- **Wastewater + environmental** surveillance
  integration.
- **Global Leaders Group on AMR** (UN High-Level
  meeting 2024 reaffirming commitments).

## Future directions

- **Diagnostic-driven prescribing** at point of care
  — rapid ID + AST in < 1 hour.
- **AI-assisted stewardship** — clinical decision
  support, EHR-integrated.
- **Vaccine R&D acceleration** for AMR pathogens.
- **Microbiome restoration** + protective live
  biotherapeutics.
- **Phage + non-traditional therapeutics** maturing
  to clinical use.
- **Sustainable financing models** for novel
  antibiotic R&D.
- **Behaviour change + societal awareness** at
  patient + clinician + policy levels.
