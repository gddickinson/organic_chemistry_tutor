# Phage therapy + bacteriophage biology

Bacteriophages are viruses that infect bacteria.
They are the most abundant biological entities on
Earth — ~ 10³¹ phage particles globally; outnumber
bacteria ~ 10:1.  After eclipse during the
antibiotic era, phages are now resurgent as a
clinical answer to MDR bacterial infection.

## Phage diversity + biology

Phages span an enormous structural + genomic range:

- **Caudoviricetes** (tailed phages) — most-studied
  classical morphologies; T-even (T2, T4 — *Myoviridae*
  with contractile tails), T-odd (T7 — *Podoviridae*
  with short tails), λ + lambdoid (*Siphoviridae*
  with long non-contractile tails).
- **Microviridae** — small ssDNA (φX174).
- **Inoviridae** — filamentous ssDNA (M13 — display +
  cloning workhorse).
- **Cystoviridae** — dsRNA, enveloped.
- **Leviviridae** — ssRNA (MS2, Qβ).
- **Tectiviridae** — internal-membrane dsDNA.
- **Plasmaviridae**, **Pleolipoviridae** — pleomorphic
  archaeal viruses.
- **Jumbo phages** — > 200 kb genomes; encode
  ribosomal RNA + tRNAs + their own DNA / RNA
  polymerases.

Genome sizes: ~ 4 kb (φX174) to ~ 750 kb (some jumbo
phages).  Genome content: structural genes + DNA
replication + transcription / translation factors
sometimes + lysis system + integrase (lysogenic
phages) + accessory genes including toxin / virulence
factor cargo.

## Lytic vs lysogenic life cycles

### Lytic

1. Adsorption to host receptor.
2. Genome injection.
3. Host shutoff + early gene expression.
4. Genome replication.
5. Late gene expression (capsid + lysis machinery).
6. Assembly + DNA packaging.
7. Lysis (typically holin → endolysin).
8. Release of progeny (10²-10⁴ per cell typical).

The lytic burst is the basis of phage therapy — kill
bacteria, release more phage, propagate the kill.

### Lysogenic / temperate

After infection, the phage genome integrates into the
host chromosome (or rarely persists as plasmid) →
**prophage** → host divides + carries phage along.
Triggered to lytic mode by SOS response / DNA damage.

Lysogenic phages can carry virulence cargo:
- **Diphtheria toxin** — corynephage β.
- **Cholera toxin** — CTX-φ.
- **Shiga toxin** — Stx-converting phages in
  enterohaemorrhagic *E. coli*.
- **Botulinum toxin** — *Clostridium botulinum* C / D
  serotypes via prophage.
- **PVL toxin in *S. aureus*** — phage Sa2mw.

Phage lysogeny is a major driver of bacterial
virulence evolution.

## Phage-host specificity

Phage receptors are specific bacterial surface
molecules:
- **LPS O-antigen + core sugars** — many gram-
  negative phages.
- **OmpA / OmpC / OmpF / LamB / FhuA / BtuB / TonA**
  — outer-membrane porins + transporters.
- **Pili / flagella** — type IV pili, F-pili (M13),
  flagellar shafts.
- **Wall teichoic acid + peptidoglycan** —
  gram-positive phages.
- **Capsule + S-layer** — variable.

Specificity is generally narrow: a single phage
typically infects only certain strains within a
species.  Combined "cocktails" cover broader strain
panels.

## Phage therapy — historical + modern

### Pre-antibiotic era (1917-1940)

Félix d'Hérelle (Pasteur Institute) discovered phages
in 1917 + immediately applied them therapeutically
(dysentery in children, plague in Egypt).  Eli Lilly
+ Squibb commercialised in the 1930s.  The advent of
broad-spectrum antibiotics in the 1940s collapsed
the Western phage industry.

The Soviet Union + Eastern Europe (Eliava Institute,
Tbilisi; Hirszfeld Institute, Wrocław) maintained phage
therapy continuously through the 20th century.

### Resurgence (2000s-)

Catalysed by:
- AMR crisis + drug-pipeline gap.
- Better understanding of phage biology + genomics.
- Compassionate-use cases proving clinical efficacy.
- Regulatory pathways for personalised therapy.

### Landmark cases

- **Tom Patterson (UCSD, 2017)** — pancreatitis →
  *Acinetobacter baumannii* MDR infection; rescued by
  IV phage cocktail (multiple phages from US Navy +
  US Army labs); the first published modern American
  case.  Co-founded UCSD CIPHR (Center for Innovative
  Phage Applications + Therapeutics).
- **Mallory Smith (CF + B. cepacia, 2015)** —
  attempted compassionate use; died but case
  galvanised CF + phage-therapy community.
- **Pranita Tamma (Hopkins) Mycobacterium abscessus
  case** — cystic-fibrosis lung transplant candidate;
  3-phage IV + topical cocktail eradicated;
  published *Nature Medicine* 2019.
- **Steffanie Strathdee book "The Perfect Predator"**
  popularised the modern phage-therapy story.
- **Belgium personalised programme** — AMR Action
  + Sciensano; > 100 patients treated with personalised
  phage matched to clinical isolate; outcome data
  published 2024.

### Modern phage-therapy paradigms

- **Personalised matching** — clinical isolate
  screened against phage library → matched cocktail
  → IV / inhaled / topical administration.
  Long turnaround (days-weeks); regulatory
  framework as compassionate use.
- **Fixed-cocktail products** — pre-formulated
  cocktails (e.g. Russian Pyophage / Intestiphage /
  Sextaphage); broader but lower individual hit rate.
- **Genetically-engineered phages** — host-range
  expansion, lytic-only conversion, payload
  delivery (e.g. CRISPR antimicrobials), de-immunisation.

## Drug-development pipeline

Active companies + programs:
- **Locus Biosciences** — CRISPR-Cas3-armed phages;
  Phase 2 UTI + Crohn's.
- **Adaptive Phage Therapeutics** — large library +
  matching service.
- **Armata Pharmaceuticals** — CF *Pseudomonas* + lung
  *S. aureus*.
- **BiomX** — IBD + acne *C. acnes* + lung CF.
- **PhagoMed** (acquired by BioNTech) — lytic enzymes
  + phage cocktails.
- **Pherecydes / Phaxiam** — bone + joint infections.
- **Pheriana** — HIV reservoir reduction.
- **Snipr Biome** — CRISPR-armed lactobacilli.

Indications under active study:
- Cystic-fibrosis lung infection (P. aeruginosa,
  B. cepacia, *M. abscessus*, *S. aureus*).
- Bone + prosthetic-joint infection (*S. aureus*,
  *Pseudomonas*).
- Diabetic foot ulcers.
- UTIs + recurrent UTI.
- Burn wounds.
- IBD (anti-IBD-flora cocktails).
- *C. difficile*.
- HIV reservoir purging (CRISPR-armed).

## Regulatory pathways

- **FDA Compassionate Use / Expanded Access** — most
  US clinical phage cases use this.
- **Clinical trials (IND)** — Phase 1/2/3 RCT
  pathway.  Several Phase 2 ongoing in 2026.
- **Personalised therapy frameworks** — EMA + FDA +
  several national regulators developing
  personalised-medicine pathways for
  per-patient-formulated phage.
- **Magistral preparation** (Belgium Royal Decree
  2018) — pharmacist-prepared per-patient phage
  treatments under defined quality framework.

## Lytic-enzyme / endolysin therapeutics

A spin-off — purified phage-encoded peptidoglycan
hydrolases ("enzybiotics") added directly:
- **Lysin CF-301 (exebacase)** — *S. aureus*; Phase 3
  in endocarditis / bacteraemia (results mixed).
- **Tonabacase** + others in development.
- Limitation: gram-negative outer membrane blocks
  external lysin access — engineered "lysocins" with
  membrane-translocation domains to overcome.

## Manufacturing + delivery

- **Production** — propagate on host bacteria;
  purification by tangential-flow + chromatography +
  ion-exchange to remove endotoxins.
- **Storage** — refrigerated (4 °C) typical; some
  formulations require -80 °C.
- **Administration** —
  - IV for systemic infection.
  - Inhaled (nebulised) for lung infection.
  - Topical for wounds.
  - Oral / rectal for GI.
  - Intra-articular for joint infection.

## Resistance to phages

Bacteria evolve phage resistance via:
- **Receptor modification / loss** — often fitness
  cost; phage-receptor + virulence-factor overlap can
  attenuate escapees.
- **Phage CRISPR-Cas immunity** (next lesson).
- **Restriction-modification systems** — hundreds of
  R-M systems.
- **Abortive infection (Abi)** — programmed
  cell death to halt phage spread.
- **Superinfection exclusion** — phage-encoded
  immunity in lysogens.

Phage cocktails address evolutionary phage-resistance
by combining phages targeting different receptors.
"Phage steering" exploits the trade-off — selecting
phage-resistant bacteria that have ALSO lost
virulence factors.

## Phage-antibiotic synergy

Sub-inhibitory antibiotics + phage often kill better
than either alone — antibiotic stress may upregulate
phage receptors or open access via cell-wall damage.
"Phage-antibiotic synergy (PAS)" is being explored
clinically for hard-to-clear chronic infections.

## Phages as research tools

- **Phage display** (George Smith + Greg Winter
  2018 Nobel) — display peptides / proteins on phage
  surface for affinity selection.  Standard in
  antibody discovery (humira / adalimumab origin).
- **Phage-assisted continuous evolution (PACE)** —
  Liu lab; phage propagation rate coupled to evolved
  protein function; enables 10²-10⁴ generations of
  directed evolution / week.
- **Cloning vectors** — λ + cosmid + phagemid
  vectors.
- **Educational** — T4 / T7 + φX174 widely used in
  microbiology teaching.
