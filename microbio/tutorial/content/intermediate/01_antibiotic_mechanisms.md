# Antibiotic mechanisms + classes

Antibiotics are the central pharmacology of clinical
infection.  Almost every clinical antibiotic targets
one of five bacterial processes: cell-wall synthesis,
protein synthesis, nucleic-acid synthesis, folate
metabolism, or membrane integrity.  Understanding the
target predicts spectrum, side effects, and resistance
patterns.

## The five mechanism classes

### Cell-wall biosynthesis inhibitors

The original wonder-drug class — Fleming's penicillin
(1928 + clinical 1942).  Targets are bacterial-only
(humans don't make peptidoglycan), so toxicity is
generally limited to immune-mediated allergy.

**β-lactams** acylate the catalytic serine of
penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) → covalently
inactivate transpeptidase activity → defective wall →
osmotic lysis (in growing cells).

Sub-classes:
- **Penicillins** — natural (penicillin G, V),
  aminopenicillins (amoxicillin + ampicillin),
  anti-staphylococcal (flucloxacillin, methicillin
  historical), anti-pseudomonal (piperacillin,
  ticarcillin).
- **Cephalosporins** — first (cefazolin), second
  (cefuroxime), third (ceftriaxone, ceftazidime),
  fourth (cefepime), fifth (ceftaroline,
  ceftobiprole — anti-MRSA).
- **Carbapenems** — broadest spectrum (imipenem,
  meropenem, ertapenem, doripenem); reserved for
  severe / MDR infections.
- **Monobactams** — aztreonam — gram-negative-only;
  safe in penicillin allergy.
- **β-lactamase inhibitors** — clavulanate, sulbactam,
  tazobactam, avibactam, vaborbactam, relebactam.
  Combined with β-lactams to restore activity against
  β-lactamase producers.

**Glycopeptides** bind the D-Ala-D-Ala terminus of
lipid II + nascent peptidoglycan → block cross-linking
sterically.

- **Vancomycin** — gram-positive only (too large for
  outer membrane).  Standard for MRSA + serious
  enterococcal + *C. difficile* (oral) infections.
- **Teicoplanin** — similar spectrum, longer t1/2.
- **Dalbavancin / oritavancin** — long-acting (single
  dose for ABSSSI).

**Lipoglycopeptides** (telavancin) + **fosfomycin**
+ **bacitracin** + **cycloserine** round out the
cell-wall arsenal.

### Protein-synthesis inhibitors

Target the bacterial 70S ribosome (composed of 30S +
50S subunits).  Selective toxicity from differences
between bacterial 70S + eukaryotic 80S ribosomes —
though mitochondrial ribosomes are 70S-like, which
explains some side effects.

**Aminoglycosides** bind the 30S subunit's A-site +
cause misreading + premature termination.

- Gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, streptomycin,
  neomycin (topical), plazomicin (newer).
- Spectrum: aerobic gram-negatives + synergy with
  cell-wall agents against enterococci + staphylococci.
- Toxicities: nephrotoxicity (proximal-tubular cell
  uptake) + ototoxicity (cochlear + vestibular hair
  cell damage; ~ 5-25 % depending on duration).
- Concentration-dependent killing → once-daily dosing
  preferred for most indications.

**Tetracyclines** bind the 30S subunit's A-site +
prevent aminoacyl-tRNA accommodation.

- Tetracycline, doxycycline, minocycline, tigecycline
  (broader, IV only), eravacycline, omadacycline.
- Bacteriostatic; broad gram +/- + atypicals
  (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Rickettsia) +
  *Borrelia* + *Plasmodium* (doxycycline malaria
  prophylaxis).
- Don't use in pregnancy + < 8 years (binds calcium
  in growing bones + teeth).

**Macrolides** + **lincosamides** + **streptogramins**
(MLS-B) all bind overlapping sites on the 50S subunit
near the peptidyl-transferase centre.

- **Macrolides** — erythromycin, clarithromycin,
  azithromycin (long t1/2 → 5-day "Z-pack"
  regimens), telithromycin (withdrawn for hepatotox),
  fidaxomicin (gut-selective for *C. difficile*).
- **Lincosamides** — clindamycin (anaerobes +
  gram-positives + suppresses toxin production in
  TSS).
- **Streptogramins** — quinupristin-dalfopristin
  (Synercid; off-market in many regions).

QT prolongation + CYP3A4 interactions (especially
clarithromycin) are key clinical concerns.

**Oxazolidinones** bind the 50S subunit P-site +
prevent 70S initiation complex formation.

- **Linezolid** — gram-positive (MRSA, VRE);
  myelosuppression with prolonged use; serotonin
  syndrome risk (MAOI activity).
- **Tedizolid** — improved tolerability + once-daily.

**Chloramphenicol** binds 50S + inhibits peptidyl
transferase.

- Broad spectrum, cheap, but rare aplastic anaemia
  (~ 1 / 10⁵-10⁶) + grey-baby syndrome restrict use
  in high-income settings.  Still used in low-resource
  settings for typhoid + meningitis.

### DNA / RNA synthesis inhibitors

**Fluoroquinolones (FQs)** inhibit bacterial DNA
gyrase (topoisomerase II) + topoisomerase IV →
double-strand breaks during replication.

- Ciprofloxacin (gram-negative + atypicals + some
  gram-positive), levofloxacin, moxifloxacin
  (broader gram-positive + anaerobic), delafloxacin
  (anti-MRSA).
- FDA black-box warnings: tendinitis + rupture, QT
  prolongation, peripheral neuropathy, CNS effects,
  hypoglycaemia, aortic aneurysm / dissection,
  exacerbation of myasthenia gravis.
- Once-popular, now reserved for specific indications
  due to side effects + collateral damage to
  microbiome.

**Rifamycins** inhibit bacterial RNA polymerase β
subunit.

- Rifampicin (TB workhorse, leprosy, prophylaxis for
  *N. meningitidis* + *H. influenzae* contacts),
  rifabutin (TB in HIV — fewer DDIs with PIs),
  rifapentine (long-acting; latent TB), rifaximin
  (gut-selective; hepatic encephalopathy + IBS-D +
  traveller's diarrhoea).
- Resistance arises rapidly with monotherapy → always
  combination in TB.
- Powerful CYP3A4 / 2C9 inducer → many DDIs.

**Metronidazole** — reduced + activated by ferredoxin
in anaerobes → DNA-damaging radical → strand breaks.

- Anaerobes, *C. difficile* (oral), protozoa
  (*Giardia*, *Entamoeba*, *Trichomonas*),
  *H. pylori* combination regimens.
- Disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol.

**Nitrofurantoin** — multiple intracellular damaging
effects; reduced concentrations in urine make it
useful only for cystitis.

### Folate-pathway inhibitors

**Sulfonamides** + **trimethoprim** sequentially
block bacterial folate biosynthesis (humans don't
make folate — we eat it):

- **Sulfonamides** — sulfamethoxazole, sulfadiazine —
  inhibit DHPS (dihydropteroate synthase).
- **Trimethoprim** — inhibits DHFR (dihydrofolate
  reductase).
- Synergistic combination **TMP-SMX (Bactrim,
  Septra, co-trimoxazole)** — broad gram-+/- +
  *Pneumocystis* + *Stenotrophomonas* + *Nocardia* +
  toxoplasmosis prophylaxis.
- Sulfa allergy is common (rash, SJS / TEN, drug-
  fever, AIN).

### Membrane disruptors

**Polymyxins** (colistin = polymyxin E, polymyxin B)
bind LPS + disrupt the outer membrane in
gram-negatives.

- Last-line for MDR / XDR / PDR gram-negatives.
- Nephrotoxicity + neurotoxicity restrict use.
- Resurrected from the 1960s by the carbapenem-
  resistance crisis.
- Plasmid-mediated **mcr-1** colistin resistance
  (since 2015) is a serious global threat.

**Daptomycin** is a calcium-dependent lipopeptide
that aggregates in the gram-positive membrane +
forms pore-like depolarising lesions.

- MRSA bacteraemia + endocarditis (right-sided);
  inactivated by lung surfactant so NOT for
  pneumonia.
- Myopathy + creatine kinase elevation.

## Spectrum + selection

A good empirical-antibiotic-choice habit:

1. **What's the syndrome?**  Pneumonia /
   pyelonephritis / cellulitis / meningitis /
   bacteraemia — each has a typical pathogen list.
2. **Community vs hospital-acquired?**  Hospital
   adds resistant + atypical organisms (MRSA,
   *Pseudomonas*, MDR Enterobacterales).
3. **Risk factors**?  Immunosuppression,
   instrumentation, recent abx, foreign body, region.
4. **Pick the narrowest agent** that covers the
   likely pathogens.
5. **De-escalate** based on culture + susceptibility.
6. **Stop** when treatment goal achieved (shorter
   courses are increasingly evidence-supported).

## PK / PD efficacy indices

Different antibiotic classes have different PK / PD
efficacy drivers (covered in PH-3.0 dose-response
lesson too):

- **Time > MIC** — β-lactams.  Frequent dosing or
  continuous / extended infusions.
- **Cmax / MIC** — aminoglycosides.  Once-daily
  high-dose maximises concentration-dependent
  killing.
- **AUC24 / MIC** — vancomycin (target 400-600
  mg·h/L), fluoroquinolones (target ≥ 100), linezolid.
- **MIC vs target** — newer drugs with mixed PK/PD
  drivers.

## Drugs by site / syndrome — quick reference

| Site | Common empirical regimen |
|------|--------------------------|
| Community pneumonia (mild outpatient) | amoxicillin OR doxycycline OR azithromycin |
| Community pneumonia (severe inpatient) | ceftriaxone + azithromycin |
| Healthcare pneumonia | piperacillin-tazobactam OR cefepime ± vancomycin ± aminoglycoside |
| Cystitis (uncomplicated) | nitrofurantoin OR fosfomycin OR TMP-SMX |
| Pyelonephritis | ceftriaxone OR ciprofloxacin |
| Cellulitis | flucloxacillin / cefazolin (CA-MRSA → TMP-SMX or doxycycline) |
| Meningitis | ceftriaxone + vancomycin (+ ampicillin if Listeria risk) |
| Bacteraemia (gram-positive cocci pending ID) | vancomycin |
| Septic shock empirical | broad gram-/+ coverage; consider dual gram-negative with MIC > breakpoint risk |
| *C. difficile* (severe) | fidaxomicin OR oral vancomycin |
| TB (active) | RIPE — rifampicin + isoniazid + pyrazinamide + ethambutol |
| HIV PJP | TMP-SMX |
| Cryptococcal meningitis | amphotericin + flucytosine → fluconazole consolidation |
