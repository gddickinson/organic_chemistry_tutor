# Antibiotic resistance

Antibiotic resistance is older than human medicine —
soil bacteria have been making antibiotics + the
genes to resist them for hundreds of millions of
years.  Modern clinical resistance reflects intense
selection pressure from medical + agricultural
antibiotic use over the past 80 years.

## The four mechanisms

Almost every clinical resistance trait fits one of
four categories.

### 1. Enzymatic inactivation

Bacterial enzymes degrade or modify the antibiotic
before it can act.

- **β-lactamases** — hydrolyse the β-lactam ring.
  - **Class A** (TEM, SHV, CTX-M ESBLs) — serine
    active site; inhibited by clavulanate.
  - **Class B (metallo-β-lactamases — NDM, VIM, IMP)**
    — Zn²⁺ active site; not inhibited by classical
    inhibitors but blocked by aztreonam +
    avibactam-equivalent classes (cefiderocol,
    aztreonam-avibactam).
  - **Class C** (AmpC) — chromosomal cephalosporinases;
    derepressed by some β-lactams in *Enterobacter*,
    *Serratia*, *Citrobacter*, *Pseudomonas*.
  - **Class D** (OXA) — serine; OXA-48 family is the
    main carbapenemase concern in Enterobacterales.
  - **CTX-M-15** is now the dominant ESBL globally.
  - **KPC** (*Klebsiella pneumoniae* carbapenemase)
    + **NDM** + **OXA-48** are the major
    carbapenemases.
- **Aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes** —
  acetyltransferases (AAC), phosphotransferases (APH),
  nucleotidyltransferases (ANT).  Hundreds of
  variants.  Plazomicin was designed to evade some
  of these.
- **Chloramphenicol acetyltransferase (CAT)**.
- **Erm methylases** — methylate 23S rRNA → MLS-B
  resistance.

### 2. Target modification

Alter the drug's target so it no longer binds.

- **Penicillin-binding-protein modifications** —
  - **PBP2a** (encoded by mecA) → MRSA + MRSE.
  - Mosaic PBP2x in penicillin-resistant
    *S. pneumoniae*.
  - Altered PBPs in *Enterococcus* (chromosomal,
    intrinsic).
- **VanA / VanB / VanC** — replace D-Ala-D-Ala with
  D-Ala-D-Lac (VanA / VanB) or D-Ala-D-Ser (VanC) →
  vancomycin no longer binds.  Plasmid-borne in
  enterococci (VRE); also VRSA from acquisition by
  *S. aureus*.
- **rpoB mutations** — confer rifampicin resistance.
  Single point mutation (most common: S531L) →
  10⁹-fold MIC increase.  Why TB requires combination
  therapy.
- **gyrA / parC mutations** — fluoroquinolone
  resistance.  Stepwise: 1st mutation → low-level R,
  2nd → high-level R.
- **23S rRNA mutations + erm methylases** — macrolide
  resistance in *S. pneumoniae* (mef + erm) +
  *Mycoplasma* + *Helicobacter*.

### 3. Efflux

Active export pumps remove the drug.

- **MFS family** — tetracycline efflux (TetA, TetB
  in Enterobacterales), macrolide efflux (MefA in
  *S. pneumoniae*).
- **RND family** — multi-substrate pumps in
  gram-negatives (AcrAB-TolC in *E. coli*, MexAB-OprM
  in *P. aeruginosa*).  Confer reduced susceptibility
  to multiple drug classes simultaneously.
- **ABC family** — energy-dependent.
- **MATE family**.

Efflux pumps are often constitutively expressed at
low level + induced under stress; many are intrinsic
to certain species' baseline resistance phenotype.

### 4. Decreased uptake / target bypass

- **OmpF / OmpC porin loss** in Enterobacterales →
  reduced β-lactam + fluoroquinolone uptake.
- **OprD loss** in *Pseudomonas* → carbapenem
  resistance.
- **LPS modification** → polymyxin / colistin
  resistance via PhoP-PhoQ + PmrA-PmrB → addition of
  4-amino-arabinose / pEtN to lipid A → reduced
  cationic polymyxin binding.  *mcr-1* is the
  plasmid-borne version of this.
- **Target-bypass** — *S. aureus* overproducing PBPs
  → bypass MIC; *S. pneumoniae* mosaic PBPs.
- **Folate-pathway bypass** — sul1 / sul2 / sul3 +
  dfrA genes encode resistant DHPS / DHFR.

## How resistance spreads

### Mutation

Spontaneous chromosomal mutation rate ~ 10⁻⁹ per
base per replication.  In a typical infection
(10⁹-10¹¹ bacteria), pre-existing resistance
mutations to single agents are essentially guaranteed.
This drives:
- TB combination therapy (mutation rates × mutation
  rate of two drugs = vanishingly small).
- HAART for HIV.
- Rifampicin always combined.

### Horizontal gene transfer (HGT)

The dominant mechanism for spread of resistance
across species + the rapid global emergence of new
resistance phenotypes:

- **Conjugation** — direct cell-cell DNA transfer via
  pilus.  Plasmids the major vehicle; conjugative
  transposons (ICEs) also.
- **Transformation** — uptake of free environmental
  DNA.  *S. pneumoniae*, *N. gonorrhoeae*,
  *H. influenzae* are naturally competent.
- **Transduction** — phage-mediated DNA transfer.
  Important in *S. aureus* (e.g. SaPI islands).

Resistance plasmids often carry MULTIPLE resistance
genes — pIncA/C plasmids carry CTX-M + carbapenemase
+ aminoglycoside modifiers + sulfonamide resistance
all at once.

### Selection pressure

Antibiotic use in:
- Human medicine (~ 30 % inappropriate per CDC).
- Animal husbandry (~ 70 % of US antibiotic use is in
  livestock).
- Aquaculture.
- Agriculture (apple + pear blight control).
- Hospitals (concentrated selection environment).

drives the emergence + maintenance of resistant
strains.  Resistance reversal upon antibiotic
withdrawal can take years + may be incomplete.

## ESKAPE pathogens

A 2008 acronym (Rice JID) for the six pathogens that
"escape" the effects of antibacterial drugs +
collectively cause the majority of nosocomial
infections + drive the AMR drug-development
pipeline:

- **E** — *Enterococcus faecium* (VRE).
- **S** — *Staphylococcus aureus* (MRSA, VISA, VRSA).
- **K** — *Klebsiella pneumoniae* (ESBL +
  carbapenemase).
- **A** — *Acinetobacter baumannii* (carbapenem-
  resistant CRAB).
- **P** — *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* (MDR / XDR).
- **E** — *Enterobacter* species (AmpC + ESBL +
  carbapenemase).

Sometimes extended to ESKAPEE (+ *E. coli*).
*Stenotrophomonas maltophilia* + *Burkholderia
cepacia* + *Achromobacter* round out the
non-fermenting gram-negative MDR concerns.

## Resistance phenotype taxonomy

CDC + ECDC standardised definitions:
- **Multidrug-resistant (MDR)** — non-susceptible to
  ≥ 1 agent in ≥ 3 antimicrobial categories.
- **Extensively drug-resistant (XDR)** — non-
  susceptible to ≥ 1 agent in all but ≤ 2 categories.
- **Pandrug-resistant (PDR)** — non-susceptible to all
  agents in all categories.

PDR isolates are still rare but increasingly reported —
particularly *Acinetobacter* + *P. aeruginosa* in
oncology + transplant + ICU settings.

## Treatment of resistant infections

### MRSA

- IV: **vancomycin** standard; **daptomycin** for
  bacteraemia / endocarditis (right-sided);
  **linezolid** for pneumonia or oral step-down;
  **ceftaroline** + **ceftobiprole** for selected
  indications.
- Oral: **TMP-SMX**, **doxycycline**, **clindamycin**
  for SSTI.

### VRE

- **Linezolid** or **daptomycin** are first-line.
- **Tigecycline** for some IAIs.
- New: **omadacycline**, **lefamulin** (limited VRE
  activity).

### ESBL Enterobacterales

- **Carbapenems** (meropenem, ertapenem, imipenem) are
  the workhorses.
- **Piperacillin-tazobactam** for non-bacteraemic
  + non-severe infections (MERINO trial).
- **Cefepime** if MIC ≤ 2.

### Carbapenem-resistant Enterobacterales (CRE)

- **Ceftazidime-avibactam** — KPC + OXA-48 producers.
- **Meropenem-vaborbactam** — KPC.
- **Imipenem-relebactam** — KPC.
- **Cefiderocol** — broad including NDM (siderophore
  cephalosporin).
- **Aztreonam-avibactam** — MBL coverage.
- **Plazomicin** — aminoglycoside-modifying-enzyme-
  resistant.
- **Tigecycline / eravacycline / colistin** — backup.

### MDR *Pseudomonas*

- **Ceftolozane-tazobactam** — improved Pseudomonas
  + ESBL coverage.
- **Cefiderocol** — broad including some MDR.
- Combination empirical therapy controversial; recent
  data favour monotherapy with susceptible drugs.

### CRAB (carbapenem-resistant *A. baumannii*)

- **Sulbactam-durlobactam** (FDA 2023).
- **Cefiderocol**, **colistin**, **tigecycline**
  combinations.
- One of the hardest gram-negative pathogens to
  treat.

### Drug-resistant TB

- **MDR-TB** (R + isoniazid R) — long-course regimens
  with bedaquiline + linezolid + pretomanid +
  fluoroquinolones; recent shorter all-oral 6-month
  BPaL / BPaLM regimens.
- **XDR-TB** (MDR + resistance to FQ + injectables) —
  more limited options; bedaquiline + linezolid +
  pretomanid + clofazimine.

## Resistance surveillance

Major systems:
- **WHO GLASS** (Global AMR Surveillance System) —
  > 100 countries.
- **CDC AR Bank + AR Lab Network** — US.
- **ECDC EARS-Net** — EU.
- **CIPARS** — Canada.
- **JANIS** — Japan.
- **PulseNet + GenomeTrakr** — outbreak WGS.

The Lancet 2022 GBD on AMR estimated **1.27 million
deaths directly attributable** to bacterial AMR in
2019 + ~ 4.95 million associated.  AMR could become
the leading cause of death globally by 2050 if
trajectories don't change (O'Neill 2016).

## Stewardship

Antimicrobial stewardship programs (ASPs):
- **Core elements** — leadership + accountability +
  drug expertise + actions (preauthorisation +
  prospective audit / feedback + IDSA/ASHP guidelines)
  + tracking + reporting + education.
- **Diagnostic stewardship** — appropriate test
  ordering, timely results, correct interpretation.
- **De-escalation** — narrow once organism +
  susceptibility known.
- **Duration optimisation** — shorter courses for
  most common syndromes (5 days for community
  pneumonia, 7 days for pyelonephritis, etc.).
- **IV-to-PO switch** when oral bioavailability
  appropriate.

## New drug pipeline

Brief progress as of 2026:
- **Cefiderocol** (Fetroja) — siderophore-cephalosporin
  for MDR gram-negatives.
- **Sulbactam-durlobactam** (Xacduro) — for CRAB.
- **Imipenem-relebactam** (Recarbrio) — for KPC + AmpC.
- **Meropenem-vaborbactam** (Vabomere) — for KPC.
- **Ceftazidime-avibactam** (Avycaz) — KPC + OXA-48 +
  some MBL.
- **Aztreonam-avibactam** — MBL-active β-lactam +
  inhibitor combo.
- **Tedizolid + delafloxacin + omadacycline +
  eravacycline + lefamulin + plazomicin** — newer
  scaffold-extensions of older classes.
- **Bedaquiline + delamanid + pretomanid** for TB.
- **Phage therapy** + **bacteriocins** + **predatory
  bacteria** + **vaccines for specific pathogens**
  (RSV, CMV, *S. aureus*, *C. difficile*) under
  development.

The pipeline remains thinner than the resistance
threat — economics + regulatory science + push +
pull incentives are active policy areas (PASTEUR Act,
EU AMR Action Plan).
