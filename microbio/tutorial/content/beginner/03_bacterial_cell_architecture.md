# Bacterial cell architecture

A bacterial cell looks deceptively simple under the
microscope — a smear of cytoplasm bounded by a wall.
The reality is a layered architecture exquisitely
adapted to growth, motility, communication, and
defence.

## The Gram divide

The 1884 Gram stain remains the first test ordered on
almost every clinical isolate.  Crystal violet + iodine
stain everything purple; ethanol decolourises;
safranin counterstain marks decolourised cells pink.

- **Gram-positive** retains crystal violet → purple.
- **Gram-negative** loses crystal violet, takes
  safranin → pink.

The colour difference reflects fundamentally different
envelope architectures.

### Gram-positive envelope

A single (inner) plasma membrane + a thick
peptidoglycan layer (20-80 nm).  Teichoic acids +
lipoteichoic acids project from the wall.  Some
species have a thin S-layer on top.

Examples: *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Streptococcus
pyogenes*, *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Bacillus
anthracis*, *Clostridium difficile*, *Listeria
monocytogenes*, *Enterococcus faecium*.

### Gram-negative envelope

Inner plasma membrane + a thin peptidoglycan layer +
a periplasmic space + an OUTER membrane bearing
lipopolysaccharide (LPS) on its outer leaflet.

Examples: *E. coli*, *Klebsiella pneumoniae*,
*Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Salmonella enterica*,
*Neisseria meningitidis*, *Helicobacter pylori*,
*Vibrio cholerae*.

LPS = endotoxin: lipid A anchor + core
oligosaccharide + O-antigen polysaccharide.  Lipid A
is recognised by host TLR4 → massive cytokine release
→ septic shock.

### Acid-fast bacteria

A third architecture: thick peptidoglycan + an outer
mycomembrane of mycolic acids (long-chain α-branched
β-hydroxy fatty acids) covalently bound via
arabinogalactan.  Resists Gram + most antibiotics +
detergents + drying.

Examples: *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, *M. leprae*,
*M. avium*, *Nocardia*.

Stain by acid-fast (Ziehl-Neelsen) or auramine
fluorescence.

## Peptidoglycan — the cell wall

Peptidoglycan is a chemical class found ONLY in
bacteria (a key reason most antibiotics targeting it
are safe in humans).

Structure:
- Glycan strands of alternating N-acetylglucosamine
  (GlcNAc) + N-acetylmuramic acid (MurNAc).
- Pentapeptide stems on each MurNAc (typically
  L-Ala-D-Glu-mDAP-D-Ala-D-Ala in gram-negatives;
  L-Ala-D-Glu-L-Lys-D-Ala-D-Ala in gram-positives).
- Cross-links between adjacent stems (DAP-D-Ala
  direct in gram-neg; pentaglycine bridge in *S. aureus*).

Synthesised by:
- MurA-MurF cytoplasmic precursor pathway.
- MraY + MurG construct lipid II at the inner-membrane
  cytoplasmic face.
- Lipid II flipped across the membrane by MurJ.
- **Transglycosylases** polymerise lipid II monomers
  into glycan chains.
- **Transpeptidases** (penicillin-binding proteins,
  PBPs) cross-link stems.

Antibiotics targeting cell-wall biosynthesis:
- **β-lactams** (penicillins, cephalosporins,
  carbapenems, monobactams) — covalent acyl-enzyme
  with PBPs.
- **Glycopeptides** (vancomycin, teicoplanin,
  dalbavancin) — bind the D-Ala-D-Ala terminus of
  lipid II.
- **Lipopeptides** (daptomycin) — disrupt membrane
  in gram-positives.
- **Bacitracin** — undecaprenyl-pyrophosphate
  recycling.
- **Fosfomycin** — MurA inhibitor.
- **Cycloserine** — D-Ala synthesis + Ddl inhibitor.

## The plasma membrane

A standard phospholipid bilayer with cholesterol-
free composition (in most bacteria; mycoplasmas
incorporate sterols from the environment).

Hosts:
- **ETC complexes** (no mitochondria — bacterial ETC
  lives in the plasma membrane).
- **Secretion systems** (T1SS-T9SS).
- **Sensor kinases** (two-component systems).
- **Flagellar motor** anchorage.

Targeted by:
- **Polymyxins** (colistin, polymyxin B) — bind LPS +
  disrupt outer membrane in gram-negatives.
- **Daptomycin** — calcium-dependent pore in
  gram-positive membranes.
- **Antimicrobial peptides** — cathelicidins,
  defensins (host-derived).

## Capsule

An extracellular polysaccharide (or rarely
polypeptide) layer beyond the cell wall.

Functions:
- Shield from phagocytosis.
- Protect from desiccation.
- Adhesion to surfaces.
- Antigen variation (escape from antibody response).

Capsule-typing classifies many pathogens:
- *S. pneumoniae* — > 100 serotypes, vaccinated against
  the most prevalent (PCV13, PCV15, PCV20, PPSV23).
- *N. meningitidis* — A / B / C / W / Y serogroups,
  multiple vaccines.
- *H. influenzae* — Hib (type b) the major paediatric
  pathogen, conjugate vaccine since 1985.
- *Klebsiella pneumoniae* — K1, K2 hypervirulent
  capsules associated with liver abscess + endophthalmitis.

## Flagella + motility

Most motile bacteria swim with flagella — long helical
filaments rotated by a membrane-embedded motor that
runs on proton-motive force (or sodium gradient in
some marine + alkalophilic species).

Flagellar architecture:
- **Filament** (FliC monomers) — the propeller.
- **Hook** + **rod** — universal joint + drive shaft.
- **Basal body** — embedded in inner membrane,
  spans peptidoglycan + outer membrane in
  gram-negatives.
- **Stator** complexes (MotA / MotB) — proton
  channels that generate torque on the rotor (FliG).

Flagellar arrangements:
- **Monotrichous** — single polar (*P. aeruginosa*,
  *Vibrio*).
- **Lophotrichous** — tuft at one pole (*Helicobacter*).
- **Amphitrichous** — both poles.
- **Peritrichous** — all over (*E. coli*, *Salmonella*).

Chemotaxis: bacteria swim by alternating "runs" (CCW
rotation, smooth swimming) + "tumbles" (CW rotation,
random reorientation).  Receptors monitor attractant /
repellent gradients; CheA-CheY phosphorylation modulates
tumble frequency.

Flagellar function is critical for pathogenesis in
*H. pylori* (penetrating mucus), *V. cholerae* (intestinal
colonisation), *Salmonella* (invasion).

## Pili / fimbriae

Shorter, non-rotating surface filaments.  Multiple types:
- **Common (type 1) pili** — adhesins (FimH at the tip
  binds mannose); critical for UPEC colonisation.
- **P-pili** (PapG) — bind globoside glycolipids;
  pyelonephritis.
- **Type IV pili** — twitching motility, transformation
  (DNA uptake), biofilm formation, conjugation.
- **Conjugative (sex) pili** — F-pilus mediates DNA
  transfer in conjugation.
- **Curli** — extracellular amyloid fibres in *E. coli*
  + *Salmonella*; biofilm structure.

## Endospores

A subset of gram-positives (*Bacillus*, *Clostridium*)
form endospores: dormant, dehydrated, multi-layered
structures that resist heat, radiation, desiccation,
chemicals, and time (some viable spores are millions of
years old in lab studies).

Endospore architecture (inside out):
- Core (highly dehydrated; 10 % water vs 80 % vegetative
  cell).
- Inner membrane.
- Cortex (modified peptidoglycan).
- Outer membrane.
- Coat (proteinaceous).
- Exosporium (extra layer in some species, e.g. anthrax).

Implications:
- **Sterilisation** must kill endospores → autoclave 121 °C
  × 15 min minimum, ethylene oxide, gamma irradiation.
- **Anthrax + tetanus + botulism + *C. difficile* + gas
  gangrene** — all spore-formers; environmental
  persistence → public-health relevance.

## Biofilms

Most bacteria in nature live in biofilms — surface-
attached or floating aggregates embedded in extra-
cellular polymeric substances (EPS) of polysaccharide
+ protein + eDNA.

Properties:
- 100-1000× more antibiotic-tolerant than planktonic.
- Quorum-sensing-coordinated.
- Persister-cell populations seed recurrence.

Clinically critical biofilms:
- **Cystic fibrosis lung** — *P. aeruginosa* biofilm
  driving morbidity + mortality.
- **Endocarditis** — *S. aureus* + *S. epidermidis* on
  damaged valves.
- **Implant infections** — prostheses + central lines +
  catheters + pacemakers.
- **Dental plaque** — multi-species community.
- **Otitis media + chronic sinusitis + chronic wounds**
  — biofilm-driven pathology.

Biofilm-disrupting strategies (mostly investigational):
- DNase + alginate lyase + glycoside hydrolases.
- Quorum-sensing antagonists.
- Bacteriophage (lytic; can penetrate biofilms).
- Surface coatings + topographical strategies.

## Internal organisation

Bacterial interiors are not "homogeneous bags" — they
have:
- **Nucleoid** — folded supercoiled chromosome.
- **Plasmids** — accessory circular DNA.
- **70S ribosomes** — clustered + organised.
- **Carboxysomes / magnetosomes / gas vesicles** —
  microcompartments with shell proteins.
- **MreB** — actin homologue, sets cell shape.
- **FtsZ** — tubulin homologue, drives cell division
  (the Z-ring).
- **Crescentin** — IF-like, sets *Caulobacter* curve.

This shatters the old "prokaryotes = unstructured"
caricature.  Modern microscopy reveals tightly-organised
sub-cellular biology rivaling eukaryotes in complexity.
