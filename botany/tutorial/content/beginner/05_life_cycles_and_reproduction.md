# Plant life cycles + reproduction

Every plant alternates between two multicellular
generations — a haploid **gametophyte** that produces
gametes by mitosis + a diploid **sporophyte** that
produces spores by meiosis.  This **alternation of
generations** is one of botany's organising principles
+ the dominance balance shifts dramatically across
the plant phylogeny.

## Alternation of generations

Generic life cycle:

```
  gametes (n)  →  fertilisation  →  zygote (2n)
   ↑                                     ↓
mitosis                               development
   ↑                                     ↓
gametophyte (n) ← germination       sporophyte (2n)
   ↑                                     ↓
spores (n) ← meiosis ←  spore mother cells (2n)
```

## Gametophyte vs sporophyte dominance across groups

### Bryophytes

- Gametophyte dominant (the visible green plant).
- Sporophyte small + dependent on gametophyte
  (the "stalk + capsule" growing out of the moss
  cushion).
- Sperm are flagellated → require liquid water for
  fertilisation.

### Ferns + lycophytes

- Sporophyte dominant (the fronds you see).
- Gametophyte small + free-living (the heart-shaped
  prothallus).
- Sperm still flagellated; water still required.

### Gymnosperms

- Sporophyte dominant (the tree).
- Gametophyte highly reduced + dependent on
  sporophyte (a few cells inside the ovule or
  pollen grain).
- Wind pollination (mostly) — pollen replaces
  swimming sperm.
- Naked seeds borne on cone scales.

### Angiosperms

- Sporophyte dominant.
- Gametophyte EXTREMELY reduced — male gametophyte
  is just 3 cells (the pollen tube + 2 sperm cells);
  female gametophyte is 7 cells / 8 nuclei (the
  embryo sac).
- Pollen + double fertilisation + flowers + fruits.

## Flowers — the angiosperm innovation

A flower is a modified shoot bearing four whorls of
modified leaves:

1. **Sepals** (calyx) — outermost; usually green +
   protective.
2. **Petals** (corolla) — pollinator attractants;
   often coloured + scented.
3. **Stamens** (androecium) — male; filament + anther
   with pollen-bearing locules.
4. **Carpels** (gynoecium) — female; stigma (receives
   pollen) + style + ovary (with ovules).

Variations:
- **Complete** vs **incomplete** — has all 4 whorls
  vs not.
- **Perfect** (bisexual) vs **imperfect** (unisexual)
  — has both stamens + carpels vs not.
- **Monoecious** vs **dioecious** — separate male +
  female flowers on the same plant vs on different
  plants.

Floral symmetry:
- **Actinomorphic / radial** — petals identical
  (lily, buttercup).
- **Zygomorphic / bilateral** — petals differ
  (pea, snapdragon, orchid).

Floral formulas + diagrams encode the structure
compactly + are taxonomically diagnostic.

## ABC model of flower development

The 1991 Coen + Meyerowitz model in *Arabidopsis* +
*Antirrhinum* explains how four whorls arise from
just three classes of homeotic genes:

- **A genes** alone → sepals (whorl 1).
- **A + B** → petals (whorl 2).
- **B + C** → stamens (whorl 3).
- **C alone** → carpels (whorl 4).

Mutations rearrange identities (e.g. C-loss → petals
in place of stamens; B-loss → sepals in place of
petals).  Now extended to ABCDE model (E = SEPALLATA
co-factors essential for all floral identities; D for
ovule).

Major floral-identity transcription factors:
- **APETALA1 + APETALA2** — A function.
- **APETALA3 + PISTILLATA** — B function.
- **AGAMOUS** — C function.
- **SEPALLATA1-4** — E function.
- **SEEDSTICK** + **SHATTERPROOF** — D function.

## Pollination

The transfer of pollen from anther to stigma.

### Pollination syndromes

Pollinator + flower co-evolution → characteristic
suites:
- **Insect (entomophily)** — coloured + scented
  + nectar-producing.  Bees: blue/yellow/UV; flies:
  putrid (carrion mimicry); beetles: bowl-shaped;
  butterflies: bright + tubular; moths: pale +
  night-scented.
- **Bird (ornithophily)** — red + tubular + nectar-
  rich; no scent (most birds have poor olfaction).
- **Bat (chiropterophily)** — large + pale + night-
  blooming + fermented-fruit smell + copious nectar.
- **Wind (anemophily)** — small + drab + petal-less
  + abundant pollen + feathery stigmas (grasses,
  many trees).
- **Water (hydrophily)** — rare; pondweeds.

### Buzz pollination

Some flowers (Solanaceae like tomato + eggplant)
release pollen only when buzzed at specific frequency
by bumblebees (~ 200-300 Hz) → poricidal anthers.

## Self vs cross-pollination

Cross-pollination promotes genetic diversity but most
plants have evolved diverse mechanisms for either:

### Outcrossing promoters

- **Dichogamy** — anthers + stigmas mature at
  different times.
- **Heterostyly** — long-styled + short-styled
  morphs (Primula, Fagopyrum).
- **Self-incompatibility (SI)** —
  - **Gametophytic SI** (Solanaceae, Rosaceae,
    Plantaginaceae) — pollen-tube growth blocked if
    pollen S-allele matches one of the pistil's S
    alleles.  S-RNase + F-box mechanism.
  - **Sporophytic SI** (Brassicaceae, Asteraceae) —
    stigma rejects pollen if pollen-coat S-haplotype
    matches one of the stigma's.  S-receptor kinase
    (SRK) + S-locus cysteine-rich (SCR) ligand.

### Selfing promoters

- **Cleistogamy** — flowers never open (peanut,
  some violets).
- **Self-compatibility** — a derived state common in
  weedy + colonising species.

## Double fertilisation — the angiosperm twist

Pollen germinates on stigma → grows pollen tube down
the style → enters ovule via micropyle → discharges
TWO sperm cells:
- **One sperm** + egg → diploid zygote → embryo.
- **Other sperm** + central cell (with two polar
  nuclei) → triploid endosperm → nutrient tissue
  for the developing embryo.

Double fertilisation is unique to angiosperms.
Endosperm = the rich nutrient store we eat in cereal
grains, coconut "meat" + "water", castor oil.

## Embryogenesis + seed development

Zygote → asymmetric divisions → embryo + suspensor.

Apical-basal polarity established by the first
asymmetric division (auxin gradients are critical;
PIN protein polar localisation mediates the auxin
flow).

Mature embryo organisation:
- **Cotyledons** — embryonic leaves; one in monocots
  ("monocotyledons"), two in dicots ("dicotyledons").
- **Hypocotyl** — embryonic stem below cotyledons.
- **Radicle** — embryonic root.
- **Plumule** — embryonic shoot apex.
- **Endosperm** — nutrient store (consumed during
  germination in monocots; absorbed by cotyledons
  pre-germination in many dicots).

Seed coat (testa) is derived from ovule integuments;
provides physical + chemical protection until
germination.

## Fruits

A ripened ovary (often with accessory tissues).

Major types:
- **Simple fruits** —
  - **Dry indehiscent** — achene (sunflower seed),
    grain (cereal), nut (acorn), samara (maple).
  - **Dry dehiscent** — capsule, follicle, legume,
    silique, silicle.
  - **Fleshy** — drupe (peach, cherry, olive), berry
    (tomato, grape, banana — botanically), pome
    (apple).
- **Aggregate fruits** — many ovaries from one flower
  (raspberry, strawberry — actually accessory).
- **Multiple fruits** — many ovaries from many
  flowers (pineapple, mulberry, fig).

Fruits exist primarily for seed dispersal:
- **Animal dispersal** — fleshy + edible (often
  bright + scented when ripe).
- **Wind** — winged, plumed, light (dandelion,
  maple, ash).
- **Water** — buoyant (coconut, mangrove).
- **Self / mechanical** — explosive (touch-me-nots,
  legume pods, witch hazel).
- **Ant (myrmecochory)** — elaiosomes attract ants
  that disperse seeds.

## Germination

Triggered by:
- Adequate water + temperature + O2.
- Sometimes light (lettuce phytochrome) or dark.
- Sometimes specific dormancy-breaking cues (cold
  stratification, smoke / karrikins for fire-adapted
  species, scarification).

Mobilisation of stored reserves:
- Starch → maltose → glucose (amylases activated by
  GA).
- Lipids → fatty acids → glyoxylate cycle → sucrose
  (glyoxysomes).
- Proteins → amino acids (proteases).

Embryonic axis emerges → radicle first → hypocotyl /
epicotyl elongation → cotyledons + first true leaves
photosynthesise.

## Vegetative + asexual reproduction

Not all reproduction is sexual — many plants reproduce
vegetatively:
- **Stolons / runners** (strawberry, spider plant).
- **Rhizomes** (ginger, bamboo, irises).
- **Tubers** (potato).
- **Bulbs** (onion, garlic, daffodil).
- **Corms** (gladiolus, taro).
- **Suckers** (aspen — single Pando clone in Utah
  ~ 100 acres + > 80 K years old).
- **Apomixis** — seeds without fertilisation
  (dandelion, blackberry; ~ 400 plant species).

Horticulturally exploited:
- **Cuttings** — root or stem fragments grow into
  whole plants.
- **Grafting** — combine scion + rootstock from
  different cultivars (most fruit trees, grapevines).
- **Layering** — bend stem to ground until it roots.
- **Tissue culture** — plant cells dedifferentiate +
  regenerate; basis of micropropagation +
  transgenic / CRISPR-edited plant production.
