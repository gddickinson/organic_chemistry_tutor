# Plant tissues + organs

A plant body is built from three vegetative organs
(root, stem, leaf) constructed from three tissue
systems (dermal, ground, vascular) that are
continuously generated by meristems.  This lesson
covers the architectural plan + the cell types that
make it work.

## Meristems — the source of new cells

Plants grow indeterminately (most species) by
maintaining populations of stem cells in **meristems**
that continually produce new tissue.  Three meristem
types:

### Apical meristems

- **Shoot apical meristem (SAM)** — at every shoot tip;
  generates leaves, stems, axillary buds, flowers.
- **Root apical meristem (RAM)** — at every root tip;
  generates root tissues + the root cap that protects
  the meristem from soil abrasion.

Both are organised stem-cell niches with:
- A central stem-cell pool (CZ in shoot; QC + IC in
  root).
- A surrounding zone of slowly-dividing organising-
  centre cells.
- Peripheral / subjacent zones of rapidly-dividing
  daughter cells that displace + differentiate
  outward.

Stem-cell maintenance signalling:
- **WUSCHEL (WUS) ↔ CLAVATA3 (CLV3)** in the SAM —
  organising centre + stem-cell feedback.
- **WOX5 + CLE40** in the RAM quiescent centre.
- Auxin maxima at root tip + leaf primordia.

### Lateral / secondary meristems

- **Vascular cambium** — between xylem + phloem;
  produces secondary xylem (wood) inward + secondary
  phloem outward.  Drives radial / girth growth.
- **Cork cambium (phellogen)** — produces cork (phellem)
  outward → bark.

Most monocots lack secondary growth (palms grow only by
SAM activity); woody dicots + gymnosperms add girth
indefinitely → tree trunks.

### Intercalary meristems

Found in grasses + horsetails — between mature tissue
zones at the base of internodes / leaf sheaths.
Allow regrowth after grazing or mowing.

## Three tissue systems

### Dermal tissue — the outer skin

**Epidermis** — single-cell-layer surface tissue.
Functions:
- Mechanical barrier.
- Cuticle (waxes + cutin) — water + UV + pathogen
  barrier.
- Stomata for gas exchange (covered below).
- Trichomes (hairs) — defence, water capture, salt
  excretion, glandular secretion (essential oils,
  resin, nectar).
- Root hairs — single-cell extensions vastly
  expanding root surface area for water + ion
  uptake.

**Periderm** — replaces epidermis in woody stems +
roots.  Cork (phellem) + cork cambium (phellogen) +
phelloderm.  Permeated by lenticels for gas exchange.

### Stomata

Pore + 2 guard cells.  Open / close in response to:
- Light (blue-light receptors phototropin 1/2).
- CO2 status of leaf (low CO2 → open).
- Water status (ABA → close).
- Temperature + humidity.
- Pathogen perception (close).

Stomatal aperture controls the trade-off between CO2
uptake (photosynthesis) + water loss (transpiration).
The most-densely-stomata-bearing surface is typically
the abaxial (lower) leaf face.

### Ground tissue — the bulk

Three cell types named by their walls:
- **Parenchyma** — thin-walled, living, multifunctional;
  most of the plant body's cells; photosynthesis
  (palisade + spongy mesophyll), storage (cortex,
  pith), wound-healing.
- **Collenchyma** — unevenly thick primary walls;
  living; flexible support of growing organs (celery
  string).
- **Sclerenchyma** — thick lignified secondary walls;
  dead at maturity; rigid support.  Two flavours:
  **fibres** (long, in bundles; hemp, jute, sisal,
  flax) + **sclereids** (variable shape; pear gritty
  cells, nut shells, peach pit).

### Vascular tissue — the plumbing

**Xylem** — water + dissolved-mineral conduit; mostly
dead at maturity.

Cell types:
- **Tracheids** — single elongated dead cells with
  pitted walls (water flow through pits between adjacent
  tracheids).  Found in all vascular plants.
- **Vessel elements** — wider, perforated end-walls
  → larger continuous conduits.  Mostly angiosperms;
  more efficient but more vulnerable to embolism.
- **Xylem parenchyma + fibres** — living parenchyma
  for storage + structural fibres.

**Phloem** — sugar + signal-molecule conduit; LIVING
at maturity (uniquely; most other dead-at-maturity
tissues).

Cell types:
- **Sieve elements** (sieve tube members in
  angiosperms; sieve cells in gymnosperms) — alive but
  enucleate; perforated end walls (sieve plates).
- **Companion cells** — adjacent to sieve elements;
  retain nucleus + organelles; metabolic + signalling
  support of sieve elements.
- **Phloem parenchyma + fibres**.

## Three vegetative organs

### Root

Functions:
- Anchor.
- Water + mineral uptake.
- Storage (often massive — beets, carrots, sweet
  potato).
- Hormone production (cytokinins, ABA precursors).
- Symbioses (mycorrhizae, N-fixing nodules).

Root system architecture:
- **Tap root** — primary root persists + dominates;
  most dicots.
- **Fibrous root** — primary root replaced by adventitious
  roots; most monocots.
- **Adventitious roots** — from non-root tissue (stems
  for cuttings; nodal roots in maize).

Tissue zonation along root axis:
1. Root cap.
2. Meristematic zone.
3. Elongation zone.
4. Maturation / differentiation zone (root hairs).

Internal organisation (concentric):
- Epidermis (with root hairs).
- Cortex (parenchyma + endodermis with Casparian
  strip controlling apoplastic-to-symplastic ion
  flow).
- Pericycle (lateral root + secondary growth source).
- Stele (vascular cylinder — xylem + phloem).

### Stem

Functions:
- Support + display of leaves + flowers + fruits.
- Conduction (xylem + phloem).
- Storage (e.g. potato tuber is a modified stem;
  sugarcane stem).
- Photosynthesis in young / specialised stems
  (cacti, asparagus).

Internal organisation differs by group:
- **Eudicot stem** — vascular bundles in a ring;
  pith in the centre; cortex outside; secondary
  growth from vascular cambium.
- **Monocot stem** — vascular bundles scattered
  through the ground tissue; no continuous cambium;
  no secondary growth in most species.

Branching:
- **Axillary buds** (in leaf axils) → branches +
  flowers.
- Apical dominance (auxin from SAM suppresses
  axillary bud growth) explained by auxin canal
  signalling + strigolactones.

### Leaf

The principal photosynthetic organ.

Anatomy:
- **Cuticle** — wax-cutin barrier.
- **Upper epidermis** — single layer; few stomata.
- **Palisade mesophyll** — columnar parenchyma packed
  with chloroplasts; primary photosynthetic site.
- **Spongy mesophyll** — loosely packed parenchyma
  with air spaces for gas exchange.
- **Vascular bundles (veins)** — branching xylem +
  phloem network.  Bundle sheath cells surround the
  vein (specialised in C4 plants — see Photosynthesis
  lesson).
- **Lower epidermis** — stomata-rich; trichomes
  variable.

Leaf morphology + shape (simple vs compound, lobed,
margin shape, venation pattern) is taxonomically
diagnostic.

Modifications:
- **Spines** (cactus, hawthorn) — defence; stem-derived
  in some, leaf-derived in others.
- **Tendrils** (peas, grapes) — climbing.
- **Bracts** (poinsettia red "petals") — leaf-derived
  pollinator attractants.
- **Bulbs + onions** — fleshy storage leaves.
- **Trap leaves** — Venus flytrap, Drosera, Nepenthes.

## Reproductive organs (briefly here; deeper in next
lesson)

- **Flowers** — angiosperm reproductive shoots; sepals
  + petals + stamens (male) + carpels (female).
- **Cones** — gymnosperm reproductive structures.
- **Spores + sporangia** — non-seed plants.
- **Fruits** — angiosperm seed-dispersal structures
  (ripened ovaries).
- **Seeds** — embryo + nutrient store + seed coat.

## Body plans across plant groups

- **Bryophytes** — gametophyte-dominant; no true
  vasculature; no roots (rhizoids); tiny.
- **Lycophytes** — tracheophyte but microphylls
  (single vein) + dichotomous branching.
- **Ferns** — megaphylls + roots + xylem; gametophyte
  + sporophyte free-living.
- **Gymnosperms** — cones; tracheids only (no vessels
  in most); naked seeds; mostly evergreen.
- **Angiosperms** — flowers + fruits + double
  fertilisation + vessels + companion cells; the
  evolutionary winners.
