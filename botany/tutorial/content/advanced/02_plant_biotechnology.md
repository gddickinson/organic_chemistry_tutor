# Plant biotechnology + crop improvement

The history of crop improvement is one of humanity's
oldest + most-consequential technological projects.
From Mendel's peas to CRISPR-edited tomatoes, the
toolbox keeps expanding.

## Domestication — the first 10 000 years

Agricultural origins ~ 12 K years ago in multiple
centres:
- **Fertile Crescent** — wheat, barley, lentil,
  pea, chickpea, fava, flax.
- **East Asia** — rice, soybean, Brassica, foxtail
  millet, mung bean, peach.
- **Mesoamerica** — maize, beans, squash (the "three
  sisters"), tomato, chili.
- **Andes** — potato, quinoa, amaranth, chenopod.
- **Africa** — sorghum, pearl millet, finger millet,
  cowpea, yam, oil palm, coffee.
- **New Guinea** — banana, sugarcane, taro, breadfruit.

The "domestication syndrome" emerged across crops
independently:
- Larger seeds + fruits.
- Loss of seed shattering / dispersal mechanisms
  (selection for retention until harvest).
- Loss of seed dormancy.
- Loss of bitter / toxic compounds.
- Reduced branching + more determinate growth.
- Greater synchrony of flowering / ripening.
- Larger flowers + showy pollinator displays
  (or wind pollination intensification).

Domestication was driven by selection of a few
key alleles + accompanied by SIGNIFICANT genetic
bottlenecks → modern crop diversity is much
narrower than that of wild relatives.

## Mendel + classical genetics

Gregor Mendel (1860s) — peas with 7 contrasting
traits → laws of inheritance.  Mendel's work was
ignored, then rediscovered ~ 1900 + integrated with
chromosome theory by Sutton + Boveri + Morgan.

Classical breeding through the early 20th century:
- Pure-line + pedigree selection.
- Mass selection + recurrent selection.
- Hybridisation + back-crossing.
- Hybrid vigour (heterosis) → hybrid corn (Henry
  Wallace's Pioneer Hi-Bred).

## The Green Revolution

Norman Borlaug (Nobel Peace 1970) + collaborators
at CIMMYT + IRRI bred semi-dwarf wheat + rice in
the 1960s.

Key features:
- **Semi-dwarf habit** (Rht alleles in wheat; sd1
  in rice — both GA pathway mutations) → less
  lodging + more harvest index.
- **Photoperiod insensitivity** → grow at multiple
  latitudes.
- **High yield potential** with N-fertiliser
  + irrigation + pesticide inputs.

Yield doubled or tripled in many regions.  Saved
~ 1 billion lives by averted famine.  Critiqued
for input-dependence + environmental degradation
+ socioeconomic disruption + biodiversity loss
+ over-reliance on a few cultivars.

## Marker-assisted selection (MAS)

DNA markers linked to QTLs (quantitative trait
loci) accelerate breeding:
- Genotype seedlings before phenotype manifests.
- Stack multiple QTLs (resistance, quality, yield)
  more efficiently than phenotypic selection alone.
- Enable backcross conversion of elite cultivars
  with introgressed traits.

Classical markers: RFLP → AFLP → SSR → SNP-array →
NGS / GBS.

Modern method:
- **Genomic selection (GS)** — train predictive
  model on phenotyped + genotyped reference
  population → predict breeding value of
  genotyped-only candidates → select breeding
  parents.  Used in cereals + cattle + dairy.
  Greatly accelerates gain-per-cycle.

## Transgenic crops — first generation

The first commercial GMO crop (Flavr Savr tomato)
was approved in 1994.  The big breakthroughs
came with **Bt** + **glyphosate-tolerance**:

### Bt crops

*Bacillus thuringiensis* Cry proteins are insecticidal
toxins targeting specific insect orders.  Transgenic
maize + cotton + soybean + potato express Cry
proteins → resistance to lepidopteran + coleopteran
pests.

- Bt corn (Cry1Ab, Cry1F, Cry3Bb1, Cry34/35Ab1,
  Vip3A) → reduced insecticide spraying;
  documented benefits to non-target insects +
  farmer health.
- Bt cotton — > 90 % of US cotton + > 95 % of
  Indian cotton.

Resistance management: refuges (non-Bt fields)
required to slow Cry-resistance evolution.  Multi-
toxin "pyramided" varieties (Cry1Ab + Cry2Ab +
Vip3A) extend the window.

### Glyphosate-tolerance (Roundup-Ready)

EPSPS (5-enolpyruvylshikimate-3-phosphate synthase)
is glyphosate's target enzyme; bacterial CP4 EPSPS
is naturally glyphosate-resistant.  Transgenic
soybean + corn + cotton + canola express CP4 EPSPS
→ tolerance to glyphosate herbicide.

Adopted on > 90 % of US soybean, corn, cotton,
sugar beet, canola.

Resistance management failed → > 50 glyphosate-
resistant weed species globally; new herbicide-
tolerance traits stacked (dicamba, 2,4-D) but
problems persist.

### Other transgenic traits

- **Virus resistance** — papaya ringspot virus
  resistance saved Hawaiian papaya industry; squash
  resistance.
- **Drought tolerance** — DroughtGard (HB4) corn
  + soybean (Argentina, US).
- **Disease resistance** — Innate potato (Hi-bred);
  Arctic apple (browning resistance via PPO RNAi).
- **Nutritional improvement**:
  - **Golden Rice** — provitamin A (β-carotene)
    accumulation; aimed at vitamin-A deficiency
    in rice-dependent populations.  Approved in
    Philippines 2021 (a long-delayed, well-known
    case study in the GMO debate).
  - High-oleic soybean.
  - Drought-tolerant cassava + corn.

## CRISPR-edited crops

Site-specific editing without foreign DNA →
products are often regulated AS NON-GMO under
USDA APHIS + several other regulatory regimes.

Approved or near-market CRISPR crops:
- **Sicilian Rouge tomato** — high-GABA,
  Sanatech Seed (Japan, 2021).
- **Calyxt Calyno soybean oil** — high-oleic, low
  saturated fat, US (2019).
- **Inari corn / soybean / wheat** — quantitative
  trait stacking.
- **Tropic mushroom** — non-browning button
  mushroom (USDA non-regulated, 2016).
- **Pairwise blackberries** — seedless via CRISPR.
- **Yield10 oilseed crops** — improved oil
  composition.
- **British wheat (CRISPR field trial)** —
  reduced acrylamide (Rothamsted).

CRISPR enables:
- **Precise mutations** in known QTLs (e.g.
  recreating sd1 / Rht-equivalent dwarfing).
- **Allele swaps** at trait loci.
- **Multi-gene stacking** with multiplexed sgRNAs.
- **Pathway engineering** — knock out repressors
  + over-express activators of biosynthetic
  pathways.
- **De novo domestication** of wild relatives —
  add domestication alleles (e.g. ground cherry
  *Physalis pruinosa*, wild tomato *Solanum
  pimpinellifolium* by Zach Lippman lab).
- **Synthetic apomixis** — engineer
  clonal-seed production for hybrid maintenance.

## Cisgenesis vs transgenesis

- **Transgenic** — DNA from a different SPECIES
  inserted (Bt corn, Roundup-Ready).
- **Cisgenic** — DNA from a sexually-compatible
  species (e.g. wild apple gene moved to cultivated
  apple).
- **Intragenic** — engineered insert containing
  only species' own DNA elements.
- **CRISPR-edited (no insert)** — no foreign DNA
  in final product; site-specific edits.

Some regulatory regimes treat cisgenic + edited
plants more permissively than transgenic.  EU
historically treated all gene-edited as GMO; 2024
proposed deregulation for cisgenic + edited
products under "New Genomic Techniques" framework.

## Plant tissue culture + transformation

The technical workhorses:

### Tissue culture

- **Callus** — undifferentiated cell mass from
  explants on auxin + cytokinin medium.
- **Shoot regeneration** — high cytokinin / low
  auxin.
- **Root regeneration** — high auxin / low cytokinin.
- **Embryogenesis** — somatic-embryo development
  (carrot, palm).
- **Protoplast culture** — wall-removed cells →
  fusion + transformation.

### Transformation methods

- **Agrobacterium tumefaciens** — natural plant
  pathogen; transfers T-DNA to plant chromosome.
  The dominant transformation system; works for
  most dicots + many monocots.
- **Particle bombardment (biolistics)** — DNA-coated
  gold / tungsten micro-projectiles fired into
  cells.  Mainly used for cereals + chloroplast
  transformation.
- **Floral dip** (Arabidopsis) — dip flower in
  Agrobacterium suspension → transformed seeds.
- **Direct DNA uptake** in protoplasts via PEG /
  electroporation.
- **Microinjection** in pollen / zygotes.

### CRISPR delivery

- **Stable transformation** (Agro / biolistics
  + selection) → integrated Cas + sgRNA cassette.
- **Transient expression** (Agroinfiltration of
  Nicotiana benthamiana) — for testing.
- **RNP delivery** — Cas9 protein + sgRNA delivered
  directly; transient + leaves no DNA → non-GMO
  status in some jurisdictions.

## Speed breeding + accelerated improvement

Modern techniques compress generation time:
- **Speed breeding** — 22-h photoperiod + temperature
  cycles → 5-6 wheat / barley / pea / chickpea
  generations / year (vs 1-2 conventional).
- **Doubled-haploid (DH)** technology — anther
  culture or wide-cross + chromosome elimination
  → instant homozygosity.
- **Embryo rescue** — wide-cross hybrids would die
  without rescue from the cross to a maternal
  parent.

## Genomic selection + machine learning

Modern breeding pipelines integrate:
- Genome-wide marker data.
- Phenotypic data with high-throughput
  phenomics (drone + satellite + sensor).
- Environmental data (G × E modelling).
- ML / deep-learning trait-prediction models.
- Optimal contribution selection for diversity
  preservation.

## Crop wild-relative + germplasm preservation

A diversity insurance:
- **Svalbard Global Seed Vault** — > 1.2 M
  accessions.
- **CGIAR centres** (CIMMYT, IRRI, ICRISAT, ICARDA,
  CIP, IITA, etc.) — international + national
  collections.
- **Genetic resources** for breeding new
  resistance + climate-tolerance traits.
- Wild relatives of wheat (*Aegilops*),
  potato (wild *Solanum*), tomato (wild
  *Lycopersicon*), banana (*Musa* relatives),
  soybean (wild *Glycine soja*) all serve as gene
  donors for resistance breeding.

## Sociopolitical context

The GM / CRISPR debate engages:
- **Public perception + risk communication**.
- **Regulatory frameworks** (US APHIS / FDA / EPA;
  EU ENGL + EFSA; national variations).
- **Intellectual property** — Monsanto + DuPont
  + Syngenta + Bayer concentration; patent +
  PVP / UPOV regimes; farmer-saved-seed
  litigation.
- **Food sovereignty + agroecology** vs
  industrial-scale biotech — different visions of
  agricultural futures.
- **Equity** — who benefits from biotech innovations
  (high-input vs smallholder; commercial vs
  subsistence).
- **Africa + Asia** — Bt cowpea (Nigeria 2019),
  CRISPR cassava + banana (Africa pipelines), GM
  brinjal (Bangladesh) all exemplify
  high-impact biotech in developing-world
  agriculture.
