# Synthetic biology + plant engineering

Plants are increasingly the chassis for synthetic
biology — their photosynthetic chemistry, modular
metabolism, and natural ability to scale from
single cells to multi-tonne biomass make them
ideal for production of food, feed, fibre, fuels,
chemicals, and pharmaceuticals.

## Why plants?

Compared to microbial chassis (yeast, *E. coli*,
*Bacillus*):
- **Free energy + carbon from sunlight + air**.
- **Massive scale + low marginal cost** — millions
  of hectares already cultivated.
- **Complex eukaryotic post-translational machinery**
  for proteins (glycosylation, disulfide bond
  formation, multi-subunit assembly).
- **Multiple compartments** (chloroplast, mitochondrion,
  ER, vacuole, peroxisome) → spatial control of
  pathway engineering.
- **Plastid genomes** allow high-copy heterologous
  expression without nuclear chromatin silencing.
- **Limited regulatory hurdles** for non-food
  applications (industrial enzymes, fibre, fuels)
  + emerging frameworks for food.

Disadvantages:
- Slow life cycles (months to years).
- Low transformation efficiency in many species.
- Containment + biosafety considerations
  (out-crossing, gene flow).
- Field variability in protein yields.
- Public + regulatory scrutiny.

## Photosynthesis enhancement

Multiple strategies under active development.

### Improving RuBisCO

- Engineer better catalytic constants (faster
  turnover, lower oxygenase rate).
- Replace plant RuBisCO with cyanobacterial /
  proteobacterial variants (some success in
  tobacco — Donald Ort + Stephen Long groups).
- **CCMs (carbon-concentrating mechanisms)** from
  cyanobacteria — install bicarbonate transporters
  + carboxysome-like microcompartments in plant
  chloroplasts (very challenging; partial progress).
- **Pyrenoid engineering** — algal pyrenoid
  components into plants.

### Photoprotection optimisation

- **NPQ relaxation tuning** — engineer faster
  recovery from photoprotective state when light
  drops; tobacco field trials showed 15-20 % yield
  increase (RIPE — Realising Increased Photosynthetic
  Efficiency consortium, Ort + Long).
- **State transition + LHC dynamics**.

### Bypassing photorespiration

- Install bacterial / synthetic glycolate
  catabolism pathways in chloroplast → less
  energetically wasteful processing of 2-PG.
- South lab + RIPE consortium showed > 40 % biomass
  increase in tobacco; rice + soybean trials
  ongoing.

### C3 → C4 conversion

- C4 Rice Project — multi-decade attempt to install
  C4 anatomy + biochemistry in rice (a C3 crop).
- Requires Kranz anatomy (bundle-sheath cell
  proliferation + chloroplast specialisation) +
  PEPC + carbonic anhydrase + decarboxylating
  enzyme + transport machinery.
- Partial progress; functional C4 not yet achieved
  in a single-cell C3 plant.

### Synthetic carbon-fixation pathways

- **Engineered alternative cycles** like CETCH (Tobias
  Erb) more efficient than Calvin cycle in vitro.
- Translation to whole-plant systems remains a
  long-term goal.

## Nitrogen fixation transfer

Holy grail of agriculture: cereals fixing their own
N like legumes.

### Approaches

1. **Transfer the entire nitrogenase operon** into
   plant chloroplast / mitochondrion.  ~ 16 nif
   genes.  O2-sensitive enzyme — needs anaerobic
   compartment.  Partial assembly demonstrated;
   functional N fixation not yet.
2. **Engineer the legume-rhizobium symbiosis pathway**
   into cereals — common-symbiosis-pathway (CSP)
   genes already exist in cereals (mycorrhizal
   shared) → repurpose for rhizobial symbiosis.
3. **Associative N fixers** — engineer free-living
   N-fixers (PivotBio, Joyn Bio) that colonise
   rhizosphere + transfer N to host.
4. **Nodule organogenesis** — re-program cereal
   roots to produce nodule-like structures
   accommodating N-fixing bacteria.

Currently active: PivotBio Proven (microbial
inoculant for corn), Joyn Bio (Bayer-Ginkgo JV),
ENSA programme (Gates Foundation), Bill Rutter
+ Eric Triplett's earlier programmes.

## Chloroplast engineering — transplastomic plants

Advantages:
- High-copy genomes (50-100 plastids × 50-100 copies
  each = 5 K-10 K copies / cell) → high heterologous
  protein yields.
- Maternal inheritance prevents pollen-mediated
  transgene flow.
- Polycistronic operons — multiple genes from one
  promoter.
- Easier to engineer entire pathways in one
  compartment.

Limitations:
- Transformation by particle bombardment + slow
  selection cycle (years for tobacco).
- Limited to certain species (tobacco, lettuce,
  tomato, potato, soybean — most cereals
  resistant).
- No glycosylation or other ER-dependent
  modifications.
- Maternal inheritance not ideal for some breeding
  schemes.

Notable products:
- **Tobacco-produced biopharmaceuticals** —
  vaccines, antibodies (limited commercial uptake).
- **Engineered chloroplast pathways** for terpenoid +
  carotenoid production.
- **Recombinant insulin in plant chloroplasts**
  (research stage).

## Plant molecular pharming

Production of pharmaceuticals + biologics in plants.

### Approaches

- **Stable transgenic crops** — tobacco, corn, rice,
  carrot, lettuce.
- **Transient expression** — agroinfiltration of
  *Nicotiana benthamiana* for fast (~ 7-14 day)
  production of recombinant proteins.
- **Hairy-root cultures** — *Agrobacterium rhizogenes*-
  transformed root cultures producing secondary
  metabolites.
- **Plant cell suspension cultures** — *Daucus carota*
  cell suspension produces taliglucerase alfa
  (Elelyso, the first plant-cell-derived
  approved biopharmaceutical, 2012, for Gaucher
  disease).

### Approved + late-stage products

- **Taliglucerase alfa (Elelyso)** — Gaucher disease
  enzyme; carrot cell culture; FDA 2012.
- **ZMapp** — anti-Ebola monoclonal antibody
  cocktail; tobacco-produced; Emergency-use 2014.
- **Medicago Covifenz** — virus-like-particle
  COVID vaccine; *N. benthamiana*-produced; Health
  Canada approved 2022; company shut down 2023.
- **Pfizer / Protalix Glucocerebrosidase** — pipeline.
- **Influenza vaccines** in plants — multiple
  companies.

## CRISPR + multiplex genome editing in plants

Plant CRISPR has matured rapidly:
- **CRISPR-Cas9 / 12 / 13** — knock-out, knock-in,
  base editing, prime editing.
- **Multiplexing** — single transcript with multiple
  sgRNAs (Cas12 self-processes its array; Cas9
  systems use tRNA / Csy4 / Cas13d processing).
- **Tissue-specific Cas expression** — limit edits
  to specific cell types or developmental stages.
- **Transient + DNA-free editing** — RNP delivery
  in protoplasts → regenerate plants without
  integrated transgene.
- **Allele replacement / homology-directed repair**
  — still difficult in plants but progressing
  (CRISPR-Cas12a + dsDNA donors; geminiviral
  replicons for donor amplification).

Recent applications:
- **De novo domestication** of wild crops (Zach
  Lippman lab — *Solanum pimpinellifolium*
  + ground cherry).
- **Disease resistance** — knock out susceptibility
  genes (e.g. *S* genes; powdery-mildew-resistant
  wheat by knocking out MLO).
- **Yield + quality traits** — high-amylose wheat,
  high-oleic soybean, low-acrylamide potato.
- **Climate resilience** — drought + heat + cold
  + salt tolerance loci.
- **Crop architecture** — height, branching, fruit
  size.

## Engineered plant-microbe + plant-fungal symbioses

- **Engineered rhizobacteria** (PivotBio, Joyn Bio) —
  N fixation in non-legumes.
- **Engineered mycorrhizae** — improve P uptake +
  drought tolerance in crops.
- **Synthetic microbiome consortia** — defined
  multi-strain inoculants.
- **Engineered endophytes** — confer pest /
  pathogen / drought resistance.

## Cell-free + minimal photosynthesis

- **Engineered chloroplasts in vitro** — light-driven
  ATP + NADPH production from isolated thylakoids.
- **Synthetic cell-free systems** with reconstituted
  Calvin or alternative carbon-fixation cycles.
- **Liposome-encapsulated photosynthetic mimics** —
  research-stage.

## Future directions

- **Whole-genome refactoring** of model plants
  (Sc 2.0-style minimal chromosome programmes
  for Arabidopsis or Brachypodium).
- **AI-designed promoters + regulatory elements** for
  precise transgene expression.
- **De novo enzyme design** (Baker lab tools
  applied to plant pathways).
- **Synthetic agriculture** — fully optimised
  plant-microbe-soil-environment systems.
- **Plant biocomputing + biosensing** — plants as
  living sensors for pollutants, pathogens, +
  environmental conditions.
- **CRISPR-edited de novo crops** from wild
  relatives in regions ill-served by current
  staples.
- **Carbon-negative agriculture** — designing crops
  + agricultural systems to net-sequester carbon.

## Regulatory + societal landscape

- **Process-based vs product-based regulation** —
  EU traditionally process-based (any GE / GMO
  scrutinised); US APHIS / FDA product-based
  (judge final product).
- **2024 EU NGT (New Genomic Techniques) framework**
  — proposal to deregulate certain
  cisgenic / edited crops; under negotiation.
- **CRISPR-edited products** treated as non-GMO
  in US, Argentina, Brazil, Australia, Japan
  (case-by-case in some).
- **Public perception + transparency** — labelling
  + consumer choice issues continue to evolve.
- **IP landscape** — patent pools (e.g. Public-
  Sector CRISPR licensing initiative), bioethics
  + benefit-sharing under Nagoya Protocol.
