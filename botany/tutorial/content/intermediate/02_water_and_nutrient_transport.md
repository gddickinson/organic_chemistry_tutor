# Plant water + nutrient transport

A 100-m redwood lifts water from soil to canopy
without a pump, against gravity, against friction,
against the cohesive force of millions of tons of
water column.  How?  Plant transport is one of
botany's most elegant topics — explained by a few
physical principles + sophisticated tissue
specialisations.

## The transpiration-cohesion-tension model

Water moves up xylem under tension (negative
pressure), driven by transpiration from leaves +
held together by water's cohesive H-bonding.

### Steps

1. **Stomatal transpiration** — water evaporates from
   mesophyll cell walls into substomatal air spaces +
   diffuses out through stomata.  Lost mass exceeds
   uptake by orders of magnitude.
2. **Tension generation** — evaporation creates
   negative pressure (~ -1 to -2 MPa typical;
   < -10 MPa in very dry conditions or tall trees).
3. **Tension transmission** — water column under
   tension stays connected by cohesive H-bonds;
   tension transmitted from leaf surface down through
   xylem to root.
4. **Bulk flow** — water + dissolved minerals move
   from soil through root → xylem → stem → leaves
   in continuous columns.
5. **Soil uptake** — roots absorb water down a
   water-potential gradient.

### Water potential (Ψ)

Plant water status is described by **water potential
(Ψ)**, measured in MPa, made up of:

Ψ = Ψs + Ψp + Ψg + Ψm

- Ψs — solute potential (always negative; more solute
  = more negative).
- Ψp — pressure potential (positive when turgid;
  negative in xylem under tension).
- Ψg — gravitational potential (~ +0.01 MPa / m
  upward).
- Ψm — matric potential (small in fully-hydrated
  systems).

Water moves from HIGHER Ψ to LOWER Ψ.

Typical values:
- Atmosphere (50 % RH at 25 °C): ~ -100 MPa.
- Leaf air space: ~ -10 to -20 MPa.
- Mesophyll cell wall: ~ -1.5 MPa.
- Xylem at leaf: ~ -0.8 MPa.
- Xylem at root: ~ -0.3 MPa.
- Root cell: ~ -0.5 MPa.
- Soil (well-watered): ~ -0.03 MPa.
- Soil (wilting point): ~ -1.5 MPa.

The huge gradient soil → leaf → atmosphere drives
flow.

### Cavitation + embolism

Tensions > ~ -2 MPa risk cavitation — air bubbles
form, breaking the water column → xylem embolism.
- Vessels (angiosperms) are wider but more vulnerable.
- Tracheids (gymnosperms) are narrower but safer.
- Plants have refilling mechanisms (root pressure
  + osmotic) for some embolisms.
- Drought-induced cavitation is a major cause of
  tree mortality in climate-stressed forests.

## Mineral nutrition

Plants need 17 essential elements:

### Macronutrients (gram-quantities / kg dry matter)

- **Nitrogen (N)** — proteins, nucleic acids,
  chlorophyll.  Often the most-limiting nutrient.
  Taken up as NO3- + NH4+.
- **Phosphorus (P)** — nucleic acids, ATP,
  phospholipids.  Taken up as H2PO4- / HPO42-.
  Often limiting in tropical / weathered soils.
- **Potassium (K)** — osmoticum, enzyme cofactor,
  stomatal guard cell turgor.
- **Calcium (Ca)** — cell-wall structure (Ca-pectate),
  signalling.
- **Magnesium (Mg)** — chlorophyll central atom,
  enzyme cofactor.
- **Sulphur (S)** — methionine + cysteine, glutathione,
  sulpholipid.

### Micronutrients

- **Iron (Fe)** — cytochromes, Fe-S clusters,
  chlorophyll synthesis (cofactor).
- **Manganese (Mn)** — OEC of PSII!
- **Zinc (Zn)** — many enzymes.
- **Copper (Cu)** — plastocyanin, lacasses.
- **Boron (B)** — cell wall (RG-II crosslinks),
  pollen-tube growth.
- **Molybdenum (Mo)** — nitrate reductase, nitrogenase
  in symbionts.
- **Nickel (Ni)** — urease.
- **Chlorine (Cl)** — OEC, K+ counterion.
- (Some plants need additional: Si in grasses;
  Na in C4 + halophytes; Co for symbiotic N
  fixation.)

### Deficiency symptoms

Each nutrient has characteristic deficiency
phenotypes used in agronomic diagnosis:
- **N** — uniform chlorosis (oldest leaves first;
  mobile element).
- **P** — purple anthocyanin accumulation; stunted.
- **K** — necrotic leaf-margin "burn".
- **Mg** — interveinal chlorosis on older leaves.
- **Fe** — interveinal chlorosis on YOUNGEST leaves
  (Fe is immobile).
- **Ca** — distorted growth, blossom-end rot in
  tomato.

## Root architecture + uptake

### Apoplast vs symplast

Two parallel pathways for water + ions through root
cortex:
- **Apoplast** — through cell walls + intercellular
  spaces (no membrane crossing).
- **Symplast** — through cytoplasm + plasmodesmata
  (one membrane crossing at root surface).

The **endodermis** has a Casparian strip — a band of
suberin around each cell that BLOCKS the apoplastic
pathway, forcing water + ions through plasma membranes
of endodermal cells → controlled selectivity at the
xylem entry.

### Active uptake

Roots actively concentrate ions against gradients
using membrane-potential-driven transport:
- H+-ATPase (P-type) pumps H+ out → membrane
  potential of -120 to -180 mV (steep gradient).
- Cation-H+ antiporters use this gradient.
- Anion-H+ symporters likewise.
- Different transporter families for different
  ions: KAT1 / AKT1 / KUP for K+, NRT1/2 for
  NO3-, PHT1 for phosphate, ZIP for Zn, IRT1 for
  Fe.

### Root hairs

Single epidermal-cell extensions; vastly amplify
absorptive surface area.  Lifespan days-weeks;
new root hairs grow continuously.

## Mycorrhizae

> 80 % of land plants form symbioses with
mycorrhizal fungi.  Two main types:

### Arbuscular mycorrhizae (AM)

- Glomeromycota fungi.
- Penetrate root cortical cells + form arbuscules
  (highly-branched intracellular exchange surfaces).
- Provide P + N + water; receive sugars from host.
- Most herbaceous plants + many woody plants form AM.

### Ectomycorrhizae (ECM)

- Basidiomycota + Ascomycota fungi.
- Form a sheath around root tips + a Hartig net
  between cortical cells (no intracellular
  penetration).
- Dominate temperate / boreal forest trees (oaks,
  pines, beeches).
- Mushroom-producing!

Networks of mycorrhizal hyphae (the "wood-wide web")
connect multiple plants + transfer carbon + nitrogen
+ defence signals between them.  Suzanne Simard's
work + Peter Wohlleben's popular books.

## Nitrogen fixation symbiosis

A small set of plants form symbioses with
nitrogen-fixing bacteria:

- **Legumes (Fabaceae)** + ***Rhizobium / Bradyrhizobium***
  — root nodules; nitrogenase enzyme reduces N2 →
  NH3.  Soybean, peanut, alfalfa, clover, peas,
  beans, chickpea, lentil, lupin.  Globally fixes
  ~ 50-70 Mt N / year.
- **Frankia** + **alder, sea buckthorn, casuarina**
  — non-legume actinorhizal symbiosis.
- **Cycads** + cyanobacteria.
- **Azolla water fern** + cyanobacteria — used in
  rice-paddy fertilisation.

Nodule development triggered by Nod-factor (LCO)
signal from rhizobia → host receptor kinases (NFR1 /
NFR5) → CCaMK kinase → DMI3 / IPD3 → nodule
organogenesis.

Engineering N-fixation into cereals (a holy grail
of agriculture) is being pursued via:
- Transferring nitrogenase + accessory genes to
  plant chloroplasts / mitochondria.
- Engineering associative N-fixers into cereal
  rhizosphere.
- Restoring CRE / common-symbiosis-pathway (CSP) genes
  in non-host plants.

## Phloem transport — Münch flow

Phloem moves photoassimilates (mostly sucrose) from
SOURCES (mature leaves) to SINKS (roots, fruits,
shoot apices, storage organs) at much higher
velocities (~ 1 m/h) than diffusion would allow.

### The pressure-flow (Münch) hypothesis

1. **Loading at source** — sucrose actively loaded
   into sieve elements at source → high solute
   concentration → low Ψs → water enters by osmosis
   from xylem → high Ψp.
2. **Bulk flow** — pressure gradient pushes sieve-
   tube content from source to sink.
3. **Unloading at sink** — sucrose actively or
   passively unloaded; water flows back to xylem.

Loading mechanisms:
- **Apoplastic** — sucrose actively loaded via
  SUT / SUC / SWEET transporters.  Most temperate
  herbs.
- **Symplastic** — sucrose moves via plasmodesmata;
  no membrane crossing.  Cucurbits, many woody
  plants.
- **Polymer trap** — symplastic loading + raffinose-
  family-oligosaccharide (RFO) synthesis traps
  oligosaccharides too large to diffuse back.

### Sources + sinks

- **Source** — net photoassimilate exporter;
  typically mature leaves.
- **Sink** — net importer; roots, fruits, growing
  shoots, tubers, seeds.

A leaf can switch from sink to source as it matures.
Source-sink relations dictate yield + harvest index
+ are tunable in crop breeding.

### Phloem-mobile signals

Beyond sugars, the phloem also transports:
- Amino acids + organic-N forms.
- Hormones (cytokinins from root → shoot, JA + SA
  systemic signals).
- Macromolecules + RNAs (FT florigen mRNA + protein,
  microRNAs).
- Viruses (most plant viruses systemic via phloem).

## Stomatal regulation — putting it together

Stomata balance CO2 uptake (photosynthesis) against
water loss (transpiration).  Guard cell turgor is
controlled by:

- **Light (blue + red)** — phototropin → PHOT1/2
  + cryptochrome → H+-ATPase activation → K+ +
  Cl- + malate accumulation → osmotic water uptake
  → open.
- **CO2 status** — low intercellular CO2 → open.
- **ABA** — drought stress → ABA → SLAC1 anion
  channel activation → K+ efflux → stomatal closure.
- **Humidity (VPD)** — high VPD → close.
- **Circadian + clock-driven** rhythms — stomata
  oscillate with predictable diurnal patterns.
- **Pathogen perception** — bacterial flagellin →
  FLS2 → close (innate immunity).

Engineering improved stomatal responsiveness
(faster opening on cloud removal, faster closing on
drought) is a quantitative target for crop
improvement.
