# Plant defence

Plants don't have circulating immune cells or
adaptive immunity in the vertebrate sense.  What
they have instead is a sophisticated combination of
constitutive structural + chemical defences + a
multi-layered induced immune system that recognises
+ responds to attackers via a network of cell-
autonomous mechanisms.

## Constitutive defences

These are present + ready before any attack.

### Structural

- **Cuticle + waxes** — first hydrophobic barrier
  on every aerial surface.
- **Cell wall** — physical barrier; lignified
  walls especially tough.
- **Trichomes** — non-glandular (mechanical
  deterrent + microclimate); glandular (secrete
  defensive compounds — *Cannabis* trichomes secrete
  THC + CBD; mint trichomes secrete menthol +
  pulegone).
- **Thorns + spines + prickles** — large
  morphological deterrents.
- **Bark + periderm** — barrier against insects +
  fungi + abiotic damage.

### Chemical (secondary metabolites)

Plants synthesise an enormous diversity of secondary
metabolites for defence.  Three major classes
(deeper coverage in the **BT-3.0 advanced "Plant
secondary metabolism" lesson**):

- **Alkaloids** (~ 12 000 known) — N-containing;
  bitter, often bioactive at low doses.  Caffeine,
  nicotine, morphine, atropine, strychnine,
  capsaicin (technically a vanilloid), cocaine,
  vinblastine, quinine.
- **Terpenoids** (~ 30 000+) — isoprene-derived;
  monoterpenes (essential oils — limonene, menthol),
  sesquiterpenes (artemisinin, parthenolide),
  diterpenes (taxol, gibberellin biosynthesis),
  triterpenes (saponins, phytosterols), tetra-
  terpenes (carotenoids).
- **Phenylpropanoids + flavonoids** (~ 10 000+) —
  shikimate-pathway-derived; lignin, anthocyanins,
  tannins, isoflavones, coumarins, salicylic acid.

Most plants are biochemical individuals — each
species has its own unique secondary-metabolite
profile.  These compounds were the lead source for
the entire pre-modern pharmacopoeia + remain a major
inspiration for drug design.

### Glucosides + bound precursors

Some defensive compounds are stored as inactive
glucosides + activated only on tissue damage when
released enzymes hydrolyse them.

- **Cyanogenic glycosides** (linamarin in cassava,
  amygdalin in bitter almond) → HCN released on
  herbivory.
- **Glucosinolates** (Brassicaceae) → released by
  myrosinase on damage → isothiocyanates (mustard
  oils, wasabi pungency, broccoli sulforaphane).
- **Iridoid glycosides + alliin** (garlic / onion
  → allicin via alliinase).

The "mustard oil bomb" + "thiosulphinate bomb" both
exploit the spatial separation of substrate (in
vacuole) + enzyme (in another compartment) — broken
on tissue damage.

## Induced defences — pathogen-triggered immunity

Plants recognise pathogens via two-tier surveillance:

### PAMP-triggered immunity (PTI)

- **PAMPs** (pathogen-associated molecular patterns)
  / **MAMPs** (microbe-associated) — conserved
  microbial molecules:
  - Bacterial **flg22** (flagellin epitope), **elf18**
    (EF-Tu epitope), **LPS**, **peptidoglycan**.
  - Fungal **chitin** + chitin oligomers.
  - Oomycete + fungal **β-glucans**.
- **PRRs** (pattern-recognition receptors) on plant
  cell surface:
  - **FLS2** (LRR-RLK) — flg22.
  - **EFR** — elf18.
  - **CERK1 + LYK4 / LYK5** — chitin.
  - **LORE** — LPS.

Recognition triggers:
1. Ca²⁺ influx + ROS burst (RBOH NADPH oxidase).
2. MAPK cascades.
3. Transcriptional reprogramming.
4. Callose deposition (1,3-β-glucan reinforcement of
   walls at infection site).
5. Stomatal closure (limit pathogen entry via
   stomata).

PTI provides BROAD-SPECTRUM basal immunity but is
relatively low-amplitude.

### Effector-triggered susceptibility (ETS)

Adapted pathogens deliver **effector proteins** that
suppress PTI:
- Bacterial type-III secretion delivers ~ 10-30
  effectors per *Pseudomonas* / *Xanthomonas*
  species.
- Oomycete + fungal effectors translocate via
  haustoria.
- Effectors target PTI signalling (MAPKs, transcription
  factors, ubiquitin-proteasome system, hormone
  pathways).

### Effector-triggered immunity (ETI)

Plants counter effectors with **R proteins** —
intracellular **NLR receptors** (NB-LRR proteins;
homologous to animal NOD-like receptors) that
recognise specific effectors directly OR indirectly
via guarded host targets.

Recognition triggers a more aggressive response:
- **Hypersensitive response (HR)** — programmed cell
  death at infection site → contains pathogen by
  removing its niche (effective vs biotrophs;
  counter-productive vs necrotrophs).
- Stronger transcriptional reprogramming, defence
  hormone (especially SA) bursts.

The **gene-for-gene** model (Flor 1955): pathogen
*Avr* (avirulence) gene product matches plant *R*
gene → resistance.  R-gene breeding is a cornerstone
of crop disease management — but pathogen evolution
defeats single-R-gene resistance routinely (the
"R-gene treadmill").

NLR diversity is enormous — *Arabidopsis* has ~ 165
NLR genes; *Aegilops tauschii* has > 1 600.

### Systemic acquired resistance (SAR)

Local infection triggers DURABLE, BROAD-SPECTRUM
resistance throughout the plant.

- SA signal mobilises systemically (azelaic acid +
  G3P + pipecolic acid + N-hydroxypipecolic acid +
  glycerol-3-phosphate are mobile signals; SA itself
  partly).
- NPR1 master regulator activates SAR-responsive
  PR-protein expression.
- Resistance lasts weeks-months + protects against
  diverse pathogens.

### Induced systemic resistance (ISR)

Triggered by beneficial root microbes (PGPR, PGPF) —
JA / ethylene-dependent, NPR1-dependent, but no
direct PR expression — primes systemic responses
for faster activation on subsequent attack.

## Anti-herbivore defences

### Direct

- Mechanical (trichomes, spines, sclerophyll).
- Chemical (alkaloids, terpenes, tannins,
  protease inhibitors, lectins, cyanogenic
  glycosides).

### Indirect

Plant attacks attract THIRD-PARTY enemies of
herbivores:
- **Volatile organic compounds (VOCs)** released
  on caterpillar feeding (cis-jasmone, methyl
  jasmonate, herbivore-induced terpenes) attract
  parasitoid wasps + predatory mites.
- **Extrafloral nectaries** + **food bodies**
  reward ant-defenders that patrol the plant
  (acacia / Mutualism cycles).
- Domatia (specialised hollows) house ant
  defenders.

### Tritrophic interactions

Ian Baldwin + others showed that wild tobacco
(*Nicotiana attenuata*) released attacks
recruit predator + parasitoid populations that
reduce caterpillar load — measurable fitness
benefit.

### Priming

Prior exposure to mild attack / chemical mimics
PRIMES the plant for faster + stronger response on
subsequent attack — without the cost of constitutive
high defence.

## Defence against necrotrophs vs biotrophs

- **Biotrophs** (live in living tissue — *Pseudomonas
  syringae*, powdery mildews, rusts) — controlled
  by SA + HR + NLR-mediated cell death.
- **Necrotrophs** (kill + consume — *Botrytis
  cinerea*, *Sclerotinia*, many *Fusarium*) — HR
  HELPS necrotrophs (free dead-tissue substrate);
  controlled by JA / ethylene + tannins +
  saponins + glucosinolates.

The SA / JA antagonism is a major signalling axis —
trade-offs between biotroph + necrotroph defence
are inherent.

## Plant viruses + RNA silencing

Plants lack vertebrate-style antibody / T-cell
adaptive immunity but they have **RNA silencing**
(RNA interference) as antiviral defence:

- Viral dsRNA → DCL2 / DCL4 (DICER-like) → 21-22 nt
  siRNAs.
- siRNAs loaded into AGO (Argonaute) complex →
  guide cleavage of viral RNA.
- Systemic spread of silencing signal protects
  cells ahead of infection.
- Many viruses encode **viral suppressors of RNA
  silencing (VSRs)** to counter — coevolutionary
  arms race.

## Hyperaccumulators + heavy-metal defence

Some plants tolerate + concentrate toxic metals
(Ni, Zn, Cd, As) in shoots — a defence against
herbivores + pathogens that can't tolerate the
metal load.  Can be exploited for **phytoremediation**
of contaminated sites.

## Crop disease management

Integrated practices:
- **Resistant cultivars** — gene stacking + R-gene
  pyramiding.
- **Crop rotation** + sanitation + clean seed +
  certified disease-free planting material.
- **Cultural** — spacing, irrigation, harvest
  timing, residue management.
- **Biological control** — predator + parasitoid +
  microbial antagonists (*Bacillus thuringiensis* +
  *Trichoderma*; covered in the BT-3.0 advanced
  biotech lesson).
- **Chemical** — fungicides + bactericides
  (limited classes), insecticides + acaricides.
- **Modern molecular** — RNAi-based crop sprays
  (e.g. Greenlight Biosciences); CRISPR-edited
  resistance genes.

Pesticide resistance evolution is a major +
worsening problem mirroring antibiotic resistance.
