# Routes of administration

How a drug enters the body decisively shapes how
quickly it acts, how much reaches its target, and how
its side-effect profile looks.  The route is a
clinical-pharmacology choice every bit as deliberate as
the molecule itself.

## The major routes

### Oral (PO — *per os*)

The most common route — convenient, painless,
self-administered.  But the drug must:

1. Survive gastric acid + intestinal proteases.
2. Cross the intestinal epithelium (paracellular for
   small hydrophilic molecules; transcellular for
   most others).
3. Survive **first-pass metabolism** in the gut wall +
   liver.

Onset typically 30-60 min; peak concentration 1-3 h.

### Intravenous (IV)

Direct bolus or infusion into the blood.  100 %
bioavailability by definition (the dose IS what reaches
the systemic circulation).  Onset ~ seconds; peak at
end of infusion.

Used when:
- Oral not feasible (unconscious, vomiting, NPO).
- Rapid onset needed (resuscitation, anaesthesia,
  emergencies).
- Drug has poor oral bioavailability (vancomycin,
  many biologics).
- Precise titration needed (anaesthesia, vasopressors,
  insulin in DKA).

Bolus pushes peak concentrations very high → safety
risks (vancomycin "red man syndrome", aminoglycoside
neurotoxicity, opioid-induced apnoea).  Infusions are
safer but more clinically demanding.

### Intramuscular (IM)

Injection into a muscle (deltoid, vastus lateralis,
gluteus).  Onset 10-30 min; sustained absorption from
the depot.

Useful for:
- Long-acting depot preparations (depot antipsychotics,
  testosterone enanthate, medroxyprogesterone).
- Vaccines (most adult vaccines are IM).
- Drugs requiring sustained release without IV access.

Avoid in patients on anticoagulants (haematoma risk).

### Subcutaneous (SC / SQ)

Into the fatty layer beneath the skin.  Slower
absorption than IM (poorly perfused), more sustained.

Used for:
- Insulin (the workhorse SC drug).
- Heparin / LMWH (enoxaparin).
- GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide weekly, liraglutide
  daily).
- Many monoclonal antibodies (adalimumab,
  pembrolizumab SC formulations, denosumab).
- Granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (filgrastim).

Patient self-administration is feasible; pen / autoinjector
devices are now standard.

### Sublingual / buccal

Drug placed under the tongue or against the cheek,
absorbed across the oral mucosa.  Bypasses first-pass
metabolism — liver-vulnerable drugs gain bioavailability.

Examples:
- Glyceryl trinitrate (GTN) for acute angina — onset
  1-3 min.
- Buprenorphine SL for opioid-use disorder.
- Lorazepam SL.
- Asenapine SL (antipsychotic).

### Inhaled

Drug delivered to the lungs as aerosol (MDI, DPI,
nebuliser).  Vast surface area (~ 70 m² in adults),
rich blood supply, no first-pass.  Either:
- **Local action** — β2-agonists, ICS, LAMA for asthma /
  COPD.  Drug acts on airway tissue directly; systemic
  absorption is a side-effect, not the goal.
- **Systemic action via lung** — general anaesthetics
  (sevoflurane, isoflurane), vapours (cannabis, nicotine
  via vaping), inhaled insulin (Afrezza — niche).

### Topical

Applied to skin or mucosa for local action.
Bioavailability typically low.  Examples:
- Steroid creams for eczema, psoriasis.
- Topical antibiotics, antifungals.
- Local anaesthetics (EMLA).
- NSAIDs (diclofenac gel).

Be alert to systemic absorption with potent agents —
topical clobetasol can suppress HPA-axis if used over
large areas.

### Transdermal

Drug formulated in a patch + crosses skin into
systemic circulation for sustained delivery.

Examples:
- Fentanyl patches (chronic pain).
- Nicotine patches (smoking cessation).
- HRT (estradiol).
- Glyceryl trinitrate.
- Scopolamine (motion sickness).
- Buprenorphine.

Steady plasma levels for 1-7 days; convenient + avoids
first-pass.  But onset is slow (hours) + reservoir
remains for hours after patch removal.

### Rectal

Suppository or enema.  Useful when oral not feasible
(vomiting, paediatric, pre-anaesthetic).  Variable
absorption — first-pass partially bypassed (lower
rectal veins drain to inferior vena cava, but mid +
upper rectal drain to portal vein).

Examples:
- Paracetamol suppository (paediatric fever / NPO).
- Diazepam rectal (status epilepticus).
- Mesalazine (ulcerative colitis distal disease).
- 5-HT3 antagonists (anti-emetics).

### Intrathecal / epidural

Direct injection into CSF (intrathecal) or epidural
space.  Bypasses the blood-brain barrier — small dose
gives big CNS effect.

Examples:
- Spinal / epidural anaesthesia (bupivacaine).
- Intrathecal baclofen (spasticity).
- Intrathecal morphine (chronic pain).
- Methotrexate (CNS leukaemia prophylaxis).
- Nusinersen (spinal muscular atrophy ASO drug).

### Intra-articular, intra-vitreal, intracerebral

Direct injection into a joint, eye, or brain.
- IA steroids for arthritis flares.
- Intra-vitreal anti-VEGF (ranibizumab, aflibercept,
  faricimab) for AMD / diabetic macular oedema.
- ICV / parenchymal AAV gene therapy (Luxturna,
  Zolgensma).

## Bioavailability (F)

The fraction of an administered dose that reaches the
systemic circulation in unchanged form.

F = (AUC_route / AUC_IV) × (Dose_IV / Dose_route)

By definition F = 1.0 for IV.  For other routes:

| Drug | Route | F |
|------|-------|---|
| Morphine | PO | 0.25 |
| Propranolol | PO | 0.25 |
| Verapamil | PO | 0.20 |
| Levodopa | PO (alone) | 0.05 (~1.0 with carbidopa) |
| Lidocaine | PO | 0.03 (so why we use IV / topical) |
| Diazepam | PO | 0.99 |
| Phenobarbital | PO | 1.00 |
| Insulin | SC | 0.55-0.95 (formulation-dependent) |
| Erythropoietin | SC | 0.20 |

Low F often reflects extensive first-pass metabolism;
high F suggests minimal hepatic extraction.

## First-pass metabolism

Oral drugs absorbed into mesenteric venous blood drain
into the **portal vein** before entering the systemic
circulation.  The liver gets first crack — and for some
drugs that means most of the dose disappears before
reaching the target.

Heavily first-pass-metabolised drugs include:
- **Lidocaine** — F = 3 % PO; use topical or IV.
- **Propranolol** — F = 25 %; need ~ 4× higher PO dose
  vs IV.
- **Morphine** — F = 25 %; use bigger PO doses or
  switch to oxycodone (F = 60 %).
- **Verapamil** — F = 20 %.
- **GTN** — F essentially 0 PO; sublingual or
  transdermal.

Genetic + clinical variation in CYPs (CYP3A4 inducers /
inhibitors, liver disease, age) modulates first-pass
extraction substantially.

## The blood-brain barrier (BBB)

A specialised feature of distribution — capillaries
in the brain have tight junctions + a thick basement
membrane + active efflux pumps (P-gp, BCRP).  Most
drugs are excluded.

To get through, a drug typically needs:
- Small (< 500 Da).
- Lipophilic (logP roughly 1-3).
- Not a P-gp / BCRP substrate.
- Or a transporter (LAT1 for amino-acid analogues like
  L-DOPA + gabapentin + baclofen).
- Or active uptake systems (glucose transporter for
  some sugar conjugates).

CNS-targeted drugs are designed to penetrate; CNS
toxicity from peripheral drugs reflects BBB leak in
disease (sepsis, meningitis, MS).  Loperamide is the
canonical "doesn't reach the brain" drug — it's a μ-
opioid agonist but P-gp pumps it back across the BBB,
so no euphoria, no respiratory depression.
