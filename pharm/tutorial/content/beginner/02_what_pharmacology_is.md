# What pharmacology is

Pharmacology is the science of how chemicals interact
with biological systems — how a drug enters the body,
finds its target, alters function, and is cleared.  It
sits at the intersection of organic chemistry,
biochemistry, physiology, and clinical medicine.

## Drugs vs poisons — Paracelsus

> **"All things are poison + nothing is without poison;
> only the dose makes a thing not a poison."**
> — Paracelsus, ~ 1538

This 500-year-old aphorism remains the foundation of
modern pharmacology + toxicology.  Aspirin at 325 mg is
analgesic; at 30 g it can kill.  Botulinum toxin at
nanograms paralyses; at micrograms diluted to femtomolar
it smooths wrinkles.  The drug-vs-poison distinction is
not chemical — it is dose, route, indication, + patient.

## What pharmacologists actually study

Two complementary axes:

- **Pharmacokinetics (PK)** — what the BODY does to the
  DRUG.  Absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion
  (ADME).  Concentration vs time profiles.
- **Pharmacodynamics (PD)** — what the DRUG does to the
  BODY.  Receptor binding, downstream cellular effects,
  whole-organism efficacy + toxicity.

A drug development programme reduces, in principle, to
finding a molecule whose PK delivers an adequate
concentration at the target tissue + whose PD produces
the desired clinical effect with acceptable safety.

## The receptor concept

The receptor concept — a specific cellular component
that a drug binds to + thereby triggers an effect — was
formulated by **John Newport Langley** (1878) +
**Paul Ehrlich** (1880s-1900s).  Ehrlich's
"corpora non agunt nisi fixata" ("agents do not act
unless bound") defined the entire experimental
programme of 20th-century pharmacology.

Modern pharmacology recognises ~ 1 500 distinct human
drug targets across multiple superfamilies:

- **G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs)** — ~ 800 in
  humans; ~ 35 % of marketed drugs target a GPCR.
- **Nuclear hormone receptors (NHRs)** — ~ 48 in humans;
  steroids, thyroid hormone, vitamin D, retinoids.
- **Receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs)** — ~ 58 in humans;
  EGFR, HER2, VEGFR, insulin R, etc.
- **Ligand-gated ion channels** — nicotinic AChR, GABA_A,
  glycine, NMDA, AMPA, kainate, 5-HT3.
- **Voltage-gated ion channels** — Nav, Cav, Kv, HCN.
- **Transporters** — SLC + ABC superfamilies; SSRIs
  (SERT), antidepressants (NET / DAT), gliflozins
  (SGLT2).
- **Enzymes** — statins (HMG-CoA reductase), ACE
  inhibitors, kinase inhibitors, COX inhibitors,
  monoamine oxidase inhibitors, integrase / protease
  inhibitors.
- **Structural / non-canonical targets** — tubulin
  (taxanes, vinca), DNA (alkylators, intercalators),
  ribosome (most antibiotics), folate metabolism (anti-
  folates), nucleic acid synthesis (anti-virals).

## Where modern drugs come from

The 20th-century model was natural-product-driven:
penicillin from *Penicillium*, taxol from Pacific yew,
artemisinin from *Artemisia annua*, statins from
*Penicillium citrinum*.  The 21st century adds:

- **Synthetic medicinal chemistry** — combinatorial,
  fragment-based, structure-based design.
- **Biologics** — monoclonal antibodies, fusion
  proteins, antibody-drug conjugates.
- **Nucleic-acid drugs** — antisense oligonucleotides,
  siRNAs, mRNA vaccines, CRISPR therapeutics.
- **Cell + gene therapies** — CAR-T cells, AAV gene
  therapy, base editing.
- **Protein-degrader modalities** — PROTACs, molecular
  glues.

## Discovery + development

A typical modern drug-development pipeline:

1. **Target identification + validation** — biology /
   genetics / disease association → druggable target.
2. **Hit identification** — high-throughput screening
   (HTS), DNA-encoded libraries (DEL), virtual
   screening.
3. **Hit-to-lead** — optimise potency, selectivity,
   solubility, ADME.
4. **Lead optimisation** — multi-parameter optimisation
   for clinical-candidate quality (CCQ).
5. **Preclinical** — IND-enabling tox + PK + safety
   pharm.
6. **Phase 1** — safety + PK in healthy volunteers (or
   patients for oncology).
7. **Phase 2** — efficacy + dose-finding in patients.
8. **Phase 3** — large pivotal RCTs vs standard of
   care.
9. **Regulatory submission** — NDA / BLA / MAA.
10. **Phase 4** — post-marketing surveillance.

Median timeline 12-15 years; capitalised cost > $2 B;
attrition ≈ 90 % from Phase 1.

## Why pharmacology matters

- Every clinical decision involves an implicit PK / PD
  judgement.
- Adverse drug events kill ~ 100 000 hospital patients /
  year in the US alone.
- Drug discovery + development is the largest single
  R&D spend in the global economy (~ $250 B / year).
- The next 25 years will see precision medicine,
  pharmacogenomics, AI-driven design, + new modalities
  reshape what drugs ARE.
