# Drug names + classes

A single medicine can carry four or more names — and
clinicians, regulators, chemists, marketers, and
patients each prefer different ones.  Knowing how the
naming conventions work is the first step to navigating
prescribing references + the literature.

## The four names of a drug

Take a familiar example: **paracetamol / acetaminophen /
Tylenol**.

1. **Chemical name** — full IUPAC.  *N*-(4-hydroxy-
   phenyl)acetamide.  Used in chemistry papers + patent
   filings.  Unique + unambiguous, but unwieldy.
2. **International Non-proprietary Name (INN)** —
   assigned by WHO; intended to be globally unique +
   pronounceable.  *Paracetamol* is the INN; the US
   adopted a different generic (USAN) — *acetaminophen*.
3. **United States Adopted Name (USAN)** — historically
   different from the INN for ~ 200 drugs (paracetamol /
   acetaminophen, salbutamol / albuterol, frusemide /
   furosemide).  USAN-INN harmonisation has reduced the
   gap since the 1990s.
4. **Brand / proprietary name** — the trademark
   chosen by the marketing authorisation holder.
   Tylenol (Johnson & Johnson), Panadol (GSK), Calpol
   (J&J UK), Doliprane (Sanofi France).  Multiple
   brands per molecule; brands change ownership +
   geography routinely.

## Stem rules — why drugs in a class sound alike

The WHO INN program assigns a **stem** to each
mechanistic / chemical drug class so newly-named drugs
within a class share a recognisable suffix or prefix:

- **-mab** — monoclonal antibody (rituximab, adalimumab,
  pembrolizumab).  Sub-stems indicate origin: -ximab
  chimeric, -zumab humanised, -umab fully human.
- **-ciclib** — CDK4/6 inhibitor (palbociclib,
  ribociclib, abemaciclib).
- **-tinib** — kinase inhibitor (imatinib, erlotinib,
  ibrutinib, dasatinib).  Sometimes paired with sub-
  class hints: -fenib for Raf, -nitinib / -lanib for
  receptor tyrosine kinase.
- **-prazole** — proton-pump inhibitor (omeprazole,
  pantoprazole, esomeprazole).
- **-sartan** — angiotensin-II AT1 receptor antagonist
  (losartan, valsartan, irbesartan).
- **-pril** — ACE inhibitor (captopril, enalapril,
  lisinopril, ramipril).
- **-statin** — HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor
  (simvastatin, atorvastatin, rosuvastatin, etc.).
- **-vir** — antiviral (acyclovir, oseltamivir,
  remdesivir, paxlovid's nirmatrelvir).
- **-floxacin** — fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin,
  levofloxacin, moxifloxacin).
- **-cycline** — tetracycline-class (doxycycline,
  minocycline, tigecycline).
- **-cillin** — penicillin-class (amoxicillin,
  flucloxacillin, piperacillin).
- **-cef-/-ceph-** — cephalosporin (cefazolin,
  cefuroxime, ceftriaxone, cefepime).
- **-mycin** — macrolide / aminoglycoside (erythromycin,
  azithromycin, gentamicin, vancomycin — careful, the
  stem is broader than one class).
- **-olol** — β-adrenergic blocker (propranolol,
  atenolol, metoprolol, carvedilol).
- **-azosin** — α1-adrenergic antagonist (doxazosin,
  prazosin, terazosin, tamsulosin).
- **-prost** — prostaglandin analogue (latanoprost,
  bimatoprost, misoprostol).
- **-glitazone** — PPARγ agonist (rosiglitazone,
  pioglitazone).
- **-gliflozin** — SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin,
  empagliflozin, canagliflozin).
- **-gliptin** — DPP-4 inhibitor (sitagliptin,
  saxagliptin, linagliptin).
- **-glutide / -tide** — peptide / GLP-1 (semaglutide,
  liraglutide, dulaglutide, tirzepatide).

A new drug name's stem reveals its likely target +
class without having to look anything up.

## ATC classification

The WHO **Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical (ATC)** code
hierarchically classifies every approved drug:

```
Level 1 — anatomical main group         (1 letter)
Level 2 — therapeutic main group        (2 digits)
Level 3 — therapeutic / pharmacological (1 letter)
Level 4 — chemical / pharmacological    (1 letter)
Level 5 — chemical substance            (2 digits)
```

Example — **atorvastatin** is `C10AA05`:

- **C** — cardiovascular system.
- **C10** — lipid-modifying agents.
- **C10A** — lipid-modifying agents, plain.
- **C10AA** — HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors.
- **C10AA05** — atorvastatin (specific substance).

ATC codes power Defined Daily Dose (DDD) statistics +
international drug-utilisation comparisons + adverse
event coding.

## Chemical / functional-group classifications

Drugs are also grouped by their dominant chemical
features:

- **Acids** — ionise to anion at physiological pH.
  NSAIDs (aspirin, ibuprofen, diclofenac), penicillins,
  furosemide, valproate.  Bind to plasma albumin, are
  tubular-secreted.
- **Bases** — ionise to cation at physiological pH.
  β-blockers, opioids, antidepressants, antipsychotics,
  most amine drugs.  Bind α1-acid glycoprotein, are
  tubular-secreted in the OCT pathway.
- **Neutrals** — non-ionisable.  Steroids, digoxin,
  benzodiazepines.
- **Esters** — pro-drugs hydrolysed by serum esterases
  (cocaine, procaine, oseltamivir, capecitabine).
  Generally short half-life unless deliberately
  designed for extended action.
- **Amides** — peptide-bond analogues; resistant to
  serum esterases (lidocaine vs procaine — the C=O is
  amide not ester, t1/2 ~ 90 min vs ~ 1 min).

## Generic vs brand vs biosimilar

- **Generic** — bioequivalent (90 % CI of AUC + Cmax
  within 80-125 % of innovator) chemical copy after
  patent expiry.  Same active ingredient,
  pharmacologically interchangeable.
- **Brand / innovator** — original FDA-approved
  product.  Higher price, identical efficacy after
  patent runs out.
- **Biosimilar** — biologic-drug analogue (NOT a
  generic — biologics are too complex for exact
  reproduction).  EMA + FDA require head-to-head
  clinical comparability.  *Filgrastim → tbo-filgrastim
  → biosimilar filgrastims*.
- **Branded generic** — a generic sold under a brand
  name to differentiate.  Common in India / emerging
  markets.
