# Pharmacogenomics

Pharmacogenomics (PGx) studies how genetic variation
affects drug response — efficacy, dosing, toxicity.
After two decades of academic promise, PGx has reached
clinical adoption: > 20 FDA labels with genetic-
testing recommendations, validated CPIC guidelines for
> 100 drug-gene pairs, and growing pre-emptive testing
programmes.

## The promise + the pragma

The promise — "the right drug, at the right dose, for
the right patient" — depends on knowing genotype + a
clinically-actionable rule.  Most variability in drug
response IS pharmacogenomic (~ 30-50 % of inter-
patient variability for major CYP-substrate drugs).

The pragma — only a fraction of clinically-relevant
genes are routinely tested, and clinician + patient +
payer inertia + alert fatigue dampen real-world impact.

## The big-five PGx genes

Five genes account for the majority of clinically-
actionable PGx:

### CYP2D6

- ~ 25 % of clinically-used drugs metabolised by
  CYP2D6.
- > 130 alleles identified.
- **Phenotypes**: poor metaboliser (PM, ~ 7 % of
  Europeans; ~ 1 % of East Asians), intermediate (IM),
  extensive (EM, "wild type"), ultra-rapid (UM, ~ 1-3
  % Europeans, up to 30 % in Ethiopian populations).
- **Drug consequences**:
  - **Codeine** → activates to morphine via CYP2D6.
    PMs get no analgesia; UMs risk morphine overdose
    (case reports of UM neonates dying after maternal
    codeine).  FDA contraindication in children +
    UM mothers.
  - **Tramadol** — same activation; same caveat.
  - **Tamoxifen** → endoxifen via CYP2D6.  PMs lose
    breast-cancer benefit (data + clinical advice
    debated; CYP2D6 inhibitors like paroxetine
    contraindicated).
  - **Atomoxetine** — PMs higher AUC; modify dose.
  - **TCAs** + **SSRIs** + **antipsychotics** — PMs
    higher concentrations + more side effects; UMs
    lower concentrations + reduced efficacy.
  - **Metoprolol / carvedilol** — PMs higher exposure;
    intolerance risk.

### CYP2C9 + VKORC1 (warfarin)

- Warfarin is the most-studied PGx drug.
- **CYP2C9*2 / *3** — reduced warfarin metabolism,
  bleeding risk on standard dose.
- **VKORC1 -1639G>A** — reduced warfarin target
  expression, hypersensitive to warfarin.
- **CYP4F2** — warfarin-dose-modifying minor effect.

CPIC dosing algorithm combines CYP2C9 + VKORC1 +
clinical factors (age, weight, INR target).  Reduces
out-of-range INRs but the COAG / EU-PACT trials
showed mixed effect on hard outcomes (bleeding,
clotting); the rise of DOACs has reduced PGx-guided
warfarin's clinical relevance.

### CYP2C19 (clopidogrel)

- **Clopidogrel** is a pro-drug; CYP2C19 activates it
  to its active metabolite.
- **CYP2C19*2 / *3** — PMs can't activate clopidogrel
  → in-stent thrombosis risk after PCI.
- ~ 30 % of East Asians + 15 % of Europeans + 4 % of
  Africans are *2 or *3 carriers.
- FDA black-box warning since 2010.
- **Clinical action**: switch PMs to ticagrelor (no
  PGx dependence) or prasugrel (CYP3A4-activated, less
  CYP2C19-dependent).
- TAILOR-PCI trial 2020 showed PGx-guided regimens
  trended toward fewer ischaemic events + bleeding
  but didn't reach the pre-specified hard endpoint.

CYP2C19 also affects:
- **PPIs** (omeprazole, lansoprazole, pantoprazole) —
  PMs higher exposure + better acid suppression.
- **Voriconazole** — PMs higher exposure + neuro tox.
- **Citalopram + escitalopram** — PMs need lower dose.

### TPMT + NUDT15 (thiopurines)

- Azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine, thioguanine activate
  via TPMT-mediated thiomethylation balanced against
  TGN incorporation into DNA.
- **TPMT *2 / *3A / *3C** PMs (~ 0.3 %) accumulate
  toxic 6-TGN → severe myelosuppression.
- TPMT testing is now standard of care before
  azathioprine / 6-MP / thioguanine.
- **NUDT15 R139C** is more important than TPMT in
  Asian populations + adds independent risk.
- CPIC + FDA labels recommend pre-treatment testing.

### DPYD (5-FU + capecitabine)

- DPD enzyme catabolises 5-FU; deficiency → severe /
  lethal 5-FU toxicity.
- Four common variants (*2A, *13, c.2846A>T,
  c.1129-5923C>G) cover ~ 50-60 % of severe-toxicity
  cases.
- ~ 7 % of Europeans carry one + ~ 0.2 % are PMs.
- Pre-emptive genotyping reduces severe toxicity by
  ~ 50 % in a Dutch nationwide programme.
- CPIC recommends 50 % dose reduction for IMs +
  alternative therapy for PMs.
- EMA recommends DPYD testing for ALL patients before
  5-FU / capecitabine.

## HLA-mediated hypersensitivity

A pharmacogenomic mechanism distinct from drug
metabolism:

- **HLA-B*57:01 + abacavir** — abacavir hyper-
  sensitivity syndrome (HSS) in 5-8 % of treated
  patients without screening; ~ 100 % avoidable with
  pre-treatment HLA testing.  PREDICT-1 trial 2008
  established the standard.  FDA black-box +
  contraindication if positive.
- **HLA-B*15:02 + carbamazepine** — Stevens-Johnson
  syndrome / TEN in Han Chinese, Thai, Filipino +
  Malay populations.  FDA recommends genotyping
  before starting in these populations.
- **HLA-A*31:01 + carbamazepine** — milder
  hypersensitivity, broader populations.
- **HLA-B*58:01 + allopurinol** — SJS / TEN /
  hypersensitivity syndrome.  Recommended in Asian +
  African populations.
- **HLA-DRB1*15:01 + clozapine + agranulocytosis** —
  emerging.
- **HLA-B*44:03 + phenytoin** — emerging.

## Other clinically-actionable genes

- **SLCO1B1*5 + simvastatin** — myopathy + rhabdo-
  myolysis.  FDA label.  Switch to rosuvastatin or
  pravastatin (less SLCO1B1-dependent).
- **G6PD deficiency + primaquine, dapsone, rasburicase**
  — haemolysis.  Mandatory screening before
  oxidant-triggering drugs.
- **UGT1A1*28 + irinotecan** — neutropenia +
  diarrhoea (active metabolite SN-38 glucuronidation
  reduced).
- **CYP3A5 expressers + tacrolimus** — need higher
  dose to achieve target trough.
- **IFNL3 / IL28B + interferon for HCV** — predicts
  response (now mostly historical with DAAs).
- **MTHFR + methotrexate** — minor; clinical utility
  debated.
- **HLA-B*57:01 + flucloxacillin** — drug-induced
  liver injury (DILI).
- **ITPA + ribavirin / azathioprine** — anaemia /
  neutropenia.

## How PGx data is generated

- **Genotyping arrays** — Illumina, Affymetrix; cheap
  ($30-100), turnaround 1-3 days.
- **Whole-exome / whole-genome sequencing** —
  comprehensive but expensive.
- **Targeted panels** — PGx-focused (VeriDose, AmpliSeq
  Pharmacogenomics Panel) cover the core CYPs +
  HLA-B*57:01 / *15:02 + VKORC1 + TPMT + DPYD + UGT1A1
  + SLCO1B1.
- **Star (*) allele nomenclature** — PharmVar database
  is the canonical reference.

## Implementation models

Three operational models:
1. **Reactive testing** — order PGx test when prescribing
   a specific drug (e.g. CYP2C19 before clopidogrel).
   Slow + reactive but targeted.
2. **Pre-emptive testing** — test once early in life,
   store results in EHR for use across decades + many
   prescriptions.  Vanderbilt PREDICT, Sanford Imagenetics,
   Mayo RIGHT, AllOfUs.
3. **Population-scale genomics** — UK Biobank, Estonian
   Biobank, GenomeAsia, FinnGen — research-mode but
   feeding clinical pipelines.

CPIC + DPWG (Dutch Pharmacogenetics Working Group) +
RCPath publish actionable guidelines that translate
genotype to prescribing recommendations for over 100
drug-gene pairs.

## Returning results to clinicians

EHR integration is the bottleneck:
- **Clinical decision support (CDS)** alerts at point
  of prescribing.
- **Discrete fields** for genotype + phenotype (vs free
  text).
- **Drug-specific alerts** triggered by both genotype +
  drug order.
- **Patient-portal access** + **direct-to-consumer**
  reports (23andMe, AncestryDNA — variable clinical
  validation).

## Limitations + ethical considerations

- **Most variability is multifactorial** — PGx
  explains ~ 30-50 % even at the strongest gene-drug
  pairs.
- **Population coverage** — variant frequencies +
  effect sizes differ across ancestries; African
  + Hispanic + Indigenous populations are
  under-represented in foundational PGx datasets.
- **Reimbursement** — variable across payers + not
  consistently covered for pre-emptive testing.
- **Consent + privacy** — genetic data is identifiable;
  Genetic Information Non-discrimination Act (GINA)
  in US protects employment + health insurance but
  not life / disability insurance.
- **Re-analysis + variant reclassification** — old
  reports may need updating as interpretation evolves.
- **Family implications** — genetic results affect
  relatives.
